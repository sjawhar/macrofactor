# 03 — Competition vs General Technique Tradeoffs (SBS Synthesis)

## Core framing

SBS framing: technique is optimization of current muscle output. Bigger long-term benches still require more upper-body muscle mass.

So the choice is rarely "right vs wrong" technique. It's usually:

- **Maximum immediate 1RM expression** (competition bias), versus
- **Sustainable loading + hypertrophy + joint tolerance** (general bias).

## Side-by-side decisions

| Decision Area          | Competition-leaning choice             | General-lifting choice                                   | Tradeoff                                                                               |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Arch size              | Larger legal arch                      | Moderate arch                                            | Bigger arch shortens ROM and may boost 1RM; moderate arch may feel more sustainable    |
| Pause strategy         | Full command-specific pause practice   | Touch-and-go acceptable                                  | Comp pause improves specificity; touch-and-go may support volume and smoother training |
| Grip width             | Often wider (up to legal max)          | Moderate/slightly narrower                               | Wider often boosts load; moderate can increase ROM stimulus and comfort                |
| Variation use          | Limited; high specificity              | More variation (incline, DB, close/moderate grips)       | Specificity peaks performance; variation supports broad development                    |
| Session success metric | Heaviest legal comp-relevant execution | Productive stimulus with repeatability and low flare-ups | Peak performance vs long-horizon consistency                                           |

## Grip-width decision logic

SBS practical test:

1. Work to ~80% with current strongest grip.
2. Hit singles 1–2 in wider, then 1–2 in narrower.
3. If alternate grip feels nearly as strong despite lower skill exposure, test it for a training block.

Use result by goal:

- Comp-focused: bias toward strongest legal 1RM profile.
- General/hypertrophy: keep at least meaningful volume in moderate grip for ROM and tissue tolerance.

## Pause policy by goal

- **Comp lifter:** regular paused work is mandatory skill practice.
- **General lifter:** pause can be a tool, not a rule; controlled touch-and-go is fine.

## Reverse-grip, incline, decline in context

- **Reverse-grip:** can improve comfort for some shoulder/elbow issues and increases upper-pec involvement for many; requires stricter spotting safety.
- **Low incline (15–30°):** useful upper-chest/front-delt emphasis.
- **Decline:** shorter ROM, sometimes useful with irritated shoulders, often lower overall payoff for many lifters.

## Programming implication

### Competition block

- Higher share of comp-specific bench (grip, pause, command rhythm).
- Accessories support weak links but do not dominate.

### General strength/hypertrophy block

- Keep bench as anchor but rotate complementary angles/grips.
- Prioritize repeatable high-quality volume and symptom management.

## Practical selection rule

When two techniques are similarly strong:

- Choose the one you can execute pain-free, recover from, and repeat weekly.

When one technique is clearly stronger but irritates tissue:

- Use it sparingly for specificity and build most volume around the robust option.
