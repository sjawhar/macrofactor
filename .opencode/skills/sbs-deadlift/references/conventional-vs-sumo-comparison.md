# Conventional vs Sumo — Comparison and Selection (SBS synthesis)

## Executive summary

- Neither stance is universally superior.
- Both are valid expressions of the same hinge pattern under different geometry.
- Best style is individual: performance + comfort + recoverability over time.

## Mechanical comparisons

### 1) Hip extension demand

- SBS analysis: hip extension demands are **more similar than commonly assumed**.
- Superficial side-view hip distance comparisons can mislead when not accounting for femur plane and force vectors.

### 2) Knee extension demand

- Sumo generally has **higher quad demand**.
- Conventional quad demand is often comparatively small (for most lifters).

### 3) Spinal extension/back demand

- Conventional generally has greater torso incline and therefore higher spinal extension demands.
- Sumo usually lowers back demand and can be easier for back-limited pullers.

### 4) Technical tolerance

- Conventional often tolerates slight groove errors better.
- Sumo tends to punish small errors more quickly (especially off floor and around knee).

## Anthropometry and anatomy

- Segment lengths heavily constrain setup:
  - Longer arms/torso usually lower start hips.
  - Longer femurs/tibias usually raise start hips.
- Hip socket/pelvis structure may strongly influence sumo comfort and lockout ability.
- Don’t use internet “hip height aesthetics” as universal standards.

## Who tends to do better (probabilistic trends)

- **Often conventional-favored:** lifters with strong backs, who tolerate higher spinal demand.
- **Often sumo-favored:** lifters with strong quads + good hip abduction capacity, or those limited by back fatigue.
- Body size trend in elite fields exists, but has many exceptions.

## Programming interaction

- Sumo can let conventional lifters train hinge pattern with less back fatigue.
- Conventional can keep sumo specialists’ back strength from becoming a bottleneck.
- Many lifters benefit from keeping both in rotation even with one primary.

## Selection protocol (required 6–12 month trial)

1. Commit to meaningful exposure to both styles.
2. Standardize key accessories and progression approach enough for fair comparison.
3. Track objective and subjective outcomes:
   - Top single quality and trend.
   - Rep performance and consistency.
   - Irritation patterns (hips/back).
   - Session-to-session fatigue and recovery.
4. Decide primary style after 6–12 months of data.
5. Keep secondary style in reduced dose for transfer and tissue balance.

## Common bad decisions to avoid

- Choosing only by social media aesthetics.
- Choosing only by one-session max.
- Forcing super-wide sumo just for ROM reduction.
- Dropping secondary-stance exposure entirely for long periods.
