# Deadlift Programming and Fatigue Management (SBS synthesis)

## Core principle

Deadlifts produce high systemic and local fatigue relative to many other lifts. The best programs are not just “hard”; they are **recoverable, repeatable, and technically stable**.

## Volume heuristics

- Start deadlift volume around **50–67% of squat volume**.
- Increase only if performance and recovery trend favorably.
- When in doubt, underdose deadlift volume and add hinge accessories.

## Frequency heuristics

- Heavy barbell deadlift often works best at lower frequency than people expect.
- Common SBS coaching pattern discussed:
  - Many do best at ~1x/week deadlift.
  - Some at ~2x/week.
  - Fewer at higher frequency.
- In contrast, hinge-pattern accessories can often be trained more frequently (e.g., 3x/week) with lower fatigue cost.

## Hinge pattern distribution model

### Primary exposure

- One main deadlift day (top work + controlled back-off work).

### Secondary exposure

- Additional hinge days with lower systemic cost:
  - RDLs
  - Back raises/hyperextensions
  - Good mornings
  - Swings

This model builds posterior-chain capacity and motor patterning without burying recovery.

## Autoregulation priorities

- If back/hips feel beaten up before deadlift day, delaying 24–48h can outperform forcing poor-quality pulls.
- Pulling hard into deep fatigue often worsens subsequent training days with little added adaptation.
- Maintain technique constraints as the guardrail:
  - If positional quality drops, reduce load/reps.
  - If quality holds, progress gradually.

## Conventional vs sumo in programming

- Sumo usually permits less back fatigue per session.
- Conventional can preserve or improve back robustness for sumo-dominant lifters.
- Hybrid strategy often useful:
  - Primary stance for specific strength.
  - Secondary stance in reduced volume for robustness and weak-link insurance.

## Sticking point-informed accessories

### Off-floor constraints

- Paused off-floor pulls
- Deficits (if tolerated)
- Position-first loading

### Midrange constraints

- Sumo: quad/glute support + prevent hips from shooting up
- Conventional: ensure setup/tension and posterior-chain strength

### Lockout constraints

- Hip thrust / end-range hip work
- Block pulls or rack pulls when indicated
- Balance/bar path correction entering lockout

## Grip and straps policy

- Platform sport lifters: keep enough strapless work for competition readiness.
- Non-platform context: use straps freely when grip is not the target adaptation.
- Protecting hands and preserving posterior-chain stimulus often improves total training quality.

## Fatigue red flags

- Bar speed collapse session-to-session at same loads.
- Setup inconsistency despite maximal intent.
- Rising back/hip irritation trend.
- Technique drift early in work sets.

When these appear, first adjust deadlift stress (load/sets/frequency) before adding complexity.

## Practical weekly template (example skeleton)

- **Day A:** Main deadlift (primary stance), moderate volume.
- **Day B:** Hinge accessory emphasis (RDL + back extension).
- **Day C:** Optional secondary stance or speed/paused variation, lower fatigue.

Tune by recovery markers and technical quality, not by fixed ideology.
