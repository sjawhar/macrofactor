# Sumo Deadlift — Setup and Execution (SBS synthesis)

## 1) Stance framework

- In sumo, **feet are outside hands**; stance is the defining difference from conventional.
- Good initial heuristic: set stance so shins are roughly vertical from front/back when knees are pushed out.
- Then individualize wider/narrower for strength, balance, and hip comfort.

## 2) Toe angle and knee tracking

- Let stance width dictate toe angle.
- Knees should generally track over first/second toe.
- Exception: ultra-wide stances may need slightly less toe-out to preserve balance.

## 3) Grip in sumo

- Hands typically directly below shoulders (or as close as knurling allows).
- Since knees are out, arms can stay very narrow, reducing ROM.

## 4) Setup patterns that work best

- Sumo is less forgiving to misgroove; simpler setup sequences outperform “timing-heavy” styles.
- SBS-preferred setup families map to:
  1. Tension hamstrings + pull hips into position.
  2. Back tight + pull hips into position.
- Avoid overly dynamic/rolling setups until highly consistent.

## 5) Distinctive sumo tension cue

- “**Rip the floor in half**” between feet as hips come down.
- You should feel high hip tension + quad engagement.
- Maintain this tension through break-off and early ascent.

## 6) Balance priorities

- Sumo penalties for balance loss are high; forward drift near knee is a common miss-maker.
- Keep pressure over midfoot and bar path close/vertical as possible.

## 7) Shin angle in sumo

- Compared to conventional, sumo generally benefits from keeping shins more vertical.
- Forward knee drift can interfere with bar path and destabilize groove.

## 8) Execution cues

### Off floor

- Chest up.
- Drive floor away (longer duration than conventional in many lifters).
- Keep “rip floor apart” active.

### Midrange to lockout

- Maintain knee-out and bar close.
- As bar clears knees, finish with hips forward and torso tall without overlean.

## 9) Common sumo weak points

- **Off floor miss:** often just global strength limit (normal in sumo).
- **Midrange miss:** often hips shooting up (hamstring takeover), reducing quad/glute contribution.
- **Lockout miss:** frequently technical (weight too far forward, stance too wide for individual hip structure).

## 10) High-value sumo corrections

- Reduce load to preserve start position.
- Use bottom-half pauses (off floor + knee-level).
- Build quads/glutes if hips shoot up.
- If lockout stalls, test slightly narrower stance or less toe-out.
- Keep some conventional pulling in training to maintain back robustness.
