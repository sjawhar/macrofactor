# Supplements and Ergogenic Aids (SBS Reference)

## SBS Framing

Supplements are usually a **small lever** relative to calories, protein, training quality, and sleep.
Use this order:

1. Fix training and diet fundamentals first.
2. Add supplements only when the expected benefit is meaningful for the user's goal.
3. Match confidence to evidence strength.

## Evidence-Quality Legend

- **Strong**: repeatable effect across multiple quality studies/meta-analyses.
- **Moderate**: plausible and sometimes useful, but effect size and consistency vary.
- **Weak**: inconsistent findings or mostly low-quality/surrogate evidence.
- **Absent/insufficient**: not enough direct evidence for reliable recommendations.

## High-Frequency Supplements in SBS Q&A

### Creatine monohydrate

- **Mechanism (brief):** Increases phosphocreatine availability, improving rapid ATP regeneration during high-intensity work.
- **Evidence quality:** **Strong**.
- **Practical use:** 3-5 g/day, taken consistently; timing is low-priority.
- **SBS stance:** Best-supported performance/body-composition supplement for lifters; boring and effective beats exotic stacks.

### Caffeine

- **Mechanism (brief):** Adenosine antagonism increases alertness, effort tolerance, and acute performance.
- **Evidence quality:** **Strong** for acute performance.
- **Practical use:** Commonly ~2-6 mg/kg about 30-90 minutes pre-training; start low to assess tolerance.
- **Tolerance notes:** Habitual use can blunt subjective impact; selective use and occasional lower-use periods may restore sensitivity for some users.
- **SBS stance:** Useful, especially when performance or training quality on a given day matters; avoid turning every session into a maximal stimulant event.

### Sodium bicarbonate

- **Mechanism (brief):** Extracellular buffering may improve high-intensity efforts by helping handle acidosis.
- **Evidence quality:** **Moderate**.
- **Practical use:** Can help in specific high-intensity contexts, but GI distress and protocol complexity limit real-world usefulness.
- **SBS stance:** Evidence-backed in principle, often impractical in practice; test in non-critical sessions if used.

### Citrulline / arginine

- **Mechanism (brief):** Nitric-oxide pathway support may increase vasodilation/blood flow and "pump" sensations.
- **Evidence quality:** **Weak to moderate** (mixed).
- **Practical use:** Some users report better session feel; objective performance effects are less consistent.
- **SBS stance:** Reasonable as an optional "nice-to-have" if budget/adherence are unaffected; not foundational.

### Ashwagandha

- **Mechanism (brief):** Adaptogenic/herbal compound proposed to influence stress signaling and recovery perceptions.
- **Evidence quality:** **Weak to moderate**.
- **Practical use:** Some studies suggest modest effects on stress markers and possibly strength-related outcomes.
- **SBS stance:** Potentially useful for some people, but expectations should stay modest and not replace sleep/recovery fundamentals.

### Melatonin, ZMA, magnesium (sleep-focused)

- **Mechanism (brief):**
  - Melatonin: circadian timing/sleep-onset support.
  - Magnesium: may support sleep quality in deficiency or poor-status contexts.
  - ZMA: combination product (zinc/magnesium/B6), often marketed for recovery/testosterone.
- **Evidence quality:**
  - Melatonin: **Moderate** for sleep onset/circadian support.
  - Magnesium: **Moderate** when deficient, weaker when already sufficient.
  - ZMA: **Weak** for unique performance benefits beyond correcting deficiencies.
- **SBS stance:** Treat as sleep-support tools, not muscle-building magic; sleep hygiene usually has larger impact.

### Joint supplements (glucosamine/chondroitin)

- **Mechanism (brief):** Proposed cartilage/joint support.
- **Evidence quality:** **Weak to moderate** for pain symptoms; weak for structural remodeling claims.
- **Practical use:** May reduce perceived joint pain in some users; response heterogeneity is high.
- **SBS stance:** Symptom-management possibility, not a reliable way to regrow cartilage or "bulletproof" joints.

### NSAIDs and hypertrophy (e.g., ibuprofen/aspirin context)

- **Mechanism (brief):** COX inhibition reduces inflammation/pain; chronic high use may theoretically interfere with some anabolic signaling.
- **Evidence quality:** **Moderate** for possible interference at high/chronic use; context dependent.
- **Practical use:** Normal occasional doses for pain are generally not a hypertrophy catastrophe.
- **SBS stance:** Don't panic over sporadic use, but avoid routine high-dose dependence if muscle growth is a top priority.

### Protein powders

- **Mechanism (brief):** Convenient protein delivery.
- **Evidence quality:** **Strong** that protein itself matters; **absent** that powders are superior to matched whole-food protein.
- **Practical use:** Use for convenience/compliance when whole-food targets are hard to hit.
- **SBS stance:** A logistics tool, not a special anabolic category.

## Practical Decision Rules

1. If diet/training/sleep are unstable, fix those before adding stack complexity.
2. For most lifters, a minimal stack often suffices: creatine (+ optional caffeine as needed).
3. Favor supplements that solve a clear problem (e.g., convenience, session readiness, sleep onset).
4. Drop products that do not produce user-noticeable value after a fair trial.
5. Avoid certainty inflation: promising mechanisms are not the same as proven outcomes.
