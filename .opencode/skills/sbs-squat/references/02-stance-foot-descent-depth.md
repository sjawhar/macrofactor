# 02 — Stance, Foot Angle, Descent, and Depth

## Stance Width: How to Choose

### Primary filters

1. **Comfort + stability**
2. **Depth quality** (without spinal collapse)
3. **Goal carryover** (powerlifting/weightlifting/sport)

### Practical process

- Start near shoulder width.
- Test slightly narrower and wider.
- Keep version that gives best force transfer, depth control, and repeatability.

## Foot Angle and Knee Tracking

- Let knee tracking guide toe angle, not fixed dogma.
- Typical middle ground: 15-20° turnout.
- Wider stance/higher abduction often needs more toe-out.
- If very wide with too much toe-out hurts balance, point toes slightly more forward.

## Descent Styles

### Sit down

- Break knees + hips together.
- Usually more upright and deeper.
- Great for quad ROM/hypertrophy.

### Sit back

- Break at hips first.
- Usually shorter depth and more torso lean.
- Often useful for powerlifting specificity.

## Descent Speed

- Descend quickly **only while controlled**.
- Faster controlled eccentric improves rebound potential.

## Depth Principles

- Most training contexts: deeper controlled ROM is beneficial.
- Bottoming out often helps use elastic rebound and improves momentum into sticking region.
- Powerlifters: optimize for legal depth, not unnecessary extra ROM.

## Why Anatomy Changes Depth

Depth limits can come from:

- hip socket architecture,
- femur orientation,
- ankle dorsiflexion,
- stance-to-anatomy mismatch,
- or control/bracing deficits under load.

Therefore, “everyone must squat ATG” is incorrect.

## Depth Troubleshooting Ladder

1. Confirm spinal position is maintained.
2. Adjust stance + toe angle.
3. Try sit-down vs sit-back bias.
4. Add heel elevation if true ankle ROM limits depth.
5. Address strength/control weak links.
