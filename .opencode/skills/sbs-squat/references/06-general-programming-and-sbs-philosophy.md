# 06 — General Programming and SBS Philosophy

## How SBS Interprets Research

Greg's style is usually **evidence-weighted, not evidence-binary**:

- Distinguish stronger evidence from weaker evidence instead of pretending all findings are equal.
- Separate "what studies suggest on average" from "what one lifter should do next week."
- Avoid absolutist claims when data are mixed.
- Make recommendations that are robust to uncertainty (simple defaults that work for most lifters).

## Evidence Strength Rubric (Use in Answers)

- **Strong:** repeated findings across studies/populations; practical direction is clear.
- **Moderate:** plausible and supported, but with mixed outcomes or smaller evidence base.
- **Weak:** mostly mechanistic/speculative, low direct evidence, or conflicting data.

When giving advice, label certainty implicitly or explicitly (e.g., "good default," "reasonable option," "tentative").

## Rest Intervals

### Practical defaults

- Compounds: around **2-3 minutes** is a strong general default.
- Isolation/smaller movements: shorter rests are often fine.

### SBS nuance

- Hypertrophy literature is mixed: shorter rests can work, but longer rests may preserve rep quality/volume in many settings.
- The best interval is the shortest one that still lets performance stay high enough for the goal.

## Periodization

### High-level hierarchy

- For many lifters, **undulating** structures outperform rigid linear progression once novice gains slow.
- **Linear** progression still works early or in constrained contexts.
- **Block periodization** is typically more useful at advanced levels where fatigue and specificity management matter more.

### SBS framing

- Periodization should solve practical problems (fatigue, specificity, peaking), not satisfy a theoretical template.
- The best plan is one you can execute consistently while progressing key lifts over time.

## Exercise Variety vs Specificity

- Some exercise variety is beneficial for development and overuse management.
- Too much variety can blunt progressive overload on priority movements.
- Practical middle ground: keep a stable core of priority lifts, rotate accessories or secondary variations strategically.

## Machines vs Free Weights

- Neither category is universally superior.
- Free weights provide broad coordination/specificity benefits.
- Machines can be excellent for stable loading, targeted hypertrophy, and fatigue management.
- SBS-consistent recommendation: use both based on goal, context, and constraints.

## Concurrent Training (Cardio + Lifting)

- Interference effect is real but usually **modest**, not catastrophic.
- Programming quality matters more than fear of cardio:
  - Manage total fatigue.
  - Separate hard endurance and hard lifting when possible.
  - Match cardio dose/mode to goals.
- A stronger aerobic base can improve work capacity and session recovery.

## Youth Training

- Start with bodyweight/calisthenic competency, then progressively load.
- Technique development and consistency matter more than chasing maximal loads early.
- "Bone growth plates will be damaged by lifting" is often overstated when training is supervised and progressed appropriately.

## Belts (Programming-Level Guidance)

- Belts generally aid heavy compound performance via IAP and improved bracing feedback.
- Belt use is not well-supported as a cause of meaningful "core weakness" in trained contexts.
- Practical default:
  1. Learn solid beltless bracing first.
  2. Use a belt for heavier work where performance and stability matter most.

## Deloads

- Prefer **active deloads** over complete inactivity in most cases.
- Typical approach:
  - Reduce volume by about **30-50%**.
  - Keep some meaningful intensity so movement patterns stay sharp.
- Full passive rest can leave lifters feeling detrained/stiff without solving the underlying programming issue.

## Decision Rules for General Questions

When questions are broad programming questions (not movement-technique troubleshooting):

1. Lead with broad SBS training principles.
2. Give practical defaults first.
3. Add caveats only where they materially change decisions.
4. Use squat examples only as illustrations, not as the answer's central frame.
