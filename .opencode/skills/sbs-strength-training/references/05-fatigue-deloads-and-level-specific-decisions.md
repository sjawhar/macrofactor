# Fatigue, Deloads, and Level-Specific Decisions

## Fatigue model in SBS context

Training creates adaptation potential, but only if recovery resources are adequate.

Primary modifiers:

- Sleep quality/quantity
- Life stress load
- Nutrition/body composition
- Joint/connective tissue tolerance
- Training density and proximity to failure

SBS principle: you get stronger outside the gym, not during the session.

## Deload triggers

Initiate deload when multiple indicators persist:

- Performance decline at normal effort
- Rep quality/bar speed decay across sessions
- Elevated soreness or tendon irritation
- Motivation drop, poor sleep, unusually high perceived effort

Do not wait for complete burnout.

## Deload execution (default 1 week)

- Volume: reduce by 30-50%
- Intensity: reduce by 5-10% (or keep moderate loads with fewer sets)
- Effort: keep 3-5 RIR
- Exercise menu: mostly unchanged to maintain skill

Outcome target: restored readiness + stable technique.

## Deload frequency by level

- Beginner: generally as-needed, often every 6-12+ weeks
- Intermediate: commonly every 4-8 weeks
- Advanced: often planned at phase boundaries or every 3-6 weeks in high-demand blocks

## Level-specific fatigue decisions

### Beginner

- Prioritize clean reps over fatigue accumulation
- Keep far from failure
- If soreness interferes with skill practice, reduce sets not frequency

### Intermediate

- Push enough volume for growth while preserving joint health
- Use accessories for bulk volume to reduce overuse risk
- Run block-based progression and deload before deep fatigue impairs execution

### Advanced

- Offseason: tolerate more cumulative fatigue to build specific work capacity
- Pre-peak: aggressively control fatigue to maximize heavy-specific output
- Last 4-6 weeks before meet: bias toward high-quality 85-95% exposures; trim accessories

## Decision tree (weekly)

1. Performance up and recovery stable → continue/progress.
2. Performance flat but recovery good → adjust overload lever (small load/rep/set increase).
3. Performance down + recovery poor → deload now.
4. Post-deload still stagnant → change exercise variant/block goal.
5. Repeated pattern over cycles → reassess level/phase and overall stress ecology.

## Plateau handling sequence

1. Verify recovery basics (sleep, stress, calories, protein)
2. Assess whether volume is below MEV or above MRV
3. Reallocate volume across week (frequency/distribution)
4. Adjust progression model (double, linear, wave)
5. Change variant to target weak point

## Re-sensitization, deloads, and "masked fitness"

### What re-sensitization means

In this context, re-sensitization means restoring training responsiveness after prolonged high stress by temporarily reducing training stress (volume and/or intensity), then rebuilding.

### Evidence quality

- Direct hypertrophy re-sensitization protocols are **limited** in the literature.
- Detraining/retraining research supports the idea of **partial re-sensitization** in some contexts.
- This supports a plausible mechanism, but not a universally proven formula.

### Practical SBS-style use

- Best reserved for **advanced plateaus** after checking basics (sleep, stress, calories, protein, exercise selection, and progression model).
- Typical implementation: 1-3 weeks of reduced volume and lower effort, then gradual re-ramp toward prior productive volumes.
- Treat as an experiment with clear success criteria (performance trend, quality reps, recovery markers), not as mandatory periodization dogma.

### Distinguish two explanations for rebound

1. **True re-sensitization:** biology becomes more responsive again after lower-stress period.
2. **Accumulated fatigue was masking fitness:** deload removes fatigue and existing fitness reappears.

Both can produce better performance after a lower-stress phase; in practice, you often cannot cleanly separate them.

### Bottom line

Use lower-stress phases confidently for fatigue management. Consider re-sensitization a plausible bonus effect, especially for advanced lifters, while keeping epistemic certainty modest.

## Common fatigue mistakes

- Treating all plateaus as "need more effort"
- Running high volume and high intensity simultaneously for too long
- Ignoring pain signals until forced layoff
- Deloading too late, then requiring multiple low-productivity weeks
