# Is the Bench Press the Best Measure of Raw Physical Strength?

## Short Answer

No. The bench press is a **sport-specific measure of upper-body pressing strength**, not a comprehensive test of raw physical strength. Raw strength is multidimensional and requires multiple tests to assess fairly.

## Why the Bench Press Falls Short

### 1. **It Measures Only One Movement Pattern**

The bench press isolates horizontal pressing—a single plane of motion. Raw physical strength encompasses:

- **Vertical pressing** (overhead press, push press)
- **Pulling strength** (deadlift, rows, pull-ups)
- **Lower-body strength** (squat, leg press)
- **Rotational and anti-rotational strength** (core stability)
- **Grip and carrying strength** (farmer carries, loaded carries)

A lifter with a 400 lb bench press but a 200 lb squat is not "stronger" in any objective sense—they are stronger in one specific movement.

### 2. **Technique Heavily Influences the Load**

The bench press is highly technique-dependent. From the SBS framework, we know that:

- **Setup quality** (scapular retraction, arch, leg drive) can add 5–10% to a lifter's 1RM without any change in muscle mass.
- **Bar path efficiency** (up-and-back initiation) directly determines whether a rep succeeds or fails.
- **Grip width, touch point, and pause style** all shift the load distribution and leverage.

A lifter with superior bench technique but less overall muscle mass can outbench someone with more muscle but poor setup. This makes the bench press a measure of **technique + strength**, not raw strength alone.

### 3. **It Favors Specific Anthropometry**

The bench press is not biomechanically neutral:

- **Shorter arm lengths** reduce range of motion and improve leverage.
- **Longer torsos** allow greater arch and shorter effective ROM.
- **Wider shoulders** provide better pressing mechanics.

A lifter with favorable bench anthropometry (short arms, long torso) will bench more than an equally muscular lifter with unfavorable proportions. This is leverage, not raw strength.

### 4. **It Doesn't Test Pulling Strength**

The bench press is a **pushing movement**. It tells you nothing about:

- Posterior chain strength (back, glutes, hamstrings)
- Pulling power (deadlift, rows, pull-ups)
- Grip strength
- Spinal stability under load

SBS programming emphasizes maintaining at least a **1:1 pull-to-press set ratio** for shoulder health and balanced development. This is because pressing dominance without pulling creates imbalances and injury risk—a sign that bench pressing alone is an incomplete strength measure.

## What Actually Measures Raw Strength Better

### **The Deadlift**

The deadlift is arguably the single best measure of raw strength because:

- It involves the **largest muscle groups** (posterior chain, quads, core).
- It has **minimal technique leverage**—you either move the bar or you don't.
- It's **less anthropometry-dependent** than the bench press.
- It tests **pulling strength**, which the bench press ignores.

A 500 lb deadlift is a more impressive raw-strength indicator than a 400 lb bench press because it requires more total muscle mass and less technique optimization.

### **The Squat**

The squat tests:

- **Lower-body strength** (quads, glutes, hamstrings)
- **Core stability** under heavy load
- **Whole-body tension** and bracing

A 500 lb squat demonstrates raw lower-body strength that the bench press cannot measure.

### **Multi-Lift Totals**

The most honest measure of raw strength is a **three-lift total** (squat + bench + deadlift) or **four-lift total** (adding overhead press). This captures:

- Lower-body strength (squat)
- Horizontal pressing (bench)
- Vertical pressing (overhead press)
- Pulling strength (deadlift)

Powerlifting competitions use the three-lift total precisely because it's a more comprehensive strength measure than any single lift.

## The Bench Press's Actual Role

The bench press is **excellent for**:

- Measuring **upper-body horizontal pressing strength** in a standardized, repeatable way.
- Assessing **chest, triceps, and front-delt development**.
- Providing a **sport-specific benchmark** for powerlifting competition.
- Identifying **technical proficiency** in a complex movement.

But it is **not** a measure of raw physical strength in isolation.

## Practical Implication

If someone asks, "How strong are you?" and you answer with only your bench press, you're giving an incomplete picture. A complete strength profile requires:

1. **A lower-body measure** (squat or leg press)
2. **A pulling measure** (deadlift or row)
3. **An overhead measure** (overhead press or push press)
4. **A grip/carry measure** (farmer carry or deadlift grip strength)

The bench press is one important piece of that puzzle, but it is not the puzzle itself.

## Conclusion

Raw physical strength is multidimensional. The bench press measures one dimension—upper-body horizontal pressing—very well. But it is not the best measure of overall raw strength. The deadlift, squat, and multi-lift totals provide more comprehensive assessments because they involve larger muscle groups, are less technique-dependent, and test multiple movement patterns.

If you want to know how strong someone truly is, ask for their squat, bench, and deadlift. The total tells you far more than the bench alone ever could.
