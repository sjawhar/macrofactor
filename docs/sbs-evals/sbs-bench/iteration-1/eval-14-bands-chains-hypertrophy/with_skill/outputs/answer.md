# Accommodating Resistance for Hypertrophy: Bands and Chains on the Bench Press

## The Core Question

Accommodating resistance—bands and chains—is often discussed for strength and competition specificity, but what about hypertrophy? The answer depends on understanding how these tools change the stimulus profile and whether those changes align with hypertrophy goals.

## How Accommodating Resistance Changes the Stimulus

**Bands and chains alter the resistance curve.** With a barbell alone, resistance is constant throughout the range of motion. With accommodating resistance:

- **Chains:** Resistance increases as more chain leaves the floor, creating a **heavier lockout** and lighter bottom position.
- **Bands:** Resistance increases as the band stretches, creating a **progressive load curve** that peaks at lockout.

This means the **stretched position (bottom of the bench)—where the pecs are longest and most vulnerable to damage—receives less tension** than with a straight barbell. The **shortened position (lockout)—where the pecs are already shortened—receives more tension.**

## Hypertrophy Implications

Hypertrophy research consistently shows that **tension in the stretched position is critical for muscle growth.** The eccentric phase and the bottom of the range of motion are where mechanical tension is highest and where muscle damage accumulates most effectively.

When you use accommodating resistance:

1. **Stretched-position loading decreases.** The bottom of the bench press—where your pecs are maximally lengthened—has less absolute tension than with a barbell alone. This is the opposite of what hypertrophy demands.

2. **Shortened-position loading increases.** The lockout has more tension, but this is where the pecs are already shortened and less mechanically stressed. While lockout strength matters, it's not the primary driver of pec hypertrophy.

3. **Range of motion may be reduced.** If you're using chains or bands to handle more total load, you might unconsciously reduce your range of motion to accommodate the heavier lockout. Shorter ROM = less stretched-position stimulus.

## Practical Recommendations for Hypertrophy

**For primary hypertrophy work, use straight barbells or dumbbells.** These provide consistent tension throughout the range of motion, with maximum mechanical tension in the stretched position where it matters most for growth.

**Accommodating resistance has a limited role in hypertrophy training:**

- **Lockout-specific accessory work:** If you have a weak lockout that limits your ability to train the full range with heavy weight, chains or bands on a secondary exercise (like board presses or pin presses) can help build lockout strength. This can then allow you to handle more weight on your primary barbell bench, indirectly supporting hypertrophy.

- **Fatigue management:** Late in a training block when accumulated fatigue makes heavy barbell work risky, accommodating resistance can allow you to maintain volume with reduced joint stress. However, this is a compromise, not an optimization.

- **Variation for novelty:** Rotating in a band or chain session occasionally can provide a different stimulus and prevent accommodation, but it shouldn't replace your primary barbell work.

## The SBS Framework Applied

From the SBS bench press model, the principle is clear: **technique is a force multiplier, not a substitute for muscle.** Accommodating resistance is a technique tool—it optimizes how you express existing strength, but it doesn't build more muscle tissue than straight-barbell work.

For hypertrophy specifically, the SBS approach prioritizes:

1. **Consistent tension throughout the range**, especially in the stretched position.
2. **Controlled eccentric loading** (2–3 second descents) to maximize mechanical tension and muscle damage.
3. **Progressive volume and load** over time, which is easier to track and scale with a barbell than with variable resistance.

Accommodating resistance complicates all three of these priorities.

## Bottom Line

**Use accommodating resistance for strength and competition specificity, not hypertrophy.** If your goal is muscle growth, prioritize:

- Straight-barbell bench press with controlled eccentrics
- Moderate grip widths that allow full range of motion
- Progressive overload in total volume and load
- Complementary exercises (incline press, dumbbell work, flyes) that provide varied stimulus

Chains and bands are valuable tools for peaking strength and managing competition-specific technique, but they're not optimal for building the muscle mass that ultimately determines your bench press ceiling.
