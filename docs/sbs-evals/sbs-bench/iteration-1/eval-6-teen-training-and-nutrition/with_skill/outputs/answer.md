# Building Muscle and Strength as a 15-Year-Old: Evidence-Based Guide

## Overview

At 15, your body is primed for rapid muscle and strength gains due to hormonal changes and high training responsiveness. The key is combining smart exercise selection, appropriate training volume, and adequate nutrition. This guide is based on Stronger By Science (SBS) evidence-based principles.

---

## Exercise Selection

**Start with compound movements** that build foundational strength and work multiple muscle groups efficiently:

- **Lower body:** Squat (back squat or goblet squat), deadlift or Romanian deadlift, leg press
- **Upper body:** Bench press, barbell rows, pull-ups or lat pulldowns, overhead press
- **Accessories:** Dumbbell work, machine exercises, and isolation movements for lagging muscles

**Why compounds first:** They teach movement patterns, build work capacity, and deliver the most stimulus per unit of time. Accessories come second—they accumulate volume with lower joint stress.

**Progression principle:** Choose exercises with stable, repeatable range of motion that you can load progressively over weeks and months.

---

## Weekly Training Volume

Use these **starting targets** for weekly hard sets (sets taken near effort, not just any set):

| Muscle Group          | Weekly Sets | Frequency |
| --------------------- | ----------- | --------- |
| Chest                 | 10-14 sets  | 2-3x/week |
| Back                  | 12-16 sets  | 2-3x/week |
| Quads                 | 10-14 sets  | 2-3x/week |
| Hamstrings            | 8-12 sets   | 2-3x/week |
| Shoulders             | 10-14 sets  | 2-3x/week |
| Arms (biceps/triceps) | 8-12 sets   | 2-3x/week |

**Practical example:** A 4-day upper/lower split hitting each muscle 2-3x per week is ideal for your age. This distributes volume across the week and allows adequate recovery.

---

## Rep Ranges, Intensity, and Proximity to Failure

**For muscle gain (hypertrophy):**

- **Rep range:** 6-15 reps per set
- **Intensity:** 60-80% of your 1-rep max (1RM)
- **Proximity to failure:** 0-3 reps in reserve (RIR) on most sets
  - This means stopping when you could do 0-3 more reps with good form
  - **Do NOT train to failure on every set**—this increases fatigue without extra muscle gain

**For strength development:**

- **Rep range:** 1-6 reps per set
- **Intensity:** 75-85% of 1RM (most work here; avoid >90% unless peaking)
- **Proximity to failure:** 1-3 RIR

**Key principle:** Beginners should stay **3+ RIR** on heavy compound lifts to preserve technique quality. As you gain experience, you can train closer to failure on accessories.

**Progressive overload:** Add weight or reps each week. Example progression:

- Week 1: 3 sets of 8 reps at 30 kg
- Week 2: 3 sets of 9 reps at 30 kg
- Week 3: 3 sets of 10 reps at 30 kg
- Week 4: 3 sets of 8 reps at 32.5 kg (reset reps, add load)

## Nutrition for Muscle Gain

### Calories

Your body needs a **calorie surplus** to build muscle. Use this starting estimate:

**Moderate gain:** 17 kcal per pound of body weight per day (37.4 kcal/kg/day)

**Example:** If you weigh 150 lbs, aim for ~2,550 kcal/day.

**Adjustment:** Track your weight daily (morning, post-restroom, same scale). If you're not gaining 0.1-0.25% of body weight per week after 2-3 weeks, add 200-300 calories.

### Protein

**Target: 1.6-2.2 g per kg of body weight per day**

**Example:** At 68 kg (150 lbs), aim for 109-150 g protein daily. A practical target is **1.8 g/kg = 122 g/day**.

**Why:** Protein is essential for muscle repair and growth. Higher protein in a surplus supports muscle gain while minimizing excess fat gain.

**Distribution:** Spread protein across 3-5 meals. Aim for at least 0.3 g/kg per meal (roughly 20-30 g per meal for most teens).

### Fat

**Target: 20-35% of total calories, with a minimum floor**

**Minimum floor:** ((height in cm - 150) × 0.5) + 30 grams per day

**Example:** At 175 cm tall, your floor is ((175 - 150) × 0.5) + 30 = **42.5 g/day minimum**.

**Why:** Fat supports hormone production (including testosterone) and overall health. Never go below the floor.

### Carbohydrates

**Target: At least 3-4 g per kg of body weight per day** (if calories allow)

**Example:** At 68 kg, aim for 204-272 g carbs daily.

**Why:** Carbs fuel high-intensity training, support recovery, and improve performance. Fill remaining calories after protein and fat with carbs.

### Practical Example (150 lb / 68 kg person)

- **Calories:** 2,550 kcal/day
- **Protein:** 122 g (488 kcal)
- **Fat:** 70 g (630 kcal)
- **Carbs:** 280 g (1,120 kcal)
- **Remaining:** ~312 kcal (flexible; can adjust carbs/fat ratio)

### Micronutrients and Hydration

- **Fiber:** Aim for 25-35 g/day (supports digestion and health)
- **Water:** Drink 3-4 liters daily; more on training days
- **Micronutrients:** Eat varied whole foods (vegetables, fruits, grains, meats, dairy). If diet is limited, consider a basic multivitamin

## Putting It Together: Sample Weekly Structure

**4-day upper/lower split:**

- **Day 1 (Upper A):** Bench press, rows, overhead press, accessories
- **Day 2 (Lower A):** Squat, leg press, leg curls, accessories
- **Day 3 (Rest or light activity)**
- **Day 4 (Upper B):** Incline bench, pull-ups, dumbbell work, accessories
- **Day 5 (Lower B):** Deadlift, leg press, leg extensions, accessories
- **Days 6-7 (Rest)**

Each session: 4-6 hard sets on main lifts (75-85% intensity), then 2-3 sets of accessories (6-15 reps, 0-3 RIR).

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Train smart, not just hard:** Use compounds, appropriate volume, and controlled intensity. Avoid grinding every set to failure.
2. **Eat in a surplus:** 17 kcal/lb is a solid starting point; adjust based on weekly weight trends.
3. **Prioritize protein:** 1.6-2.2 g/kg supports muscle growth and minimizes fat gain.
4. **Be consistent:** Progress comes from weeks and months of steady training and eating, not single sessions.
5. **Recovery matters:** Sleep 7-9 hours, manage stress, and take deload weeks every 4-8 weeks if needed.

At 15, you have a significant advantage in hormonal responsiveness and recovery capacity. Use it wisely with consistent, evidence-based training and nutrition, and you'll see substantial gains over the next 1-2 years.
