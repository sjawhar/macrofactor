# The SBS Combine: A 12-Week Fitness Testing Battery

## Overview

An SBS-aligned fitness testing battery should measure **multiple strength and power capacities** that reflect real athletic performance and training progress. Rather than a single metric, the "SBS Combine" tracks:

1. **Maximal strength** (lower body, upper body, posterior chain)
2. **Positional strength** (setup quality, tension, technique consistency)
3. **Muscular endurance** (rep performance at submaximal loads)
4. **Power and rate of force development** (explosive capacity)
5. **Movement quality** (technique consistency under fatigue)
6. **Work capacity** (metabolic resilience)

Testing every 12 weeks allows sufficient training stimulus to produce measurable changes while avoiding excessive testing fatigue.

---

## Core Testing Battery (Minimum 5 Tests)

### 1. **Deadlift 1RM (Conventional or Sumo)**

**Purpose:** Maximal posterior-chain strength; primary hinge pattern.

**Protocol:**

- Warm up with progressive singles: 50%, 70%, 85%, 90% of estimated max
- Attempt 1RM with 2–3 minute rest between attempts
- Maximum 3 attempts
- Record best lift

**Tracking:**

- Absolute load (kg/lbs)
- Technique quality (video review for setup consistency, bar path, lockout control)
- Grip type used (bare hands, mixed, hook, straps)

**Why it matters:** Deadlift 1RM is the most direct measure of hip extension strength and posterior-chain power. SBS framework emphasizes that setup quality and tension are the force multipliers—a 1RM test reveals whether technique is improving alongside load.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% increase for intermediate lifters; 5–10% for newer lifters.

---

### 2. **Back Squat 1RM**

**Purpose:** Maximal lower-body strength; quad-dominant pattern.

**Protocol:**

- Warm up with progressive singles: 50%, 70%, 85%, 90% of estimated max
- Attempt 1RM with 2–3 minute rest between attempts
- Maximum 3 attempts
- Record best lift

**Tracking:**

- Absolute load (kg/lbs)
- Depth consistency (parallel or below)
- Technique quality (knee tracking, torso angle, bar position)

**Why it matters:** Squat 1RM balances deadlift testing by measuring quad strength and knee extension power. SBS programming typically uses squat volume at 1.5–2x deadlift volume, so tracking squat progress independently is essential.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% increase for intermediate lifters; 5–10% for newer lifters.

---

### 3. **Bench Press 1RM**

**Purpose:** Maximal upper-body pressing strength; horizontal pressing pattern.

**Protocol:**

- Warm up with progressive singles: 50%, 70%, 85%, 90% of estimated max
- Attempt 1RM with 2–3 minute rest between attempts
- Maximum 3 attempts
- Record best lift

**Tracking:**

- Absolute load (kg/lbs)
- Grip width (consistency across tests)
- Technique quality (bar path, touch point, lockout control)

**Why it matters:** Bench press provides a counterbalance to lower-body testing and measures pressing strength independent of leg drive. It's a fundamental movement for general strength athletes.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% increase for intermediate lifters; 5–10% for newer lifters.

---

### 4. **Deadlift 5RM (Submaximal Strength Endurance)**

**Purpose:** Measure strength endurance and technique consistency under fatigue.

**Protocol:**

- After 1RM testing (or on a separate day), load to ~85% of the 1RM just tested
- Perform 5 consecutive reps with controlled tempo (2 sec down, 1 sec pause, explosive up)
- Rest 3 minutes
- Perform a second set of 5 reps
- Record total reps completed and any form breakdown

**Tracking:**

- Total reps completed (target: 10/10)
- Rep quality (video review for form consistency, especially on reps 4–5)
- Load used (% of 1RM)

**Why it matters:** SBS emphasizes that **technique consistency expands recoverable volume**. A 5RM test reveals whether a lifter can maintain position under fatigue—a critical marker of training readiness and injury resilience. If form collapses on reps 4–5, it signals either inadequate recovery or a technique/strength imbalance.

**12-week expectation:** Maintain 10/10 reps with improved form quality, or increase load while maintaining reps.

---

### 5. **Vertical Jump (Power Assessment)**

**Purpose:** Measure lower-body power and rate of force development.

**Protocol:**

- Stand with feet hip-width apart, arms at sides
- Perform a countermovement (quick dip) and jump as high as possible
- Measure height reached (use wall chalk mark, force plate, or jump mat)
- Perform 3 trials with 1 minute rest between
- Record best jump height

**Tracking:**

- Peak jump height (cm or inches)
- Consistency across trials (coefficient of variation)
- Asymmetry (if testing single-leg jumps)

**Why it matters:** Vertical jump measures explosive power independent of maximal strength. It reflects rate of force development and neuromuscular coordination—capacities that don't always correlate with 1RM. Improvements in jump height suggest better power output and athletic performance.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% improvement in jump height.

---

### 6. **Grip Strength (Isometric Handgrip)**

**Purpose:** Measure grip endurance and support strength.

**Protocol:**

- Use a calibrated hand dynamometer
- Stand with arm at 90° elbow flexion, neutral wrist
- Squeeze maximally for 3 seconds
- Perform 3 trials per hand with 1 minute rest between
- Record best result per hand

**Tracking:**

- Peak grip strength (kg or lbs) per hand
- Asymmetry between hands (should be <10%)

**Why it matters:** Grip strength is a marker of overall strength and correlates with posterior-chain pulling capacity. SBS notes that grip can limit deadlift volume—tracking grip independently reveals whether grip is a bottleneck or improving alongside posterior-chain strength.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% improvement.

## Extended Battery (Optional, for Comprehensive Assessment)

### 7. **Broad Jump (Horizontal Power)**

**Purpose:** Measure horizontal power and lower-body explosiveness.

**Protocol:**

- Stand with feet hip-width apart at a line
- Perform a countermovement and jump forward as far as possible
- Measure distance from starting line to heel landing
- Perform 3 trials with 1 minute rest between
- Record best distance

**Tracking:**

- Peak distance (cm or inches)
- Consistency across trials

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% improvement.

---

### 8. **Deadlift Deficit Pull (Off-Floor Strength)**

**Purpose:** Isolate off-floor strength and identify sticking points.

**Protocol:**

- Stand on a 2–4 inch platform (or deficit block)
- Perform a 1RM deadlift from this elevated starting position
- Record load

**Tracking:**

- Absolute load (kg/lbs)
- Comparison to standard deadlift 1RM (deficit pull should be ~85–90% of standard)

**Why it matters:** SBS emphasizes that setup quality and tension are the force multipliers. A deficit pull reveals whether a lifter struggles with the initial pull-off or has adequate off-floor strength. If deficit pull is disproportionately weak, it signals a need for more bottom-half work.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% improvement.

---

### 9. **Prone Plank Hold (Core Endurance)**

**Purpose:** Measure core stability and anti-extension endurance.

**Protocol:**

- Assume a prone plank position (forearms on ground, body straight)
- Hold for maximum time without form breakdown (hips sagging or rising)
- Record time in seconds

**Tracking:**

- Hold duration (seconds)
- Form quality (video review for hip position consistency)

**Why it matters:** Core endurance is foundational for maintaining spinal position under load. SBS notes that back rounding and form collapse are often driven by core fatigue or control deficits. A plank hold reveals whether core endurance is limiting deadlift or squat performance.

**12-week expectation:** 10–20% improvement in hold time.

---

### 10. **Trap Bar Deadlift 1RM (Alternative Hinge Pattern)**

**Purpose:** Measure hip extension strength with a more quad-friendly loading pattern.

**Protocol:**

- Warm up with progressive singles: 50%, 70%, 85%, 90% of estimated max
- Attempt 1RM with 2–3 minute rest between attempts
- Maximum 3 attempts
- Record best lift

**Tracking:**

- Absolute load (kg/lbs)
- Comparison to conventional deadlift 1RM (trap bar is typically 5–15% higher)

**Why it matters:** Trap bar deadlift is less demanding on the back and more quad-dominant than conventional deadlift. If trap bar 1RM is disproportionately higher than conventional, it suggests back strength is the limiting factor. This informs accessory selection and training focus.

**12-week expectation:** 2–5% improvement.

## Testing Protocol & Scheduling

### Pre-Test Preparation (48 hours before)

- Ensure adequate sleep (7–9 hours)
- Maintain normal nutrition and hydration
- Avoid heavy training 48 hours prior
- Perform light mobility work the day before

### Test Day Structure

1. **General warm-up** (5–10 min): light cardio, dynamic stretching
2. **Movement-specific warm-up** (10–15 min): progressive loading to test weight
3. **Test execution** (30–60 min depending on battery size)
4. **Cool-down** (5–10 min): light stretching, breathing work

### Rest Between Tests

- 1RM attempts: 2–3 minutes
- Between different movement patterns: 5–10 minutes
- Between test days: 48–72 hours minimum

### Recommended Test Order (Single Day)

1. Vertical jump (power, fresh CNS)
2. Bench press 1RM
3. Back squat 1RM
4. Deadlift 1RM
5. Deadlift 5RM (submaximal, lower CNS demand)
6. Grip strength (low fatigue impact)
7. Prone plank (finisher, low load)

### Interpretation Framework

**Strength Gains (1RM tests):**

- **2–5% improvement:** Normal progress for intermediate lifters
- **5–10% improvement:** Excellent progress; likely reflects improved technique, recovery, or training stimulus
- **0–2% improvement:** Stagnation; may indicate inadequate training stimulus, recovery issues, or need for program change
- **Regression (>2% loss):** Red flag; investigate recovery, nutrition, sleep, or overtraining

**Strength Endurance (5RM test):**

- **Maintained reps with improved form:** Excellent; technique is improving
- **Increased reps at same load:** Good progress; strength endurance improving
- **Decreased reps or form breakdown:** May indicate inadequate recovery or need for accessory work

**Power (Jump tests):**

- **2–5% improvement:** Normal progress
- **Improvement exceeds strength gains:** Suggests improved rate of force development and neuromuscular coordination
- **Power decreases while strength increases:** May indicate fatigue accumulation or need for more power-specific work

**Grip Strength:**

- **Improvement parallels deadlift gains:** Grip is not a bottleneck
- **Grip stagnates while deadlift improves:** Grip may be limiting volume; consider grip-specific work or straps for volume

**Core Endurance (Plank):**

- **10–20% improvement:** Normal progress
- **Stagnation or regression:** May indicate inadequate core work or excessive fatigue

## SBS-Aligned Interpretation

### Key Principles from SBS Framework

1. **Technique is anatomy-constrained, not aesthetics-constrained.**
   - Use video review during testing to identify whether form changes reflect improved technique or compensation patterns
   - A 1RM increase with worse form is a red flag; prioritize technique consistency

2. **Setup quality is the force multiplier.**
   - During 1RM testing, note setup consistency across attempts
   - If setup degrades on later attempts, it suggests fatigue or tension issues
   - Use the 5RM test to assess whether setup quality is maintained under fatigue

3. **Deadlift fatigue is underappreciated.**
   - If deadlift 1RM improves but 5RM reps decrease, it may indicate excessive fatigue accumulation
   - Consider reducing deadlift frequency or volume if recovery is poor

4. **Conventional vs. Sumo comparison.**
   - Test both styles at 12-week intervals to determine which is stronger and more sustainable
   - A 6–12 month comparison period is needed to make a confident specialization decision
   - Track pain/irritation patterns alongside performance

## 12-Week Cycle Recommendations

### Weeks 1–4: Baseline & Adaptation

- Establish baseline testing numbers
- Focus on technique consistency and movement quality
- Moderate volume and intensity

### Weeks 5–8: Strength Building

- Increase intensity (80–90% 1RM work)
- Maintain moderate volume
- Prioritize setup quality and tension

### Weeks 9–11: Deload & Preparation

- Reduce volume by 40–50%
- Maintain intensity (technique work at 70–80%)
- Ensure full recovery before testing

### Week 12: Testing

- Execute full testing battery
- Compare to baseline
- Plan next 12-week cycle based on results

## Conclusion

The SBS Combine is a **practical, evidence-informed testing battery** that measures multiple fitness capacities relevant to strength athletes and generally fit individuals. By testing every 12 weeks, you capture meaningful progress while allowing sufficient training stimulus between assessments.

The key is **consistency in testing protocol** (same time of day, same warm-up, same equipment) and **video documentation** of technique to ensure that strength gains reflect improved performance, not compensation patterns. SBS principles emphasize that setup quality and technique consistency are the true force multipliers—a testing battery that ignores these in favor of raw numbers misses the point.

Use this battery to inform your training decisions: if deadlift 1RM improves but 5RM reps decrease, adjust recovery. If grip strength stagnates, add grip-specific work. If vertical jump decreases while strength increases, prioritize power work. The battery is a diagnostic tool, not just a scorecard.
