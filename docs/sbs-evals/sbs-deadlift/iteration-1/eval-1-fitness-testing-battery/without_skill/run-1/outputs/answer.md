# The SBS Combine: A 12-Week Fitness Testing Battery

## Overview

A comprehensive fitness testing battery for a generally strong, fit, and athletic person should assess multiple physical capacities that matter for real-world performance and longevity. The following tests can be repeated every 12 weeks to track progress across strength, power, endurance, mobility, and movement quality.

---

## 1. **Strength Tests**

### 1.1 Barbell Back Squat — 1RM or 3RM

- **Why**: Measures lower body maximal strength and foundational movement quality
- **Protocol**: Warm up progressively, then attempt 1-rep max (or 3-rep max if you prefer less CNS fatigue). Rest 3–5 minutes between attempts.
- **Tracking**: Record the weight and RPE. Note any form breakdowns.
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 1.2 Barbell Deadlift — 1RM or 3RM

- **Why**: Assesses posterior chain strength, hip extension power, and spinal stability
- **Protocol**: Similar to squat—progressive warm-up, then max attempt(s). Allow full recovery between attempts.
- **Tracking**: Record weight, RPE, and bar path consistency
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 1.3 Barbell Bench Press — 1RM or 3RM

- **Why**: Measures upper body pushing strength and horizontal pressing power
- **Protocol**: Warm up, then max attempt(s). Ensure consistent touch point and pause (if paused bench is your standard)
- **Tracking**: Record weight, RPE, and grip width consistency
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 1.4 Weighted Pull-Up or Chin-Up — 1RM or 3RM

- **Why**: Assesses upper body pulling strength and relative strength
- **Protocol**: Add weight via belt or dumbbell between legs. Record bodyweight + added weight
- **Tracking**: Total weight lifted (bodyweight + load)
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Alternative**: Barbell Bent-Over Row if pull-ups aren't accessible

---

## 2. **Power & Explosiveness Tests**

### 2.1 Vertical Jump

- **Why**: Measures lower body power and rate of force development
- **Protocol**: Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, swing arms, and jump as high as possible. Measure reach height vs. jump height (or use a jump mat for flight time)
- **Tracking**: Record best of 3 attempts in inches or centimeters
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Note**: Perform after warm-up but before heavy strength work

### 2.2 Broad Jump

- **Why**: Assesses horizontal power and lower body explosiveness
- **Protocol**: Stand at a line, swing arms, and jump forward as far as possible. Measure from start line to heel landing
- **Tracking**: Record best of 3 attempts in feet or meters
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 2.3 Medicine Ball Chest Pass (or Overhead Throw)

- **Why**: Measures upper body power and core stability
- **Protocol**: Use a 10–14 lb medicine ball. Stand 2 feet from a wall, chest pass the ball explosively, catch, and repeat for distance or height of impact
- **Tracking**: Record distance or wall height reached
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 3. **Muscular Endurance Tests**

### 3.1 Max Reps at Bodyweight — Push-Ups

- **Why**: Assesses upper body muscular endurance and movement quality under fatigue
- **Protocol**: Strict form (chest to deck, full lockout). Perform max reps in one set
- **Tracking**: Record total reps
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 3.2 Max Reps at Bodyweight — Dips

- **Why**: Measures lower chest, triceps, and shoulder stability endurance
- **Protocol**: Full range of motion (elbows ~90°). Perform max reps in one set
- **Tracking**: Record total reps
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Alternative**: Weighted dips at a fixed load if bodyweight is too easy

### 3.3 Weighted Sled Push or Prowler — Distance or Time

- **Why**: Assesses lower body muscular endurance and work capacity
- **Protocol**: Load sled with moderate weight (50–100 lbs depending on fitness level). Push for max distance in 30–60 seconds, or max time to cover a fixed distance
- **Tracking**: Record distance covered or time to distance
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 4. **Cardiovascular Endurance Tests**

### 4.1 5K Run Time

- **Why**: Measures aerobic capacity and sustained effort tolerance
- **Protocol**: Run 5 kilometers at a steady, challenging pace (not all-out sprint, but hard effort). Use a track, treadmill, or GPS watch
- **Tracking**: Record time and average pace
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Note**: Perform on a day with adequate recovery

### 4.2 1-Mile Run Time

- **Why**: Shorter aerobic test; useful if 5K is too taxing or time-limited
- **Protocol**: Run 1 mile at hard effort
- **Tracking**: Record time
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Alternative**: 2K row on a rowing machine if running isn't accessible

### 4.3 VO₂ Max Proxy — 3-Minute All-Out Effort

- **Why**: Estimates aerobic power and lactate threshold
- **Protocol**: On a bike, rower, or treadmill, go all-out for 3 minutes. Record average power (watts), distance, or pace
- **Tracking**: Record output metric
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 5. **Mobility & Movement Quality Tests**

### 5.1 Overhead Squat (Bodyweight)

- **Why**: Assesses ankle, hip, thoracic, and shoulder mobility; core stability
- **Protocol**: Perform 5 reps with hands overhead, feet shoulder-width apart, full depth
- **Tracking**: Note any compensations (forward lean, heel lift, knee cave, asymmetry)
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks
- **Scoring**: Pass/fail or rate quality on a 1–5 scale

### 5.2 Single-Leg Deadlift (Bodyweight or Light Load)

- **Why**: Assesses hip stability, balance, and unilateral strength
- **Protocol**: Perform 5 reps per leg with light weight or bodyweight, maintaining a neutral spine
- **Tracking**: Note balance, symmetry, and any form breakdown
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 5.3 Shoulder Mobility — Overhead Reach

- **Why**: Measures shoulder and thoracic mobility
- **Protocol**: Lie on your back, arms overhead, and measure the distance from your hands to the floor (or note if hands touch floor)
- **Tracking**: Record distance or pass/fail
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 5.4 Hip Mobility — 90/90 Stretch Hold

- **Why**: Assesses hip external rotation and internal rotation
- **Protocol**: Sit with one leg at 90° in front (external rotation) and one leg at 90° to the side (internal rotation). Hold and note any asymmetry or discomfort
- **Tracking**: Note range and symmetry
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 6. **Work Capacity & Conditioning Tests**

### 6.1 Assault Bike — 1-Minute Max Effort

- **Why**: Measures anaerobic power and work capacity
- **Protocol**: Warm up 2–3 minutes, then go all-out for 1 minute. Record calories burned or average watts
- **Tracking**: Record output metric
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 6.2 Kettlebell Swings — Max Reps in 5 Minutes

- **Why**: Assesses hip power, grip endurance, and metabolic conditioning
- **Protocol**: Use a moderate weight (35–53 lbs). Perform max reps in 5 minutes (rest as needed)
- **Tracking**: Record total reps
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 6.3 Burpee Test — Max Reps in 3 Minutes

- **Why**: Measures full-body conditioning and movement quality under fatigue
- **Protocol**: Perform max burpees (chest to deck, full jump) in 3 minutes
- **Tracking**: Record total reps
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 7. **Body Composition & Anthropometry**

### 7.1 Bodyweight

- **Why**: Tracks overall mass changes
- **Protocol**: Weigh yourself at the same time each day (morning, post-void, pre-food)
- **Tracking**: Record weight
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks (or weekly for trend data)

### 7.2 Body Fat Percentage

- **Why**: Distinguishes muscle gain from fat gain
- **Protocol**: Use DEXA, hydrostatic weighing, or bioelectrical impedance (same method each time for consistency)
- **Tracking**: Record percentage and estimated lean mass
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

### 7.3 Circumference Measurements

- **Why**: Tracks muscle growth and fat loss in specific areas
- **Protocol**: Measure chest, waist, hips, arms, and thighs at the same anatomical landmarks each time
- **Tracking**: Record all measurements
- **Frequency**: Every 12 weeks

---

## 8. **Testing Protocol & Best Practices**

### Scheduling

- **Dedicate 2–3 days** to testing (don't do all tests in one day)
- **Day 1**: Strength tests (squat, deadlift, bench, pull-up)
- **Day 2**: Power tests (vertical jump, broad jump, medicine ball) + mobility tests
- **Day 3**: Endurance tests (5K run, bike sprint, kettlebell swings)
- **Separate testing days by 48 hours** to allow recovery

### Pre-Test Preparation

- Sleep 7–9 hours the night before
- Eat a normal meal 2–3 hours before testing
- Warm up thoroughly (5–10 minutes light cardio + dynamic stretches)
- Perform 2–3 submaximal reps of the test movement before going all-out

### Recording & Tracking

- Use a spreadsheet or app to log all results
- Include date, weather (if outdoor), sleep quality, and any notes
- Calculate percent change from previous test
- Look for trends over 2–3 testing cycles (6–9 months)

### Adjustments Over Time

- If a test is consistently failing or regressing, investigate:
  - Inadequate recovery
  - Poor nutrition
  - Overtraining
  - Technique breakdown
- Modify training to address weak points
- Rotate or replace tests if they become stale or less relevant

---

## 9. **Interpreting Results**

### Expected Progress

- **Strength**: 2–5% improvement per 12 weeks (beginners may see 5–10%)
- **Power**: 3–8% improvement per 12 weeks
- **Endurance**: 2–5% improvement per 12 weeks
- **Mobility**: Qualitative improvement (better range, less compensation)
- **Body composition**: 1–2 lbs of muscle gain or fat loss per 12 weeks (depending on goal)

### Red Flags

- **Strength declining** while bodyweight stable → overtraining, poor recovery, or technique breakdown
- **Power declining** → CNS fatigue, inadequate sleep, or detraining
- **Endurance declining** → reduced aerobic training or overtraining
- **Mobility worsening** → increased training volume without adequate mobility work
- **Body composition worsening** → caloric surplus/deficit not aligned with goal

## Conclusion

A comprehensive SBS Combine testing battery should assess **strength, power, endurance, mobility, and work capacity**. By repeating these tests every 12 weeks, you'll have objective data to track progress, identify weak points, and adjust your training accordingly. The key is consistency in testing conditions, honest effort, and honest assessment of results.

This battery is scalable—a beginner might focus on strength and mobility, while an advanced athlete might emphasize power and conditioning. Adjust based on your goals and available equipment.
