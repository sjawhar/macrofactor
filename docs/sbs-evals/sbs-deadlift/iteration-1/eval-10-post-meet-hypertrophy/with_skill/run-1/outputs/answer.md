# Easing Into High-Volume Hypertrophy After a Powerlifting Meet: SBS Framework

## The Core Challenge

Post-meet, you're in a unique position: your nervous system is fresh from peaking, your technique is sharp, but your tissues are fatigued from meet prep. Jumping directly into high-volume hypertrophy work risks:

1. **Compounding fatigue** — deadlifts are disproportionately fatiguing; adding volume too fast can bury recovery and degrade technique
2. **Losing strength gains** — if volume ramps too aggressively, you may sacrifice the strength foundation you just built
3. **Technique drift** — fatigue erodes positional quality, which then requires rebuilding

The SBS approach is **conservative volume progression with technical stability as the guardrail**.

---

## Phase 1: Deload and Transition (Week 1–2)

### Immediate post-meet (3–7 days)

- **Reduce deadlift frequency to 1x/week** (if you were pulling 2x/week for meet prep).
- **Lower intensity to 60–70% 1RM** for technical work only.
- **Focus on movement quality**, not load. Use this window to assess any technique drift from peaking.
- **Add hinge accessories** (RDLs, back raises, good mornings) at moderate volume — these build posterior-chain capacity with lower systemic fatigue than heavy deadlifts.

### Why this matters

Your CNS needs 7–10 days to fully recover from meet stress. Jumping into heavy work immediately risks poor quality and injury. Light technical work maintains the groove without adding fatigue debt.

---

## Phase 2: Volume Introduction (Week 3–6)

### Deadlift structure

**Start deadlift volume at 50–67% of your squat volume** (SBS heuristic). If you're squatting 12–16 sets/week, begin deadlifts at 6–10 sets/week.

**Example weekly template:**

- **Day A (Main deadlift):**
  - 2–3 singles at 80–85% 1RM (technical work, not max effort)
  - 3–4 sets of 3–5 reps at 70–75% 1RM
  - 2–3 sets of 6–8 reps at 65–70% 1RM
  - **Total: ~8–10 sets**

- **Day B (Hinge accessory):**
  - RDLs: 3–4 sets of 6–8 reps
  - Back raises/hyperextensions: 3 sets of 8–10 reps
  - **Total: ~6–7 sets, lower fatigue cost**

- **Day C (Optional secondary stance or variation):**
  - If conventional is primary, add 1–2 sets of sumo at moderate load
  - Or use paused deadlifts (1–2 sets of 3–5 reps) for positional reinforcement
  - **Total: ~3–4 sets**

### Progression rules

- **Week 3–4:** Establish baseline volume at prescribed loads. Prioritize technical consistency.
- **Week 5–6:** If technique holds and recovery is good, add 1–2 sets to Day A (moving toward 10–12 sets).
- **Autoregulation:** If back/hips feel beaten up before deadlift day, delay 24–48h rather than forcing poor-quality pulls.

---

## Phase 3: Hypertrophy Ramp (Week 7+)

### Shift rep ranges and volume

Once you've established 10–12 sets/week of deadlifts with stable technique:

- **Increase rep ranges** to 5–8 reps (vs 3–5 during transition).
- **Reduce intensity slightly** to 65–75% 1RM to accommodate higher reps.
- **Add volume gradually:** increase by 2–3 sets/week, not 5–6 at once.

**Example hypertrophy-focused week:**

- **Day A (Main deadlift):**
  - 2 singles at 80% 1RM (technical anchor)
  - 4 sets of 5–6 reps at 72–75% 1RM
  - 3 sets of 6–8 reps at 68–70% 1RM
  - **Total: ~12 sets**

- **Day B (Hinge accessory):**
  - RDLs: 4 sets of 6–8 reps
  - Back raises: 3 sets of 8–12 reps
  - Good mornings: 2 sets of 5–6 reps
  - **Total: ~9 sets, distributed fatigue**

- **Day C (Secondary stance or variation):**
  - Sumo deadlifts: 3 sets of 5–6 reps at 70% 1RM
  - Or paused deadlifts: 2 sets of 4–5 reps
  - **Total: ~5 sets**

### Why this structure preserves strength

- **Technical anchor singles** (80% 1RM) maintain CNS efficiency and prevent strength loss.
- **Moderate intensity (65–75%)** allows higher reps without excessive fatigue.
- **Accessory distribution** builds hypertrophy in posterior chain without burying recovery.
- **Secondary stance work** prevents weak-link development (e.g., if conventional is primary, sumo work keeps back robust).

---

## Critical Principles for Success

### 1. Deadlift fatigue is underappreciated

Deadlifts produce **high systemic and local fatigue** relative to squats and bench. A 12-set deadlift day is not equivalent to a 12-set squat day in recovery cost.

**Action:** Start conservatively. If you're tempted to add 15+ sets/week of deadlifts, resist. Build to that over 8–12 weeks.

### 2. Technique is the guardrail

If positional quality drops (hips shooting up, bar drifting forward, setup inconsistency), **reduce load/reps immediately**. Don't push through poor technique hoping it improves.

**Red flags:**

- Bar speed collapse session-to-session at same loads
- Setup inconsistency despite maximal intent
- Rising back/hip irritation trend
- Technique drift early in work sets

When these appear, adjust deadlift stress (load/sets/frequency) before adding complexity.

### 3. Hinge accessories are your volume lever

Instead of adding more heavy deadlifts, add **RDLs, back raises, good mornings, and swings**. These build posterior-chain capacity and motor patterning with lower systemic fatigue.

**Frequency:** Hinge accessories can often be trained 3x/week; heavy deadlifts usually work best at 1–2x/week.

### 4. Conventional vs sumo in hypertrophy

- **Sumo** usually permits less back fatigue per session, making it easier to accumulate volume.
- **Conventional** builds back robustness and is excellent for hypertrophy if recovery is managed.
- **Hybrid strategy:** Primary stance for specific strength; secondary stance in reduced volume for weak-link insurance.

If you're conventional-dominant, keep 1–2 sets of sumo work in your plan to prevent back from becoming the bottleneck.

## Sample 12-Week Progression

| Week  | Deadlift Sets/Week | Rep Range | Intensity | Notes                            |
| ----- | ------------------ | --------- | --------- | -------------------------------- |
| 1–2   | 4–6                | 3–5       | 60–70%    | Deload, technical focus          |
| 3–4   | 8–10               | 3–5       | 70–75%    | Establish baseline               |
| 5–6   | 10–12              | 3–5       | 70–75%    | Add 1–2 sets if stable           |
| 7–8   | 12–14              | 5–6       | 68–73%    | Shift to hypertrophy reps        |
| 9–10  | 14–16              | 5–8       | 65–72%    | Increase volume, lower intensity |
| 11–12 | 16–18              | 6–8       | 65–70%    | Peak hypertrophy phase           |

**Accessory volume** (RDLs, back raises, etc.) increases in parallel, reaching 8–12 sets/week by week 12.

## Strength Preservation Tactics

### 1. Keep technical singles

Perform 2–3 singles at 80–85% 1RM at the start of each deadlift session. This maintains CNS efficiency and prevents strength loss.

### 2. Use secondary stance strategically

If conventional is primary, add 2–3 sets of sumo at 70% 1RM once/week. This builds robustness without excessive fatigue.

### 3. Monitor bar speed

If bar speed collapses at the same load week-to-week, you're accumulating too much fatigue. Reduce volume or frequency.

### 4. Manage grip

Use straps freely during hypertrophy work to preserve posterior-chain stimulus when grip would otherwise limit. Keep 1–2 strapless sets/week for competition readiness (if applicable).

---

## Red Flags: When to Dial Back

- **Back/hip irritation rising** → reduce deadlift frequency to 1x/week, increase accessory work
- **Setup inconsistency** → reduce load 5–10%, focus on tension-first setup
- **Bar speed collapse** → reduce volume by 2–3 sets, add 48h recovery before next deadlift day
- **Technique drift early in sets** → reduce reps/sets, prioritize quality over quantity

---

## Summary: The Easing-In Framework

1. **Weeks 1–2:** Deload. Light technical work, 1x/week deadlifts at 60–70% 1RM.
2. **Weeks 3–6:** Introduce volume conservatively (6–12 sets/week). Establish baseline with stable technique.
3. **Weeks 7+:** Ramp hypertrophy volume gradually (add 2–3 sets/week). Shift rep ranges to 5–8. Maintain technical singles for strength.
4. **Throughout:** Use hinge accessories (RDLs, back raises) as your primary volume lever. Autoregulate based on recovery and technique quality.

**Key principle:** Deadlifts are disproportionately fatiguing. Start at 50–67% of squat volume and build conservatively. Technique stability is the guardrail. If quality drops, reduce load/reps immediately.

This approach preserves your meet-prep strength gains while building hypertrophy sustainably.
