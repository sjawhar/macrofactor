# Bands and Chains for Absolute Strength: Do They Actually Work?

## The Short Answer

Yes, bands and chains work—but not as a primary strength-building tool. They're a **specialized accessory** for specific problems, not a replacement for heavy barbell training.

---

## The Theory (Why They Sound Good)

The logic behind accommodating resistance is mechanically sound:

- **Bands and chains increase load toward lockout**, where you're mechanically strongest and can handle more force.
- **This allows overload exposure** in the range where you're most capable of producing force.
- **The theory predicts** improved lockout strength and explosiveness, which should transfer to your conventional deadlift.

This is not wrong. The mechanics are real. The question is whether the transfer is worth the complexity and whether it's the best use of your training time.

---

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

### What Works

1. **Lockout-specific strength improves** when using bands/chains regularly. If your sticking point is the final 6–12 inches, accommodating resistance can help you get stronger in that range.

2. **Explosiveness can improve** because you're training force production in a range where you can move faster and with higher velocity.

3. **Overload exposure is real**—you can handle more total load with bands/chains than with a straight bar, which provides a novel stimulus.

### What Doesn't Work (Or Works Poorly)

1. **Transfer to raw deadlift strength is modest and indirect.** Lockout strength helps, but it's not the primary limiting factor for most lifters. Most deadlifts miss at the knee or midrange, not at lockout.

2. **They don't build absolute strength as efficiently as heavy barbell work.** A 500 lb deadlift with bands is not the same stimulus as a 500 lb deadlift without them. The bar speed is higher, the time under tension is lower, and the positional demand is different.

3. **They're fatiguing relative to their strength-building return.** Bands and chains add complexity and systemic fatigue without the same raw strength adaptation you'd get from a heavy single or triple on a straight bar.

4. **They don't address the most common limiting factors:**
   - Off-floor weakness (bands/chains are weakest here)
   - Positional control and setup quality
   - Posterior-chain endurance
   - Back strength and stability

---

## When Bands and Chains Actually Make Sense

Use them if:

1. **Your sticking point is genuinely at lockout** (last 6–12 inches), and you've ruled out hip extensor weakness, glute activation, and technique issues.

2. **You're an advanced lifter** (500+ lb deadlift) who has already built a strong foundation with heavy barbell work and is looking for specialized overload.

3. **You're managing back fatigue** and need to train lockout strength without the full-body demand of a heavy conventional deadlift.

4. **You're specifically training for competition** and want to build confidence in the top range of the lift.

---

## The SBS Perspective: Hierarchy of Strength Building

From most to least effective for absolute strength:

1. **Heavy barbell deadlifts** (conventional or sumo, depending on your style)
   - Builds global posterior-chain strength
   - Teaches positional control
   - Highest carryover to competition

2. **Hinge accessories** (RDL, good mornings, back raises)
   - Build posterior-chain capacity with lower fatigue
   - Improve mobility and control
   - Sustainable frequency

3. **Lockout-specific work** (block/rack pulls, hip thrusts)
   - Address lockout weakness directly
   - Lower fatigue than full deadlifts
   - Good for back-limited lifters

4. **Accommodating resistance** (bands, chains)
   - Specialized overload for advanced lifters
   - Useful for lockout confidence
   - Not a primary strength builder

---

## Practical Recommendation

If you're asking this question, you probably don't need bands and chains yet. Instead:

1. **Master heavy barbell deadlifts first.** Build a 500+ lb deadlift with straight-bar work.

2. **Identify your actual sticking point** by filming your lifts and analyzing where you slow down or miss.

3. **Use targeted accessories** based on that sticking point:
   - Off-floor weakness → paused deadlifts, deficits
   - Midrange weakness → paused knee-level work, quad/glute support
   - Lockout weakness → hip thrusts, block pulls, then consider bands/chains

4. **If you do use bands/chains**, keep them as a **supplemental tool** (1–2x per month), not a primary deadlift variation. Use them for 3–5 reps after your main heavy work, or as a standalone accessory session.

---

## The Bottom Line

Bands and chains work for what they're designed to do: increase load toward lockout and build explosiveness. But they're not a shortcut to absolute strength. The lifters who get the most out of accommodating resistance are those who've already built a strong foundation with heavy barbell work and know exactly why they're using them.

If you're still building your deadlift, spend your energy on:

- Heavy singles and triples
- Paused deadlifts for positional control
- Posterior-chain accessories (RDL, back raises, good mornings)
- Consistent, high-quality technique

That's where the real strength comes from.
