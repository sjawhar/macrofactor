# Bands and Chains for Building Absolute Strength

## The Theory

The logic behind bands and chains is sound in principle. Both create **accommodating resistance**—they increase the load as you move through the range of motion, particularly in the stronger, lockout positions. Here's why this appeals to strength athletes:

1. **Bands**: Tension increases as the band stretches. At the bottom of a squat or deadlift, there's minimal band tension; at the top (lockout), tension is maximal.
2. **Chains**: As you lift, more chain leaves the ground and adds to the bar weight. Again, maximum load at lockout.

The theory is that this matches the strength curve—you're weakest at the bottom and strongest at the top, so accommodating resistance lets you overload the top position where you can handle more weight.

## Do They Actually Work?

**Yes, but with important caveats.**

### What the Evidence Shows

Accommodating resistance (bands and chains) does produce strength gains, but the research is nuanced:

- **Strength gains are real**: Studies show that training with bands/chains produces measurable increases in absolute strength, particularly in the lockout position.
- **Not superior to traditional loading**: The critical finding is that bands/chains don't consistently outperform straight-weight training for overall strength development. When compared head-to-head, traditional heavy barbell work produces similar or sometimes better results.
- **Specificity matters**: Gains are most pronounced in the specific positions trained. If you train with accommodating resistance, you get very good at lockouts with accommodating resistance—but that doesn't always transfer perfectly to raw barbell strength.

### Why They Work (When They Do)

1. **Overload in strong positions**: You can handle more total load in the lockout, which provides a novel stimulus.
2. **Reduced joint stress at the bottom**: Less load at the weakest point can reduce injury risk while still providing a strength stimulus.
3. **Novelty and variation**: Changing stimulus can drive adaptation, especially if you've plateaued on straight weight.
4. **Accommodating resistance matches strength curves**: The theory isn't wrong—it does align with how strength varies through a range.

### Why They're Not a Magic Bullet

1. **Strength is position-specific**: Your body adapts to the specific demands you place on it. If you train with bands, you become strong with bands. Raw barbell strength may not transfer as directly.
2. **Straight weight is simpler and proven**: Heavy barbell training with progressive overload is the most reliable method for building absolute strength. It's been tested across decades and thousands of lifters.
3. **Accommodating resistance adds complexity**: Bands and chains are harder to load precisely, harder to track progress, and require more setup. This complexity can actually reduce consistency.
4. **The bottom position matters**: While accommodating resistance reduces load at the bottom, the bottom position is where many lifters are weakest and where they need the most stimulus. Reducing load there might not be optimal for everyone.

## Practical Recommendations

**Bands and chains work best as supplemental tools, not primary strength builders:**

- **Use them for variation**: Rotate them in 3-4 week blocks to provide novelty and prevent accommodation.
- **Combine with straight weight**: Your primary work should still be heavy barbell lifts. Use accommodating resistance as an accessory or variation.
- **Best for lockout-specific weakness**: If your sticking point is clearly in the top half of a lift, accommodating resistance is a logical choice.
- **Consider the cost-benefit**: The setup complexity and tracking difficulty may not be worth it for most lifters. Simpler methods (pauses, pin presses, heavy singles) often produce similar results.

## The Bottom Line

The logic is sound, and they do work—but they're not a shortcut to strength. They're a tool for variation and addressing specific weaknesses. For building absolute strength, the fundamentals remain unchanged:

1. Heavy barbell training with progressive overload
2. Consistent technique
3. Adequate volume and frequency
4. Proper recovery

Bands and chains can enhance these fundamentals, but they don't replace them. If you're not already strong on straight weight, adding accommodating resistance won't fix that. If you are strong and looking for a new stimulus or addressing a specific weakness, they're worth trying for 3-4 weeks.

The best strength tool is the one you'll use consistently and track progress on. For most lifters, that's still the barbell.
