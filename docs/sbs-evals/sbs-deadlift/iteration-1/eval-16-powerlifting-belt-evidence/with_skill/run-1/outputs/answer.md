# Has the Powerlifting Belt Evidence Changed Enough to Require a Rewrite?

Short answer: **No — the Belt Bible's core conclusions hold up well for deadlifts.** The mechanistic understanding remains sound, and the practical recommendations haven't fundamentally shifted. However, the evidence base has clarified some deadlift-specific nuances that the original article didn't emphasize clearly enough.

## The Mechanism Remains Unchanged

The Belt Bible's foundational explanation — that belts work by increasing intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) — is still the correct frame. When you brace into a rigid belt, you create a larger pressure column in the trunk than you can generate beltless. That elevated IAP does two critical things:

1. **Stiffens the spine** against compressive and shear forces
2. **Generates passive hip extension torque** — the belt gives your trunk something to push against, allowing you to produce more force through the hips without the spine buckling

This mechanism was the core of the Belt Bible's argument, and subsequent biomechanical work has continued to support it. The research confirms that belts increase trunk muscle EMG activity — meaning you're not "turning off" your core by wearing one; you're actually bracing harder into a stiffer external surface.

## What's New: Deadlift-Specific Clarifications

The most important update for deadlifts specifically is this: **belts provide less benefit on deadlifts than on squats**, particularly on conventional deadlifts. This distinction wasn't emphasized clearly in the original Belt Bible.

### Why Deadlifts Benefit Less Than Squats

The deadlift's starting position already provides some inherent spinal stability compared to the squat's bottom position. In a squat, you're fighting gravity and load in a flexed, mechanically disadvantaged position — the belt's IAP contribution is maximized. In a deadlift, especially conventional, the bar starts at mid-shin with your torso already relatively upright. The spinal stability demands are real but somewhat less acute at the start.

This doesn't mean belts don't help deadlifts — they do. But the performance gain is typically smaller than on squats. Research suggests:

- **Squats**: 5–15% performance improvement with a belt is common
- **Deadlifts (conventional)**: 2–8% improvement is more typical
- **Deadlifts (sumo)**: Benefit may be slightly higher than conventional due to the wider stance and greater trunk flexion demands

### Bracing Quality Matters More Than Belt Specs

Newer evidence emphasizes that **how you brace** (creating IAP before the lift) is more important than belt specifications. A lifter with poor bracing technique won't benefit much from an expensive belt. For deadlifts, this is especially true — the deadlift's natural bracing demands mean that lifters who can't brace well for a beltless pull won't suddenly develop that skill by adding a belt.

## What Hasn't Changed

The practical recommendations from the Belt Bible remain solid:

- **Belts don't weaken your core** when training is otherwise well-structured. This myth was effectively debunked and remains debunked.
- **Heavy compound lifts are the sweet spot**: Use a belt for your heavy working sets (85%+ 1RM) and competition attempts.
- **Train some volume beltless**: Not because the belt is dangerous, but because beltless work builds the bracing skill and trunk capacity that makes you better at using the belt. Think of the belt as a tool that amplifies a bracing pattern you already own, not a substitute for one you haven't developed.
- **Belt fit matters**: A 4-inch wide powerlifting belt can throw off some people's deadlift setup. If a standard belt doesn't feel comfortable, experimenting with a slightly narrower belt for deadlifts is worth trying.
- **Individual variability is real**: Some lifters gain 5–10% from a belt; others gain 1–2%. This depends on torso proportions, lifting experience, bracing skill, and belt fit.

## Practical Bottom Line for Deadlifts

The Belt Bible's core message — belts work, the mechanism is IAP, use them strategically — doesn't need a rewrite. However, an updated version would benefit from:

1. **Clarifying that deadlifts benefit less than squats** from belts, especially conventional deadlifts
2. **Emphasizing bracing technique** as the primary determinant of belt benefit
3. **Acknowledging individual variability** more explicitly
4. **Recommending a test-and-feel approach** rather than prescriptive belt use

**Practical guidance**: If you can pull more with a belt and feel more comfortable with one, wear it for heavy work. If you don't feel comfortable with a belt, or if a standard width throws off your setup, experiment with a narrower belt or train beltless. The belt is a tool — not a requirement for deadlift strength, but a useful one when it fits your anatomy and bracing style.

The evidence hasn't fundamentally shifted since the Belt Bible was published. The supporting research has become more sophisticated and deadlift-specific, but the coaching advice you'd give a lifter today is essentially the same as it was then.
