# Does Being 16–19 Hurt Strength Progress? (And Why 405 Isn't the Benchmark You Think It Is)

Short answer: no, being a teenager doesn't hurt your strength progress — and the fact that you didn't hit 405 on linear progression tells us almost nothing meaningful about how you're doing.

Let's unpack both of those.

## Age 16–19 Is Not a Limiter

Teenagers are not at a disadvantage for strength development. If anything, the hormonal environment during late adolescence is quite favorable for building muscle and getting stronger. You're not fighting your biology here. What you _are_ dealing with is simply being early in your training career, which means you're still accumulating the foundational strength, motor skill, and tissue capacity that takes years to build — regardless of age.

The real variable isn't your age. It's your training history, your body size, your leverages, and how well your programming has matched your recovery capacity. A 16-year-old who's been training seriously for two years and eating well can absolutely out-deadlift a 25-year-old who's been half-assing it for the same period.

## 405 Is an Arbitrary Internet Benchmark

Here's the thing about "405 on linear progression" as a benchmark: it's not a real threshold. It's a number that circulates online because it's a round number (four plates) that sounds impressive, and people pattern-match their experience to it. But where linear progression stalls is enormously variable across individuals.

Body size alone explains a huge chunk of this. A 180 lb lifter and a 240 lb lifter running the same novice program will not stall at the same absolute load — the heavier lifter has more muscle mass, more leverages working in their favor, and a higher ceiling before the program's recovery demands outpace adaptation. Limb proportions matter too. Someone with shorter femurs and a longer torso is often mechanically advantaged in the deadlift compared to someone with the opposite build, and that shows up in absolute numbers even when relative strength is similar.

The point is: stalling at 315, 365, or 455 are all equally valid outcomes of running linear progression to its limit. The number itself doesn't tell you whether you did it right or whether you're behind.

## The Right Question: Are You Still Progressing?

The more useful frame isn't "what load did I stall at?" — it's "what's my rate of progress, and is it appropriate for my training stage?"

SBS methodology uses progression timescale to classify where a lifter is:

- **Novice:** Adding weight session-to-session (every workout)
- **Intermediate:** Adding weight week-to-week or every couple of weeks
- **Advanced:** Adding weight month-to-month or slower

If you were adding weight every session for months and then stalled, that's linear progression working exactly as designed. You extracted the fast early gains, and now you need a more sophisticated program that manages fatigue and stimulus more carefully. That's not failure — that's graduation.

If you're still making progress on any timescale, your program is working. The internet's expectation of where you "should" be is irrelevant.

## What to Do Now That Linear Progression Has Run Its Course

Once session-to-session progress stalls, the programming shift is straightforward: move to intermediate programming that manages fatigue more deliberately. For the deadlift specifically, this matters a lot because deadlifts are disproportionately fatiguing compared to the squat and bench. A few practical principles:

- **Drop deadlift volume to roughly 50–67% of your squat volume.** Most lifters underestimate how much deadlift fatigue accumulates and overdo it.
- **Keep heavy deadlift frequency lower than you might expect** — many lifters do best pulling heavy once per week, supplemented by hinge accessories (RDLs, back raises, good mornings) on other days. These build posterior-chain capacity with far less systemic cost.
- **Use accessories to address your specific sticking point.** If you're missing off the floor, paused pulls and deficit work are your tools. If you're losing position mid-pull, erector and lat work becomes the priority.
- **Autoregulate honestly.** If your back and hips feel beaten up going into a deadlift session, delaying 24–48 hours beats forcing low-quality pulls. Technique consistency is what expands your recoverable volume over time.

## The Bottom Line

You're 16–19, you ran linear progression, and you stalled somewhere. That's the normal arc. Your age didn't hold you back, and the load you stalled at doesn't define your ceiling. What matters now is transitioning to a program that can drive progress on a weekly or biweekly timescale, managing deadlift fatigue intelligently, and staying consistent over the years ahead. The lifters who end up genuinely strong aren't the ones who hit arbitrary benchmarks fastest — they're the ones who keep making progress, however slowly, for a long time.
