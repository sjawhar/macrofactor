# Why Deadlifts Are More Fatiguing Than Squats: An SBS Analysis

## The Core Claim

The assertion that deadlifts are "more fatiguing" than squats is **empirically supported in SBS coaching practice**, but the reasons are more nuanced than simple biomechanics. This is not a universal law—it's a pattern that holds for most lifters under typical programming, with important exceptions.

---

## Why Deadlifts Produce Disproportionate Fatigue

### 1. **Systemic Fatigue Load**

Deadlifts demand simultaneous engagement of multiple large muscle groups across a longer range of motion than squats:

- **Posterior chain dominance**: Erectors, glutes, hamstrings, and upper back all contribute meaningfully to the lift.
- **Grip and forearm demand**: Unlike squats (where the bar sits on shoulders), deadlifts require sustained grip tension throughout the pull, adding a secondary fatigue vector.
- **CNS demand**: The deadlift requires higher motor recruitment intensity per rep, especially at heavier loads, because the lift is less "grooved" than a squat for most lifters.

Squats, by contrast, distribute load more predictably through the legs and core, with less demand on grip and upper back.

### 2. **Eccentric Demand Asymmetry**

- **Squats**: The eccentric (lowering) phase is controlled but relatively brief and mechanically straightforward.
- **Deadlifts**: The eccentric is often performed with less control (a controlled drop or partial lower), and when performed with full control, it's mechanically demanding because the lifter must resist the bar's descent while maintaining spinal position.

Even when deadlifts are performed with touch-and-go reps, the eccentric demand is higher than in squats because the bar must be lowered under tension to maintain position.

### 3. **Recovery Demand Mismatch**

Deadlifts create fatigue in tissues that recover more slowly:

- **Erector spinae and posterior chain**: These muscles have high oxidative demand and recover slower than quadriceps-dominant tissues.
- **CNS fatigue**: The deadlift's higher motor recruitment intensity produces greater CNS fatigue per rep, which carries over to subsequent training days.

Squats, while also demanding, distribute fatigue more evenly across quad-dominant tissues that often recover faster.

### 4. **Positional Complexity Under Fatigue**

As fatigue accumulates in deadlifts:

- Setup quality degrades (hips rise, tension is lost, bar drifts forward).
- Technique consistency becomes harder to maintain.
- The risk of form breakdown increases, which either forces load reduction or creates injury risk.

Squats, once grooved, are more forgiving under fatigue—the groove is more automatic, and form degradation is less catastrophic.

---

## The SBS Programming Response: Conservative Volume

Because of these fatigue characteristics, SBS recommends:

### **Start deadlift volume at 50–67% of squat volume**

This is not arbitrary. It reflects the empirical observation that:

- Most lifters cannot recover from deadlift volume equal to squat volume.
- Attempting to match squat volume in deadlifts typically leads to:
  - Degraded technique on subsequent deadlift sessions.
  - Carryover fatigue that impairs other training days.
  - Higher injury risk due to accumulated positional breakdown.

### **Frequency Heuristic: Lower Heavy Deadlift Frequency**

The SBS coaching pattern shows:

- **~1x/week heavy deadlift** works best for most lifters.
- **~2x/week** works for some, but requires careful load management.
- **Higher frequency** is rare and usually only sustainable with reduced intensity or volume per session.

In contrast, **hinge accessories (RDLs, back raises, good mornings, swings) can be trained 3x/week** with lower systemic fatigue cost. This allows posterior-chain development without the full-body fatigue of heavy deadlifting.

---

## Critical Nuance: It's Not Universal

### When deadlifts may NOT be more fatiguing:

1. **Sumo deadlifts** often produce less back fatigue than conventional deadlifts, shifting demand to quads. For some lifters, sumo may be less fatiguing overall.

2. **Lighter deadlift work** (e.g., speed pulls, paused reps at 60–70% 1RM) can be less fatiguing than heavy squat sessions.

3. **Individual variation**: Some lifters have better deadlift recovery than others due to:
   - Stronger erectors and posterior chain (less relative demand).
   - Better movement efficiency (less CNS demand).
   - Favorable recovery capacity for posterior-chain tissues.

4. **Programming context**: A lifter doing 3 squat sessions per week at high volume will accumulate more total fatigue than a single heavy deadlift session.

---

## The Mechanism: Why Lower Volume Works

The conservative deadlift volume recommendation works because:

1. **Quality over quantity**: Fewer, higher-quality deadlift sessions preserve technique and reduce injury risk.

2. **Accessory distribution**: Hinge accessories provide posterior-chain stimulus with lower systemic cost, allowing total hinge volume to remain adequate without overloading the heavy deadlift.

3. **Recovery prioritization**: By keeping heavy deadlift frequency low, lifters preserve recovery capacity for other lifts and avoid the compounding fatigue that comes from multiple heavy deadlift sessions per week.

4. **Autoregulation margin**: Lower baseline deadlift volume leaves room for autoregulation—if a lifter feels strong, they can add volume; if fatigued, they can reduce without falling below minimum effective dose.

---

## Practical Implications

### For most lifters:

- **Deadlift volume**: 1–2 heavy sessions per week, 3–6 sets of 1–5 reps per session.
- **Hinge accessory volume**: 2–3 sessions per week, 2–4 sets of 5–12 reps per session.
- **Total posterior-chain stimulus**: Adequate for strength and hypertrophy without excessive fatigue.

### Red flags for excessive deadlift fatigue:

- Bar speed collapse session-to-session at the same loads.
- Setup inconsistency despite maximal intent.
- Rising back/hip irritation.
- Technique drift early in work sets.

When these appear, adjust deadlift stress (load/sets/frequency) before adding complexity.

---

## Conclusion

Deadlifts are more fatiguing than squats for most lifters because they:

1. Demand simultaneous engagement of multiple large muscle groups.
2. Require higher grip and CNS demand.
3. Create fatigue in slower-recovering tissues (posterior chain).
4. Degrade in quality faster under fatigue.

This is why SBS recommends **starting deadlift volume at 50–67% of squat volume** and **keeping heavy deadlift frequency low (1x/week for most)**. This is not a limitation—it's a recognition of how the body actually recovers from deadlifts, and it allows for sustainable, long-term strength development.

The key insight: **More deadlift volume is not better; more _recoverable_ deadlift volume is better.** Conservative programming with accessory support produces better outcomes than attempting to match squat volume in deadlifts.
