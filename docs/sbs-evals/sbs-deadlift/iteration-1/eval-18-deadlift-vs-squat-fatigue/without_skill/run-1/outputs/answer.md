# Why Deadlifts Are Considered More Fatiguing Than Squats (And Whether They Should Be)

## The Core Claim

The conventional wisdom in strength training is that deadlifts are inherently more fatiguing than squats and therefore require lower training volume. This claim is partially true but oversimplified, and the reasoning behind it matters more than the blanket statement.

## Why Deadlifts Feel More Fatiguing

### 1. **Systemic Fatigue and CNS Demand**

Deadlifts do produce greater systemic fatigue than squats for several legitimate reasons:

- **Whole-body recruitment**: Deadlifts require stabilization and contribution from the entire posterior chain, core, and upper back in a way that squats don't. Even though squats are compound movements, deadlifts demand more total muscle involvement.
- **Central nervous system (CNS) fatigue**: Deadlifts are typically performed with heavier relative loads (lower rep ranges, higher intensity). Heavy singles, doubles, and triples accumulate CNS fatigue faster than the higher-rep work that often accompanies squats.
- **Recovery demand**: The systemic stress of deadlifting—particularly heavy deadlifting—requires more recovery resources than equivalent squat work.

### 2. **Mechanical Advantage and Load Tolerance**

- Squats allow for higher absolute volumes because the movement pattern is more mechanically efficient for the legs. You can squat 3×8 more easily than you can deadlift 3×8 at equivalent intensity.
- Deadlifts have a less favorable mechanical position for the lower back and require more bracing tension throughout the lift, which is metabolically expensive.

### 3. **Psychological Fatigue**

- Deadlifts feel heavier and more intimidating. The movement requires maximal tension from the start, whereas squats allow for a more "rhythmic" descent and ascent.
- This psychological component is real and contributes to perceived fatigue.

## The Nuance: It Depends on How You Program

The claim that "deadlift volume should be lower" is **context-dependent** and often misapplied:

### When Lower Deadlift Volume Makes Sense

1. **Heavy deadlift training (1-5 reps)**: If you're deadlifting at 85%+ of 1RM, you should do fewer total reps per week than you squat. Heavy deadlifts accumulate fatigue quickly.
   - Example: 3-4 sets of 1-3 reps per week is reasonable for heavy deadlifting.

2. **When deadlifts are the primary lift**: If deadlifts are your main focus (e.g., in a deadlift-focused block), you may need to reduce squat volume to manage total lower-body fatigue.

3. **Limited recovery capacity**: Athletes with poor sleep, high stress, or inadequate nutrition will fatigue faster from deadlifts.

### When Deadlift Volume Can Be Higher

1. **Moderate-rep deadlifting (6-10 reps)**: Deadlifts performed at 70-80% intensity can accumulate significant volume without excessive fatigue. Many programs successfully use 3-4 sets of 6-8 deadlifts.

2. **Deadlifts as a secondary lift**: If squats are your primary lower-body lift, deadlifts can be programmed with moderate volume (8-12 total reps per week) without issue.

3. **Variation deadlifts**: Deficit deadlifts, paused deadlifts, or other variations can be done for higher reps with less systemic fatigue than competition deadlifts.

4. **Trained athletes**: Experienced lifters with strong work capacity can tolerate higher deadlift volumes than beginners.

## The Real Principle: Intensity Determines Fatigue

The key insight is that **fatigue is driven by intensity (% of 1RM), not the lift itself**:

- A set of 5 heavy deadlifts (90% 1RM) is more fatiguing than a set of 5 heavy squats (90% 1RM).
- A set of 8 moderate deadlifts (75% 1RM) is less fatiguing than a set of 3 heavy deadlifts (90% 1RM).
- A set of 10 moderate squats (75% 1RM) is roughly equivalent in fatigue to a set of 10 moderate deadlifts (75% 1RM).

## Practical Programming Recommendations

### For Strength-Focused Programs

- **Deadlifts**: 2-3 sessions per week, 3-6 total reps per session at 85%+ intensity
- **Squats**: 2-3 sessions per week, 6-12 total reps per session at 80%+ intensity
- **Rationale**: Heavy deadlifts accumulate fatigue faster, so lower volume is appropriate.

### For Hypertrophy-Focused Programs

- **Deadlifts**: 1-2 sessions per week, 8-12 total reps per session at 70-80% intensity
- **Squats**: 2-3 sessions per week, 12-20 total reps per session at 70-80% intensity
- **Rationale**: At moderate intensities, deadlifts can handle more volume, but squats are mechanically superior for accumulating hypertrophy volume.

### For General Strength + Hypertrophy

- **Deadlifts**: 1-2 sessions per week, 6-10 total reps per session
- **Squats**: 2-3 sessions per week, 8-15 total reps per session
- **Rationale**: Balance fatigue management with the mechanical advantages of each lift.

## The Bottom Line

**The claim is true but incomplete:**

1. **Deadlifts are more fatiguing** when performed at high intensity (85%+ 1RM) because they demand greater systemic effort and CNS activation.

2. **Deadlift volume should usually be lower** than squat volume, but this is primarily because:
   - Most people deadlift heavier (higher intensity)
   - Deadlifts are often programmed as a primary lift with lower reps
   - The movement pattern is less mechanically efficient for high-volume work

3. **If you deadlift at moderate intensity (70-80% 1RM), you can handle more volume** than the conventional wisdom suggests.

4. **The real rule is: match volume to intensity.** Heavy deadlifts need lower volume. Moderate deadlifts can handle more volume. This applies equally to squats.

The mistake many lifters make is assuming deadlifts are inherently more fatiguing regardless of how they're programmed. In reality, a set of 8 deadlifts at 75% 1RM is not dramatically more fatiguing than a set of 8 squats at 75% 1RM. The fatigue difference comes from intensity, not the lift itself.
