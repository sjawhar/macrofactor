# Teen Training and Nutrition for Muscle and Strength Gain

## Exercise Selection

### Compound Movement Foundation

A 15-year-old should prioritize compound exercises that work multiple muscle groups and joints:

- **Lower Body**: Squats (barbell, dumbbell, or bodyweight), deadlifts (conventional or trap bar), lunges, leg press
- **Upper Body Push**: Bench press, incline press, overhead press, push-ups
- **Upper Body Pull**: Rows (barbell, dumbbell, machine), pull-ups, lat pulldowns, face pulls
- **Posterior Chain**: Romanian deadlifts, good mornings, back extensions

### Exercise Progression

- Start with lighter weights to master movement patterns and form
- Prioritize movement quality over load—poor form increases injury risk and reduces effectiveness
- Include 1-2 isolation exercises per session (bicep curls, tricep extensions, leg curls) after compound work
- Rotate exercises every 4-8 weeks to prevent adaptation plateaus and maintain engagement

### Safety Considerations

- Avoid maximal loads and 1-rep max attempts; focus on controlled, moderate weights
- Ensure proper supervision or coaching, especially when learning new movements
- Include adequate warm-up and mobility work
- Progress gradually—add 2-5 lbs per week for upper body, 5-10 lbs per week for lower body

## Weekly Training Volume

### Frequency and Split

A 15-year-old should train **3-5 days per week**:

- **3-day full-body split** (beginner): Each session hits all major muscle groups
  - Example: Monday, Wednesday, Friday
  - Allows 48 hours recovery between sessions
- **4-day upper/lower split** (intermediate):
  - Upper body (2x/week), Lower body (2x/week)
  - Example: Monday upper, Tuesday lower, Thursday upper, Friday lower
- **5-day push/pull/legs split** (more advanced):
  - Push (chest, shoulders, triceps), Pull (back, biceps), Legs (2x/week)
  - Requires good recovery and nutrition

### Sets Per Muscle Group

- **Beginner**: 9-12 total sets per muscle group per week
- **Intermediate**: 12-18 total sets per muscle group per week
- **Advanced**: 15-25 total sets per muscle group per week

Example for a 3-day full-body:

- 3 sets of compound lift (squat, bench, or row)
- 2-3 sets of secondary compound (leg press, incline press, or pull-ups)
- 2-3 sets of isolation work (leg curls, lateral raises, bicep curls)

## Reps, Intensity, and Failure

### Rep Ranges

- **Strength focus (1-6 reps)**: 80-90% of 1-rep max; 3-5 sets; 2-3 min rest
- **Hypertrophy focus (6-12 reps)**: 65-85% of 1-rep max; 3-4 sets; 60-90 sec rest
- **Endurance focus (12-20 reps)**: 50-70% of 1-rep max; 2-3 sets; 30-60 sec rest

For a 15-year-old, **6-12 rep range is optimal** because it:

- Builds muscle size and strength simultaneously
- Allows for proper form and technique development
- Reduces injury risk compared to very heavy loads
- Provides good metabolic stimulus

### Approach to Failure

- **Do NOT train to absolute failure regularly**—this increases injury risk and central nervous system fatigue
- Train to **1-2 reps in reserve (RIR)** on most sets
- On final sets of compound lifts, can approach 0-1 RIR
- Leave 2-3 reps in the tank on isolation exercises
- This approach allows for better recovery and sustainable progression

### Progressive Overload

- Add 1-2 reps per set each week, or
- Add 2-5 lbs (upper body) / 5-10 lbs (lower body) when hitting target reps
- Maintain form quality—never sacrifice technique for heavier weight

## Calories and Macronutrients

### Caloric Surplus for Muscle Gain

A 15-year-old should eat in a **modest caloric surplus** of **300-500 calories above maintenance**:

- **Maintenance calories**: Varies by age, sex, height, weight, and activity level
  - Typical range for active 15-year-olds: 2,200-3,000 calories/day
  - Use online calculators or track intake for 1-2 weeks to establish baseline
- **Surplus approach**: Add 300-500 calories to maintenance
  - Slower surplus = more muscle, less fat gain
  - Faster surplus = faster growth but more fat accumulation
  - Aim for 0.5-1 lb weight gain per week

### Protein

- **Target: 0.7-1.0 grams per pound of body weight** (or 1.6-2.2 g/kg)
- For a 150 lb teen: 105-150g protein daily
- Distribute across 3-4 meals for optimal muscle protein synthesis
- Sources: chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, lean beef, legumes, protein powder

### Carbohydrates

- **Target: 4-7 grams per kilogram of body weight** (or 1.8-3.2 g/lb)
- For a 150 lb teen: 270-480g carbs daily
- Prioritize around training: carbs before and after workouts
- Sources: rice, oats, potatoes, bread, pasta, fruits, vegetables

### Fats

- **Target: 0.5-1.5 grams per kilogram of body weight** (or 0.23-0.68 g/lb)
- For a 150 lb teen: 55-110g fat daily
- Essential for hormone production and overall health
- Sources: olive oil, nuts, avocados, fatty fish, eggs

### Hydration

- Drink **0.5-1 oz of water per pound of body weight daily** (or 15-30 ml/kg)
- For a 150 lb teen: 75-150 oz (2.2-4.4 liters) daily
- Increase intake on training days

### Meal Timing

- **Pre-workout (1-2 hours before)**: Carbs + moderate protein (banana + peanut butter, oatmeal + eggs)
- **Post-workout (within 2 hours)**: Protein + carbs (chicken + rice, protein shake + fruit)
- **Throughout the day**: Consistent protein intake at each meal

## Key Principles for Teen Athletes

1. **Consistency over perfection**: Hitting targets 80% of the time is better than perfect adherence 20% of the time
2. **Sleep is critical**: Aim for 8-10 hours per night—this is when muscle growth occurs
3. **Recovery matters**: Include 1-2 rest days per week; don't train hard every single day
4. **Track progress**: Log workouts and weight to monitor trends
5. **Adjust as needed**: If not gaining weight after 2-3 weeks, add 200 calories; if gaining too fast, reduce by 200
6. **Avoid extreme dieting**: Never cut calories severely while trying to build muscle
7. **Be patient**: Muscle gain is slow—expect 0.5-2 lbs of muscle per month with proper training and nutrition
8. **Focus on health first**: Prioritize movement quality, injury prevention, and sustainable habits over short-term gains
