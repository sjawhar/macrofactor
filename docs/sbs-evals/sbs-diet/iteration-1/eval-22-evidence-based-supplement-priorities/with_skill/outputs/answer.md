# Evidence-Based Supplement Priorities: What Works vs. Hype

**Framework:** SBS Diet (Stronger By Science) evidence-based approach to supplementation.

---

## Core Principle

Supplements are **tools for filling gaps**, not replacements for solid fundamentals. The SBS hierarchy is:

1. **Calories and macros** (non-negotiable)
2. **Protein distribution** (3-6 feedings/day, ≥0.3 g/kg per feeding)
3. **Fiber and hydration** (14 g/1000 kcal fiber; 3-4 L water/day)
4. **Micronutrient adequacy** (RDA/AI targets, stay below UL)
5. **Supplements** (only after the above are solid)

If you're not hitting #1-4, supplements won't fix it.

---

## Tier 1: Evidence-Based (Actually Useful)

### Protein Powder

**Status:** Useful tool, not magic.

- **Why:** Convenient way to hit protein targets (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day for lifters).
- **Evidence:** Whey, casein, plant-based blends all work equally well for muscle retention/gain when total protein and calories are matched.
- **When to use:** When whole-food protein is inconvenient or expensive.
- **Dose:** Aim for ≥0.3 g/kg protein per feeding; ~2-3 g leucine per dose.
- **Red flag:** If you're using protein powder to hit calories instead of hitting protein targets, you're doing it wrong.

### Creatine Monohydrate

**Status:** One of the most-studied supplements. Genuinely effective.

- **Why:** Increases phosphocreatine availability in muscle, improving high-intensity performance and muscle retention.
- **Evidence:** Consistent 5-15% strength/power gains in resistance training; modest hypertrophy benefit (~0.5-1 kg lean mass over 8-12 weeks).
- **Dose:** 3-5 g/day (no loading phase needed; takes 3-4 weeks to saturate).
- **Cost:** ~$0.05-0.10/day. Best ROI of any supplement.
- **Safety:** Decades of safety data; no UL set by regulatory bodies.
- **Caveat:** Only works if you're training hard and eating enough protein/calories.

### Caffeine

**Status:** Effective ergogenic aid.

- **Why:** Increases alertness, reduces perceived effort, improves power output and endurance.
- **Evidence:** 3-6 mg/kg body weight improves performance in most people; effects are dose-dependent and individual.
- **Dose:** 200-400 mg pre-workout (or 3-6 mg/kg).
- **Timing:** 30-60 min before training.
- **Caveat:** Tolerance builds; cycling (5-7 days off every 4-6 weeks) helps maintain effect. Not a substitute for sleep.

### Vitamin D (if deficient)

**Status:** Useful if you're low; not a performance enhancer if replete.

- **Why:** Supports immune function, bone health, mood, and possibly muscle function.
- **Evidence:** Deficiency (<20 ng/mL) is associated with poor outcomes; supplementation to 30-50 ng/mL is reasonable.
- **Dose:** 1000-4000 IU/day (or 25,000 IU weekly) depending on baseline and sun exposure.
- **When to supplement:** If you live in a high-latitude climate, train indoors, or have low baseline levels.
- **Red flag:** Megadosing (>10,000 IU/day long-term) without monitoring can cause toxicity.

### Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)

**Status:** Useful if your diet is low in fatty fish; modest benefit otherwise.

- **Why:** Supports cardiovascular health, inflammation management, and possibly cognitive function.
- **Evidence:** Benefit is clearest in people with low baseline intake or elevated triglycerides.
- **Dose:** 1-3 g combined EPA+DHA/day (or eat fatty fish 2-3×/week).
- **Caveat:** High doses can thin blood; check with your doctor if on anticoagulants.

### Iron (if deficient)

**Status:** Essential if you're low; not a performance enhancer if replete.

- **Why:** Oxygen transport; deficiency causes fatigue and poor performance.
- **Evidence:** Supplementation works only if you're actually deficient (check ferritin/serum iron).
- **Dose:** 8-18 mg/day (RDA); higher doses only if clinically deficient.
- **Red flag:** Iron overload is toxic; don't supplement without testing.
- **Higher-risk groups:** Vegetarians/vegans, menstruating women, endurance athletes.

### Magnesium (if deficient)

**Status:** Useful if low; modest benefit for sleep/recovery if replete.

- **Why:** Supports muscle function, sleep quality, and stress response.
- **Evidence:** Deficiency is common (especially in high-training contexts); supplementation to RDA (310-420 mg/day) is reasonable.
- **Dose:** 200-400 mg/day (split doses reduce GI upset).
- **Form:** Glycinate or threonate may be better absorbed than oxide.
- **Caveat:** Excess magnesium causes diarrhea; titrate up gradually.

### Zinc (if deficient)

**Status:** Essential if low; not a performance enhancer if replete.

- **Why:** Immune function, protein synthesis, hormone production.
- **Evidence:** Deficiency impairs performance; supplementation to RDA (8-11 mg/day) is reasonable.
- **Dose:** 8-11 mg/day (RDA); higher doses only if deficient.
- **Red flag:** Chronic excess (>40 mg/day) can impair copper absorption.
- **Higher-risk groups:** Vegans, people in caloric deficit, high-sweat athletes.

## Tier 2: Modest Evidence (Situational)

### Beta-Alanine

**Status:** Small, consistent effect; not a game-changer.

- **Why:** Increases muscle carnosine, which buffers lactate in high-rep/high-intensity work.
- **Evidence:** ~2-3% improvement in performance in 1-4 min efforts (e.g., 8-15 rep sets, 400m sprint).
- **Dose:** 3-5 g/day (split into 3-5 doses to avoid tingling).
- **Caveat:** Takes 4-6 weeks to saturate; tingling is harmless but annoying.
- **When useful:** If you do a lot of high-rep hypertrophy or short-duration cardio.

### Beetroot Juice / Nitrates

**Status:** Small, consistent effect; mainly for endurance.

- **Why:** Increases nitric oxide, improving blood flow and oxygen efficiency.
- **Evidence:** ~2-3% improvement in endurance performance; minimal benefit for strength.
- **Dose:** 500 mL (6-8 mmol nitrate) 2-3 hours pre-workout.
- **Caveat:** Expensive; whole beets or beet powder are cheaper alternatives.
- **When useful:** If you do endurance training (running, cycling, rowing).

### Citrulline Malate

**Status:** Modest evidence for pump and recovery; not a strength game-changer.

- **Why:** Increases arginine and nitric oxide; may reduce fatigue and improve blood flow.
- **Evidence:** ~5-10% improvement in rep volume in some studies; effects are inconsistent.
- **Dose:** 6-8 g pre-workout.
- **Caveat:** Expensive; creatine + caffeine are better ROI.

### Sodium Bicarbonate (Baking Soda)

**Status:** Small effect; mainly for high-rep/high-intensity work.

- **Why:** Buffers lactate in high-intensity efforts.
- **Evidence:** ~2-3% improvement in 1-7 min efforts; GI distress is common.
- **Dose:** 0.3 g/kg body weight, 60-90 min pre-workout.
- **Caveat:** Causes bloating and diarrhea in many people; not worth the hassle for most.

### Tart Cherry Juice

**Status:** Modest evidence for recovery; not a performance enhancer.

- **Why:** Contains anthocyanins, which may reduce inflammation and improve sleep.
- **Evidence:** Small improvements in recovery and sleep quality in some studies; effects are inconsistent.
- **Dose:** 240-480 mL/day (or 1-2 servings of concentrate).
- **Caveat:** Expensive; sleep and stress management are more important.

## Tier 3: Weak or No Evidence (Mostly Hype)

### BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids)

**Status:** Hype. Whole protein is better.

- **Why:** Marketed as muscle-building, but they're just 3 of 9 essential amino acids.
- **Evidence:** No advantage over whole protein when total protein is matched. Leucine alone (from whole protein) is sufficient.
- **The problem:** BCAAs are expensive and incomplete; you're paying for marketing.
- **Better alternative:** Whey protein or any complete protein source.

### HMB (β-Hydroxy β-Methylbutyrate)

**Status:** Weak evidence; not worth the cost.

- **Why:** Marketed as a muscle-building metabolite of leucine.
- **Evidence:** Small effects in untrained people or during extreme deficits; negligible in trained lifters eating adequate protein.
- **Dose:** 3 g/day.
- **Caveat:** Expensive; leucine from whole protein is cheaper and sufficient.

### Tribulus Terrestris

**Status:** Hype. No effect on testosterone.

- **Why:** Marketed as a testosterone booster.
- **Evidence:** No consistent effect on testosterone or performance in humans.
- **Better alternative:** Train hard, eat enough, sleep 7-9 hours.

### Testosterone Boosters (D-Aspartate, Fenugreek, etc.)

**Status:** Hype. Minimal to no effect.

- **Why:** Marketed as natural testosterone enhancers.
- **Evidence:** Most show no effect or tiny effects in specific populations (e.g., infertile men). Not useful for healthy lifters.
- **Better alternative:** Train hard, eat enough, sleep 7-9 hours, manage stress.

### Fat Burners (Caffeine + Synephrine + Others)

**Status:** Hype. Caffeine is the only active ingredient.

- **Why:** Marketed as metabolism boosters.
- **Evidence:** The only consistent effect is from caffeine (which you can get for $0.10/day). Other ingredients add cost, not benefit.
- **Better alternative:** Caffeine alone + caloric deficit.

### Collagen / Gelatin

**Status:** Weak evidence; not better than whey protein.

- **Why:** Marketed for joint health and skin.
- **Evidence:** Collagen is incomplete protein (low in leucine); no clear advantage over whey for muscle or joint health.
- **Caveat:** If you have joint pain, training technique and load management matter more.
- **Better alternative:** Whey protein + adequate total protein.

### Glutamine

**Status:** Hype. No advantage over whole protein.

- **Why:** Marketed as immune-supporting and muscle-sparing.
- **Evidence:** No advantage over whole protein when total protein is matched. Your body makes glutamine from other amino acids.
- **Better alternative:** Whey protein or any complete protein.

### Carnitine

**Status:** Hype for most people. Useful only if deficient.

- **Why:** Marketed as a fat-burning amino acid.
- **Evidence:** No effect on fat loss in people with adequate carnitine (which is most people eating meat). Tiny effect in vegans/vegetarians.
- **Better alternative:** Caloric deficit + training.

### Conjugated Linoleic Acid (CLA)

**Status:** Hype. Minimal effect.

- **Why:** Marketed as a fat-burning fatty acid.
- **Evidence:** ~0.5-1 kg fat loss over 12 weeks (vs. placebo); not clinically meaningful.
- **Better alternative:** Caloric deficit + training.

### Garcinia Cambogia / Hydroxycitric Acid (HCA)

**Status:** Hype. No effect on fat loss.

- **Why:** Marketed as an appetite suppressant.
- **Evidence:** No consistent effect on weight loss in humans.
- **Better alternative:** Caloric deficit + training.

### Conjugated Linoleic Acid (CLA)

**Status:** Hype. Minimal effect.

- **Why:** Marketed as a fat-burning fatty acid.
- **Evidence:** ~0.5-1 kg fat loss over 12 weeks (vs. placebo); not clinically meaningful.
- **Better alternative:** Caloric deficit + training.

### Proprietary "Blends"

**Status:** Hype. Underdosed and unproven.

- **Why:** Marketed as synergistic formulas.
- **Evidence:** Ingredients are often underdosed; synergy claims are rarely tested.
- **Better alternative:** Buy individual supplements with transparent dosing.

## Tier 4: Avoid (Dangerous or Banned)

### Anabolic Steroids and Prohormones

**Status:** Illegal and dangerous.

- **Why:** Synthetic testosterone and derivatives.
- **Evidence:** Effective for muscle gain, but with serious side effects (liver damage, cardiovascular disease, hormonal disruption, infertility).
- **Legal status:** Controlled substances in most countries.

### Stimulant-Heavy Fat Burners (Ephedrine, DMAA)

**Status:** Banned or dangerous.

- **Why:** Extreme stimulants marketed for fat loss.
- **Evidence:** Effective for fat loss, but with serious cardiovascular risks (heart attack, stroke).
- **Legal status:** Banned by FDA (DMAA) or heavily restricted (ephedrine).

## SBS Supplement Decision Framework

**Use this checklist before buying any supplement:**

1. **Are calories and macros dialed in?** (If no, stop here.)
2. **Is protein intake adequate?** (1.6-2.2 g/kg for lifters; if no, buy protein powder.)
3. **Is fiber intake adequate?** (14 g/1000 kcal; if no, eat more vegetables.)
4. **Is hydration adequate?** (3-4 L/day; if no, drink water.)
5. **Are micronutrients covered?** (RDA/AI targets; if no, test and supplement deficiencies.)
6. **Is training hard and consistent?** (If no, supplements won't help.)
7. **Is sleep 7-9 hours/night?** (If no, fix sleep first.)

**Only after all seven are yes:** Consider Tier 1 supplements (creatine, caffeine, vitamin D if low, omega-3 if low, iron/magnesium/zinc if deficient).

## Practical Supplement Stack (Evidence-Based)

### Minimal Stack (Best ROI)

- **Protein powder** (if convenient)
- **Creatine monohydrate** (3-5 g/day)
- **Caffeine** (200-400 mg pre-workout)
- **Multivitamin** (if diet is inconsistent)

**Cost:** ~$30-50/month. **Effect:** 5-15% performance improvement if training is hard.

### Comprehensive Stack (If budget allows)

- **Protein powder**
- **Creatine monohydrate**
- **Caffeine**
- **Vitamin D** (if low; 2000-4000 IU/day)
- **Omega-3** (if low fish intake; 1-3 g EPA+DHA/day)
- **Magnesium** (if low; 200-400 mg/day)
- **Zinc** (if low; 8-11 mg/day)

**Cost:** ~$60-100/month. **Effect:** Covers most micronutrient gaps + modest performance boost.

## The Bottom Line

**What actually works:**

- Calories and macros (non-negotiable)
- Protein distribution (3-6 feedings/day)
- Creatine (5-15% strength/power boost)
- Caffeine (3-6% performance boost)
- Micronutrient adequacy (especially if deficient)

**What's mostly hype:**

- BCAAs, HMB, testosterone boosters, fat burners, collagen, glutamine, carnitine, CLA, garcinia
- Proprietary blends with underdosed ingredients
- Anything marketed as a "shortcut" to muscle or fat loss

**The hard truth:**
Supplements are 5-10% of the equation. The other 90% is:

1. Eating enough calories and protein
2. Training hard and consistently
3. Sleeping 7-9 hours/night
4. Managing stress
5. Being patient (body composition changes take weeks/months, not days)

If you're not nailing #1-5, no supplement will fix it. If you are nailing #1-5, creatine + caffeine + adequate micronutrients will give you the remaining 5-10%.
