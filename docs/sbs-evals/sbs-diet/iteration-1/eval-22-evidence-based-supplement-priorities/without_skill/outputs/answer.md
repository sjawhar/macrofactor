# Evidence-Based Supplement Priorities

## Supplements with Strong Evidence

### Tier 1: Essential & Universally Recommended

**Vitamin D**

- Evidence: Extensive RCTs show deficiency is common and linked to bone health, immune function, and mood
- Dosage: 1,000-4,000 IU daily for most adults; higher for deficient individuals
- Cost-benefit: Excellent—cheap, safe, and most people benefit from supplementation
- Note: Blood levels should be 30-50 ng/mL; many people are deficient

**Creatine Monohydrate**

- Evidence: Hundreds of peer-reviewed studies; one of the most researched supplements
- Effect: 5-15% strength/power gains in resistance training; improves muscle mass over time
- Dosage: 5g daily (no loading phase needed, though 20g/day × 5-7 days speeds saturation)
- Safety: Excellent safety profile; no kidney damage in healthy individuals
- Cost-benefit: Exceptional—$10-20/month for proven results

**Protein Powder**

- Evidence: Not a supplement per se, but a food; meeting protein targets (0.7-1g per lb bodyweight) is essential for muscle building
- Efficacy: Whey, casein, and plant-based proteins all work if total protein intake is adequate
- Cost-benefit: Convenient and cost-effective vs. whole food sources

### Tier 2: Strong Evidence for Specific Goals

**Caffeine**

- Evidence: Hundreds of studies; 3-6mg/kg bodyweight improves strength, power, and endurance
- Dosage: 200-400mg per session; tolerance develops, so cycling is beneficial
- Caveat: Individual response varies; some people are poor metabolizers
- Cost-benefit: Excellent if you tolerate it

**Beta-Alanine**

- Evidence: Solid research for high-rep resistance training and repeated sprint performance
- Effect: ~2-3% improvement in work capacity; requires 4-6 weeks of loading (3-5g daily)
- Caveat: Causes harmless tingling sensation; benefits mainly for 1-10 minute efforts
- Cost-benefit: Modest gains; useful for specific training blocks

**Sodium Bicarbonate (Baking Soda)**

- Evidence: Reduces blood acidity during high-intensity efforts; improves repeated sprint performance
- Dosage: 0.3g per kg bodyweight, 60-90 minutes before training
- Caveat: GI distress is common; requires experimentation
- Cost-benefit: Cheap but modest gains; mainly for competitive athletes

**Citrulline Malate**

- Evidence: Improves blood flow and reduces fatigue in high-rep training
- Dosage: 6-8g, 60 minutes before training
- Effect: ~10-15% improvement in reps-to-failure; better pump sensation
- Cost-benefit: Modest cost for modest gains

## Supplements with Weak or Mixed Evidence

### Tier 3: Modest Evidence, Niche Use

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Fish Oil)**

- Evidence: Mixed; cardiovascular benefits are overstated in recent meta-analyses
- Reality: Whole fish provides benefits beyond just omega-3s; supplementation shows minimal benefit for healthy people with adequate fish intake
- Useful for: People who don't eat fatty fish; may help with inflammation in specific conditions
- Cost-benefit: Low priority unless you avoid fish

**Magnesium**

- Evidence: Deficiency is real and linked to sleep and muscle function
- Reality: Most people get adequate magnesium from food; supplementation helps only if deficient
- Useful for: Sleep quality (glycinate or threonate forms); muscle cramps if deficient
- Cost-benefit: Worth trying if you have sleep issues or muscle cramps; cheap

**Zinc**

- Evidence: Essential mineral; deficiency impairs immune function and testosterone
- Reality: Most people get enough from food; supplementation only helps if deficient
- Useful for: Vegetarians/vegans; people with poor absorption
- Cost-benefit: Low priority unless deficient

**Vitamin B12**

- Evidence: Essential for energy and neurological function
- Reality: Deficiency is real but uncommon in omnivores; vegans/vegetarians should supplement
- Useful for: Vegans, vegetarians, people with absorption issues
- Cost-benefit: Cheap insurance for at-risk groups

## Supplements with Weak Evidence (Mostly Hype)

### Tier 4: Minimal or No Meaningful Evidence

**BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids)**

- Evidence: Minimal benefit if total protein intake is adequate
- Reality: Whole protein sources (whey, meat, eggs) contain BCAAs plus other amino acids
- Verdict: Waste of money for most people; only useful if training fasted (rare scenario)

**Testosterone Boosters (Tribulus, Fenugreek, D-Aspartic Acid)**

- Evidence: Weak to nonexistent for healthy men
- Reality: May have minor effects in deficient populations; no effect in normal-range individuals
- Verdict: Hype; save your money

**Fat Burners (Caffeine + Proprietary Blends)**

- Evidence: Caffeine works; the rest is mostly filler
- Reality: Thermogenic effect is modest (~100-200 kcal/day); diet and training matter far more
- Verdict: Expensive caffeine; just drink coffee

**Collagen Peptides**

- Evidence: Weak for joint health; no better than regular protein
- Reality: Collagen is just protein; your body breaks it down and rebuilds it
- Verdict: Marketing hype; regular protein is cheaper and equally effective

**Glutamine**

- Evidence: Minimal benefit for muscle building or recovery in people eating adequate protein
- Reality: Your body produces glutamine; supplementation doesn't meaningfully increase muscle protein synthesis
- Verdict: Hype; not worth the cost

**Carnitine**

- Evidence: Only useful if you have a carnitine deficiency (rare)
- Reality: Your body produces carnitine; supplementation doesn't improve fat loss or performance in healthy people
- Verdict: Hype for most people

**Conjugated Linoleic Acid (CLA)**

- Evidence: Minimal fat loss benefit; studies show ~1-2 lbs over months
- Reality: Effect size is too small to matter practically
- Verdict: Hype; diet and training are what matter

**Garcinia Cambogia / Hydroxycitric Acid**

- Evidence: No meaningful effect on weight loss in humans
- Reality: Animal studies don't translate; human trials show no benefit
- Verdict: Pure hype; avoid

**Detox Supplements / Cleanses**

- Evidence: None; your liver and kidneys already detoxify
- Reality: These products are unnecessary and sometimes harmful
- Verdict: Marketing scam; avoid entirely

**Nootropics (Most Unproven Ones)**

- Evidence: Most lack human RCT evidence
- Reality: Caffeine + sleep + exercise are the proven cognitive enhancers
- Verdict: Hype for most; stick to basics

## Practical Supplement Strategy

### For Most People (Minimal Stack)

1. **Vitamin D** — 2,000-4,000 IU daily (unless you get sun exposure)
2. **Protein powder** — As needed to hit daily protein target
3. **Creatine** — 5g daily if you do resistance training
4. **Caffeine** — Pre-workout if you tolerate it

**Cost:** ~$30-50/month | **Evidence:** Excellent

### For Serious Strength Athletes

Add to the above:

- Beta-alanine (3-5g daily during training blocks)
- Citrulline malate (6-8g pre-workout)
- Magnesium (if sleep is poor)

**Cost:** ~$60-80/month | **Evidence:** Good to excellent

### For Endurance Athletes

- Caffeine (3-6mg/kg pre-event)
- Sodium bicarbonate (if doing repeated high-intensity efforts)
- Electrolytes (if training >90 minutes)

**Cost:** ~$20-40/month | **Evidence:** Good

## Key Principles

1. **Whole food first** — Supplements fill gaps, not replace diet
2. **Deficiency-driven** — Supplement if you're deficient, not "just in case"
3. **Dose matters** — Many supplements fail because people use ineffective doses
4. **Consistency beats novelty** — Creatine for 6 months beats trying 10 different things
5. **Training and diet are 95%** — No supplement replaces hard work and good nutrition
6. **Be skeptical of proprietary blends** — If the dose isn't listed, it's probably ineffective
7. **Beware of marketing** — "Natural," "clinically proven," and "scientifically formulated" are marketing terms, not guarantees

---

## Bottom Line

**Worth your money:**

- Vitamin D, creatine, protein powder, caffeine

**Maybe worth it:**

- Beta-alanine, citrulline malate, magnesium (if deficient), omega-3s (if you don't eat fish)

**Waste of money:**

- BCAAs, testosterone boosters, fat burners, collagen, glutamine, carnitine, CLA, garcinia, detox products, most nootropics

The best supplement is the one you'll actually use consistently. Creatine and vitamin D are cheap, safe, and proven—start there.
