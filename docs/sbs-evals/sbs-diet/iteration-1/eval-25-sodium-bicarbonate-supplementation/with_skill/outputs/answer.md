# Sodium Bicarbonate Supplementation for HIIT and Lactate-Threshold Workouts

## Short Answer

**Yes, sodium bicarbonate has legitimate evidence for reducing fatigue in high-intensity interval and lactate-threshold work.** However, its practical utility depends on your training context, tolerance, and whether you've already optimized the fundamentals (calories, protein, carbs, hydration).

---

## The Mechanism

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) works as a **buffer** for lactate and hydrogen ions produced during high-intensity effort. During HIIT and lactate-threshold work, muscles accumulate lactate and H⁺ ions, which contribute to fatigue and reduced force production. Bicarbonate helps neutralize these ions, theoretically extending time-to-fatigue or allowing higher power output before fatigue sets in.

This is not a myth—the mechanism is real and well-studied.

---

## Evidence Quality

The research consensus is **moderately strong**:

- **Meta-analyses** show sodium bicarbonate supplementation produces small but consistent improvements in high-intensity exercise performance (typically **2–3% gains** in power output, time-to-fatigue, or repeated-sprint capacity).
- **Effect sizes** are small but real, especially in repeated-effort protocols (multiple sets/rounds of HIIT, lactate-threshold intervals).
- **Responders vs. non-responders**: Not everyone benefits equally. Individual variation is substantial.

---

## When It's Most Useful

Sodium bicarbonate is most likely to help in these contexts:

1. **Repeated high-intensity efforts** (e.g., multiple rounds of 30-second sprints with short rest, or multiple lactate-threshold intervals)
2. **Sports with lactate accumulation** (rowing, cycling, running, combat sports, team sports with repeated sprints)
3. **Training phases** where you're pushing lactate-threshold work or HIIT as a primary stimulus
4. **When fundamentals are already solid** (adequate calories, protein, carbs, hydration)

It's **less useful** for:

- Single, all-out efforts (one max-effort set)
- Steady-state endurance work (lactate is not the primary limiter)
- Strength training (lactate buffering is not the main performance driver)

---

## Practical Dosing and Timing

**Standard protocol:**

- **Dose**: 0.3 g/kg bodyweight (roughly 20–30 g for most people)
- **Timing**: 60–90 minutes before the workout
- **Frequency**: Acute use (single dose before a session) is most studied; chronic daily use is less common

**Example**: A 70 kg person would take ~21 g of sodium bicarbonate 60–90 minutes before a HIIT or lactate-threshold session.

---

## Side Effects and Tolerability

This is the **biggest practical limitation**:

- **GI distress** is common (nausea, bloating, diarrhea, cramping)
- Severity varies widely; some people tolerate it well, others do not
- **Mitigation strategies**:
  - Take with food or a carbohydrate-containing drink
  - Use enteric-coated capsules (slower absorption, less GI upset)
  - Start with a lower dose and titrate up
  - Time it 60–90 minutes before exercise (not immediately before)

If GI distress is severe, the supplement is not practical for you, and that's a legitimate reason to skip it.

---

## How It Fits Into the SBS Framework

From a **Stronger By Science diet perspective**, sodium bicarbonate is a **performance supplement**, not a nutrition fundamental. The priority hierarchy is:

1. **Calories** (matched to your goal)
2. **Protein** (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day for lifters)
3. **Carbohydrates** (3–4 g/kg/day for high-intensity training; 6+ g/kg for endurance)
4. **Fat** (above the height-based floor)
5. **Hydration** (3–4 L/day baseline)
6. **Micronutrients and fiber** (RDA/AI targets)
7. **Meal timing and pattern** (adherence-driven)
8. **Supplements** (including sodium bicarbonate)

**If any of the first six are not dialed in, fix those first.** A 2–3% performance gain from bicarbonate is meaningless if you're undereating carbs, under-hydrated, or not hitting protein targets.

---

## Carbohydrate Context (Critical for HIIT/Lactate-Threshold Work)

Sodium bicarbonate works best when **carbohydrate availability is adequate**. Here's why:

- HIIT and lactate-threshold work are **carbohydrate-dependent** energy systems
- If you're under-fueled (low carbs, low calories), lactate buffering becomes secondary to fuel availability
- **SBS carb targets for high-intensity training**: at least **3–4 g/kg/day** (or 6+ g/kg for endurance-heavy training)

**Practical implication**: Before adding sodium bicarbonate, ensure you're hitting carb targets for your training volume. A well-fueled athlete with adequate carbs will see more benefit from bicarbonate than an under-fueled one.

---

## Hydration and Electrolytes

Sodium bicarbonate adds **sodium** to your intake. This is generally not a problem if:

- You're already meeting baseline hydration (3–4 L/day)
- Your diet includes normal sodium sources (salt in food, electrolyte drinks during long sessions)
- You're not in an extreme heat or sweat-loss scenario

For most people, the sodium from a single bicarbonate dose is negligible relative to daily intake. However, if you're already managing sodium carefully (e.g., for water-weight control in a cut), account for it.

---

## Individual Variation and Responder Status

**Not everyone responds equally.** Factors affecting response:

- **Genetics**: Some people have naturally higher buffering capacity; others benefit more from exogenous bicarbonate
- **Training status**: Well-trained athletes may see smaller relative gains than untrained individuals
- **Fiber type composition**: Athletes with more fast-twitch muscle may respond better (though this is not a practical screening tool)
- **GI tolerance**: If you can't tolerate it, you can't use it

**Practical approach**: If you're curious, try it in a non-critical training session first. If you tolerate it and feel a difference, use it for important HIIT or lactate-threshold sessions. If you don't tolerate it or see no benefit, skip it.

---

## Bottom Line

**Sodium bicarbonate is a legitimate, evidence-backed supplement for HIIT and lactate-threshold training.** It produces small but real performance gains (2–3%) in repeated high-intensity efforts.

**However:**

- It's a **tier-2 intervention**—only useful if fundamentals (calories, protein, carbs, hydration) are already solid
- **GI side effects** are the main practical barrier; not everyone can tolerate it
- **Individual response varies widely**; some people benefit, others don't
- It's **not a substitute** for adequate carbohydrate intake, which is the primary fuel for HIIT/lactate-threshold work

**Recommendation**: If you're doing regular HIIT or lactate-threshold training, your carbs are dialed in, and you tolerate sodium bicarbonate well, it's worth trying in key sessions. If GI distress is an issue or you haven't optimized the fundamentals, focus there first.
