# Sodium Bicarbonate Supplementation for HIIT and Lactate-Threshold Workouts

## Summary

Yes, sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) supplementation has demonstrated utility for HIIT and lactate-threshold style workouts, though the effect size is modest and individual responses vary considerably.

## Mechanism

During high-intensity exercise, muscles produce lactate and hydrogen ions (H+), which accumulate and lower muscle pH. This acidification is thought to contribute to fatigue and reduced performance. Sodium bicarbonate acts as a buffer, helping neutralize these hydrogen ions and maintain a higher muscle pH during intense effort. This buffering capacity may delay fatigue onset and allow athletes to sustain higher power outputs or work longer before hitting their limit.

## Evidence for HIIT and Lactate-Threshold Work

The research generally supports sodium bicarbonate's effectiveness for:

- **High-intensity interval training (HIIT)**: Studies show modest improvements in repeated sprint performance and total work output, particularly in efforts lasting 1–10 minutes where lactate accumulation is a primary limiting factor.
- **Lactate-threshold workouts**: Performance at or near lactate threshold (the intensity where lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared) may improve, allowing athletes to sustain higher intensities for longer.

Typical improvements range from 2–3% in performance metrics, which is meaningful in competitive contexts but not transformative.

## Practical Considerations

**Dosing**: The standard protocol is 0.3 g per kg of body weight, taken 60–90 minutes before exercise. For a 70 kg person, this is roughly 21 grams (about 5 teaspoons).

**Gastrointestinal side effects**: A major limitation is that sodium bicarbonate commonly causes nausea, bloating, and GI distress, especially at higher doses. This can offset performance gains or make training uncomfortable. Splitting the dose or taking it with food may help.

**Individual variability**: Some athletes respond well; others see minimal benefit or experience intolerable GI symptoms.

**Timing and context**: The effect is most relevant for efforts where lactate accumulation is the primary fatigue driver—roughly 1–10 minutes of near-maximal intensity. For longer, steady-state efforts or very short sprints, the benefit is less clear.

## Verdict

Sodium bicarbonate is a legitimate ergogenic aid for HIIT and lactate-threshold training, backed by reasonable evidence. However, it's not a magic bullet. The performance gain is modest, and GI tolerance is often the limiting factor. It's worth experimenting with in training (not competition) to see if you tolerate it well and notice a meaningful difference. If you can manage the side effects, it may provide a small edge in high-intensity work. If GI distress is severe, the cost-benefit doesn't favor its use.
