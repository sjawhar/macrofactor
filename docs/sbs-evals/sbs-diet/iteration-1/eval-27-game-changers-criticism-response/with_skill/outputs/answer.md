## What "Game Changers" Actually Says

### On Meat and Fighting Performance

The film makes this argument:

- Shows a study where elite athletes (including fighters) had improved blood flow and erectile function after eating a plant-based meal vs. a meat-based meal
- Implies this blood-flow difference translates to better athletic performance
- Suggests meat consumption impairs vascular function, which would hurt performance

**The actual evidence gap:**

- The film does NOT show that plant-based diets improve _fighting performance_ in controlled trials
- It shows acute vascular response to a single meal, not chronic performance outcomes
- It does NOT control for total calories, protein, or carbohydrate content of the meals compared
- Elite fighters (the film's examples) typically eat meat and perform at the highest level—the film doesn't address this contradiction

**SBS-consistent framing:** The film presents a mechanistic hypothesis (vascular function → performance) without showing the outcome it claims (actual performance improvement). This is a reasoning gap, not a lie.

### On Cows vs. Humans Muscularity

The film's argument:

- Shows a cow (herbivore) with large muscles
- Asks: "How did the cow get so big eating only plants?"
- Implies humans should be able to do the same

**The actual evidence gap:**

- Cows are ruminants with a specialized 4-chambered stomach that ferments plant material for ~20 hours/day
- Humans have a simple stomach and cannot extract the same amino acid density from plants
- Cows also have different protein requirements, growth rates, and lifespans than humans
- The film does NOT address these biological differences

**SBS-consistent framing:** The film uses a rhetorical comparison without accounting for fundamental digestive and metabolic differences. This is a false analogy, not a lie.

## The Broader Point

"Game Changers" is a well-made advocacy film. Advocacy films are designed to persuade, not to present all counterarguments. That's not dishonest—it's the format.

A fair critique of the film is: "It presents compelling mechanistic arguments and athlete testimonials, but doesn't show controlled outcome data on performance, and it doesn't address why elite meat-eating athletes exist."

An unfair critique would be: "It's a lie" or "It proves nothing."

The truth is usually in the middle: the film makes interesting points that deserve investigation, but it doesn't prove what it implies.
