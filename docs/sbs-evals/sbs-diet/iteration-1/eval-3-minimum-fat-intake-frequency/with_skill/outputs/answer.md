# Is It Okay to Treat Minimum Fat Intake as a Weekly Target Instead of Daily?

## Short Answer

**No.** The SBS framework treats the fat floor as a **daily minimum**, not a weekly average. Hitting it daily is the standard approach.

---

## Why Daily, Not Weekly

### 1. **The Fat Floor Is a Physiological Minimum**

The SBS height-based fat floor exists to protect essential functions:

- **Hormone production** (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol all require adequate fat)
- **Fat-soluble vitamin absorption** (vitamins A, D, E, K)
- **Cell membrane integrity** and nervous system function
- **Satiety and adherence** (fat helps you feel full and stick to your diet)

These functions operate **every single day**. A day with zero or near-zero fat intake creates a physiological deficit that cannot be "made up" the next day.

### 2. **The Floor Formula Is Conservative by Design**

The SBS formula:

- Height < 150 cm: **30 g/day minimum**
- Height ≥ 150 cm: **((height_cm − 150) × 0.5) + 30 g/day**

This is already a **minimum safety threshold**, not a target. It's set low enough to be achievable even in aggressive cuts. Treating it as a weekly average would mean some days fall below this floor, which defeats its purpose.

### 3. **Practical Adherence and Tracking**

Weekly averaging introduces tracking complexity and behavioral risk:

- **Harder to monitor**: You'd need to track cumulative fat across the week and adjust mid-week, which is cognitively taxing.
- **Easier to rationalize undereating**: "I'll eat 20g today and make it up Friday" often becomes "I'll eat 20g today and 20g Friday" (still short).
- **Inconsistent nutrient timing**: Fat-soluble vitamins and hormone precursors are needed consistently, not in bursts.

---

## What the SBS Framework Actually Says

From the skill and references:

> "This is a **minimum floor**, not a default target."

The framework emphasizes:

1. **Set fat with a hard floor** (step 4 of the decision framework)
2. **Enforce height-based fat floor immediately** if fat intake is very low in a cut
3. **Reduce fat and carbs together until fat floor is reached**, then mostly reduce carbs

All of these instructions assume **daily compliance**.

---

## When Weekly Averaging Might Seem Appealing (But Isn't Recommended)

You might think weekly averaging helps if:

- **You have a very low-calorie day**: Instead of hitting 50g fat on a 1500 kcal day, you eat 30g and plan to eat 70g the next day.
- **You want flexibility**: Some days you prefer higher carbs, some days higher fat.

**The SBS response**: Adjust your daily targets to be sustainable, not your monitoring window. If your cut is so aggressive that hitting the fat floor daily feels impossible, the cut itself is too aggressive—not the floor.

---

## The Right Approach

**Daily minimum fat intake** is the standard:

1. **Calculate your height-based floor** (use the formula above).
2. **Hit that floor every single day**, regardless of other macros.
3. **Adjust carbs and protein** around the fat floor to fit your calorie target.
4. **If you can't hit the floor daily**, your calorie target is too low—raise it or reduce the deficit.

---

## Summary

The SBS framework treats the fat floor as a **daily physiological requirement**, not a weekly accounting exercise. Hitting it daily ensures consistent hormone production, vitamin absorption, and satiety. Weekly averaging would undermine the floor's protective purpose and create unnecessary tracking complexity.

**Recommendation**: Treat minimum fat as a daily target. If it feels unsustainable, the issue is your overall calorie target, not the frequency of the floor.
