# Safe Weekly Tuna Consumption and Mercury Limits

## Mercury Content in Canned Tuna

Canned tuna contains mercury, but the amount varies by type:

- **Light canned tuna** (skipjack): ~0.12 mcg/g of mercury (lower mercury)
- **Albacore/white canned tuna** (yellowfin): ~0.35 mcg/g of mercury (higher mercury)

Light tuna is generally preferred due to lower mercury levels.

## FDA and EPA Guidelines

The FDA and EPA provide guidance on fish consumption:

- **General population**: Can safely consume 2-3 servings of fish per week (a serving is ~3-4 oz or ~85-113g)
- **For canned tuna specifically**: The FDA suggests limiting consumption to about 6 oz (170g) per week for the general adult population when considering mercury exposure

## Safe Weekly Tuna Intake

**For most adults:**

- **Light canned tuna**: Up to 2-3 cans per week (5-6 oz per can) is generally considered safe
- **Albacore/white canned tuna**: Limit to 1 can per week due to higher mercury content

**For vulnerable populations** (pregnant women, nursing mothers, young children):

- More restrictive limits apply—typically 1-2 servings per week of light tuna, and albacore should be avoided or severely limited

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Vary your fish choices**: Don't rely solely on tuna. Include lower-mercury fish like salmon, sardines, and cod
2. **Choose light tuna**: When eating canned tuna, opt for "light" varieties over "white" or "albacore"
3. **Monitor portion sizes**: A standard can is typically 5 oz; track weekly consumption
4. **Space out consumption**: Spread tuna meals throughout the week rather than eating multiple servings in one day

## Mercury Accumulation

Mercury accumulates in the body over time, so consistent high intake poses greater risk than occasional consumption. The concern is chronic exposure rather than single meals.

## Bottom Line

For most healthy adults, **2-3 cans of light canned tuna per week is safe**. If you prefer albacore/white tuna, limit to **1 can per week**. Varying your protein sources and choosing light tuna varieties minimizes mercury exposure while maintaining the nutritional and economic benefits of canned tuna.
