# L-Citrulline Supplementation: Should You Take It, Dosing, and Timing

## Short Answer

**Should you take it?** L-citrulline is not essential for body composition or strength gains if your diet covers basic micronutrient needs and you're training hard. It may provide modest ergogenic benefits for endurance and pump, but it's optional and lower-priority than nailing calories, protein, and training.

**Dosing:** 6–8 g per day (typical range: 6–10 g).

**Timing:** 30–60 minutes before training for acute performance benefits; can also be taken daily for chronic vascular adaptations.

## Evidence Summary

### What L-Citrulline Does

L-citrulline is a non-essential amino acid that increases nitric oxide (NO) availability in the body. This improves:

- **Endothelial function** (blood vessel relaxation and blood flow)
- **Aerobic performance** (modest VO₂ max and time-to-fatigue improvements)
- **Muscle pump and vascularity** (subjective, but real)
- **Recovery markers** (some evidence for reduced soreness and faster lactate clearance)

It does **not** directly build muscle or burn fat. It's a vascular/performance aid.

### Strength of Evidence

- **Endurance performance**: Moderate evidence for 1–3% improvements in time-to-fatigue and aerobic capacity
- **Strength/hypertrophy**: Weak-to-modest evidence; most benefits are indirect (better pump → better training feel → potentially better adherence)
- **Recovery**: Mixed evidence; some studies show reduced soreness, others show minimal effect
- **Safety**: Very safe at recommended doses; no major adverse effects reported

## Dosing Recommendations

### Standard Dose

**6–8 g per day** is the most commonly studied and effective range.

- **Lower end (6 g)**: Sufficient for most people; good cost-benefit
- **Upper end (8–10 g)**: May provide slightly more benefit, but diminishing returns
- **Above 10 g**: No clear additional benefit; wasteful

### Timing Options

#### Option 1: Pre-Workout (Acute Benefit)

- **30–60 minutes before training**
- Dose: 6–8 g
- Best for: Endurance, pump, and acute performance
- Rationale: Allows time for absorption and peak NO availability during training

#### Option 2: Daily Consistent Intake (Chronic Adaptation)

- **Spread across the day** (e.g., 3–4 g with breakfast, 3–4 g with lunch)
- Dose: 6–8 g total daily
- Best for: Sustained vascular adaptations and recovery
- Rationale: Chronic NO elevation may improve baseline endurance and recovery

#### Option 3: Combination (Optimal)

- **Daily baseline**: 3–4 g with meals
- **Pre-workout boost**: Additional 3–4 g 30–60 min before training
- Total: 6–8 g daily, with emphasis around training
- Best for: Maximum acute + chronic benefits

### Form Considerations

- **L-citrulline malate** (most common): 6–8 g of the compound; contains ~2 g citrulline + ~4 g malate
  - Malate may add modest fatigue-reduction benefits
- **Pure L-citrulline**: 6–8 g of citrulline directly
- **Citrulline malate 2:1 ratio**: Common supplement form; 8 g provides ~5.3 g citrulline + ~2.7 g malate

**Practical choice**: Citrulline malate 2:1 at 8 g per serving is standard and effective.

## Practical Implementation

### If You Decide to Use It

1. **Buy citrulline malate 2:1** (most cost-effective and well-studied form)
2. **Start with 6 g per day** (one standard scoop)
3. **Take 30–60 minutes before training** for acute benefits, or split across the day for chronic adaptation
4. **Expect results in 1–2 weeks** (some people notice pump immediately; vascular adaptations take longer)
5. **Track subjective markers**: pump, endurance feel, soreness, recovery speed
6. **Reassess after 4 weeks**: If you notice better training feel or performance, keep it; if not, it's not worth the cost for you

### Cost-Benefit Reality

- **Cost**: ~$10–20/month for quality citrulline malate
- **Benefit**: 1–3% endurance improvement, better pump, possibly faster recovery
- **Comparison**: Much smaller ROI than fixing diet, sleep, or training consistency
