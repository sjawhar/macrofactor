# On Less Evidence-Based Approaches for New Lifters

## The Question

Is there a case for new lifters to start with less evidence-based methods (bro splits, dreamer bulks, ego lifting) because they might build useful physical or psychological adaptations?

## Short Answer

No. The case is weak, and the downsides outweigh any speculative benefits.

## The Reasoning

### Why This Argument Sounds Appealing

There's an intuitive appeal to the idea that "struggling with suboptimal methods builds character" or "inefficiency teaches you something." It mirrors how some people argue that handwriting is better than typing because it's harder. But this confuses difficulty with value.

### The Physical Adaptation Problem

New lifters have the highest capacity for progress on _any_ reasonable program. This is the "newbie gains" window—a 6-12 month period where they'll make rapid strength and muscle gains regardless of program quality, as long as they:

- Lift consistently
- Eat enough protein
- Progress gradually

Starting with a suboptimal program doesn't teach the body "useful adaptations" that evidence-based training wouldn't. It just means:

- Slower progress on the same timeline
- Higher injury risk (bro splits often neglect weak points; ego lifting ignores form)
- Wasted potential during the most responsive period

You don't get "bonus adaptations" from inefficiency. You get the same adaptations, slower.

### The Psychological Adaptation Problem

The argument that ego lifting or dreamer bulks build "useful psychological adaptations" is speculative and backwards:

**Ego lifting** (lifting heavy with poor form to inflate numbers) teaches:

- That numbers matter more than movement quality
- That injury risk is acceptable for ego
- Poor movement patterns that become harder to unlearn

**Dreamer bulks** (eating in massive surplus) teach:

- That discipline doesn't matter
- That you can't trust your own judgment about nutrition
- That gaining 50 lbs of fat to gain 10 lbs of muscle is acceptable

These aren't useful psychological adaptations. They're habits that will need to be unlearned.

The _useful_ psychological adaptations come from:

- Consistent effort over time (works on any program)
- Seeing progress (happens faster on better programs)
- Learning to trust a system (happens when the system works)
- Discipline with nutrition and training (better learned with evidence-based approaches)

### The Sunk Cost Problem

There's a hidden cost: if a new lifter spends 6 months on a bro split and gains 15 lbs of muscle, then switches to an evidence-based program and gains 20 lbs in the next 6 months, they've lost 5 lbs of potential progress. That's not recovered. The "learning experience" cost them real gains.

### When Suboptimal Methods Might Make Sense

There are narrow cases where less-than-optimal approaches are acceptable:

1. **Adherence matters more than optimization** — If a new lifter will only stick with a bro split because they find it fun, and they'd quit an optimal program, the bro split wins. But this is about adherence, not about the method being good.

2. **Local context** — If a new lifter has access to a gym buddy who only does bro splits, training together might be worth the suboptimality. Again, this is about adherence and consistency, not the method itself.

3. **Ego lifting as a phase** — Some ego lifting is probably inevitable and harmless in small doses. A new lifter will occasionally load up a bar to see what they can do. This is different from _starting_ with ego lifting as a philosophy.

None of these cases argue for _intentionally_ starting with suboptimal methods. They argue for accepting suboptimality when it's the cost of consistency.

### The Evidence-Based Alternative

Evidence-based beginner programs (Starting Strength, StrongLifts, 5/3/1 for Beginners, Novice Linear Progression) are:

- Simple (easier to learn than bro splits)
- Forgiving (they work even if executed imperfectly)
- Progressive (they scale with the lifter's growing strength)
- Injury-resistant (they emphasize form and gradual progression)

They don't require perfect execution. They work _because_ they're robust to beginner mistakes.

## Conclusion

The case for intentionally starting new lifters on less evidence-based methods is not compelling. The speculative psychological benefits don't materialize, the physical adaptations aren't unique to suboptimal training, and the cost is real progress lost during the most responsive period.

New lifters should start with evidence-based methods. If adherence is the limiting factor, the solution is to find an evidence-based method they'll actually do—not to switch to a method that's both suboptimal _and_ less likely to be sustained.
