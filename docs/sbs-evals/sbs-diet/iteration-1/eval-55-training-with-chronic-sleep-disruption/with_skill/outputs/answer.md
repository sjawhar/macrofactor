# Sleep Disruption, Body Composition, Recovery, and Training: SBS Evidence-Based Guidance

## Executive Summary

Chronic sleep disruption impairs recovery, increases injury risk, and reduces training performance—but the SBS framework addresses this through **calorie/macro optimization, training intensity management, and recovery prioritization**, not through diet alone. Sleep quality is foundational; diet supports but cannot replace it.

---

## Body Composition with Sporadic Sleep

### The Core Problem

Sleep disruption increases:

- **Cortisol and inflammatory markers** (impairs recovery)
- **Hunger hormones** (ghrelin ↑, leptin ↓) → harder to maintain calorie targets
- **Insulin resistance** → worse nutrient partitioning
- **Metabolic rate variability** → TDEE becomes less predictable

### SBS Approach: Calorie and Macro Stability

**1. Set conservative calorie targets**

- If cutting: use **moderate deficit** (13 kcal/lb / 28.6 kcal/kg) rather than aggressive (11 kcal/lb / 24.2 kcal/kg)
- If gaining: use **conservative surplus** (+5-10% above TDEE, or +125-250 kcal/day) rather than aggressive
- **Rationale**: Sleep disruption increases adherence difficulty and metabolic unpredictability; narrower margins reduce risk of overshooting/undershooting

**2. Prioritize protein aggressively**

- **Lifters**: 1.8-2.2 g/kg/day (bias toward high end)
- **Non-lifters**: 1.4-1.6 g/kg/day
- **Why**: Protein preserves lean mass during sleep-impaired recovery, increases satiety (helps manage hunger hormone dysregulation), and supports immune function

**3. Enforce fat floor strictly**

- Height-based minimum: `((height_cm - 150) × 0.5) + 30` grams/day
- **Why**: Sleep disruption impairs hormonal function; adequate fat supports testosterone, cortisol regulation, and nutrient absorption

**4. Carbs: match training demands, not preference**

- If training intensity is high: **3-4 g/kg/day minimum** (sleep-deprived athletes need glycogen support)
- If training is moderate/low: **40-60% of calories** is acceptable
- **Why**: Sleep disruption reduces glycogen resynthesis; carbs become more critical for performance preservation

### Monitoring Adjustments

- **Weigh daily** (morning, post-restroom, same scale) — sleep disruption causes water/sodium shifts; use **1-2 week trend windows**, not daily noise
- **Track intake consistently** — hunger dysregulation makes logging harder; use pre-portioned meals or apps to reduce decision fatigue
- **Adjust slowly**: if rate misses target after 1-2 weeks, change calories by **±250 kcal** (not ±500), then reassess

## Recovery with Sporadic Sleep

### What Diet Cannot Fix

Sleep is **non-negotiable** for:

- Muscle protein synthesis (peaks during deep sleep)
- Glycogen resynthesis
- Immune function
- Hormonal balance (testosterone, cortisol, growth hormone)

**No macro adjustment compensates for chronic sleep loss.** If sleep is severely disrupted (e.g., <5 hours/night consistently), training volume and intensity must be reduced regardless of diet.

### What Diet Can Optimize

**1. Protein distribution**

- Aim for **3-4 substantial protein feedings/day** (≥0.3 g/kg per feeding, ~2-3 g leucine each)
- **Why**: Distributed protein maximizes muscle protein synthesis windows; sleep disruption reduces the efficiency of each window, so frequency matters more
- Example: 80 kg person → 24 g protein per feeding × 4 meals = 96 g/day distributed

**2. Carbohydrate timing around training**

- Consume **1-2 g/kg carbs in the 2-4 hours before training** (if possible)
- Consume **0.8-1.2 g/kg carbs + 0.2-0.4 g/kg protein within 2 hours post-training**
- **Why**: Sleep disruption impairs glycogen resynthesis; pre-loading and post-training refueling become more critical

**3. Micronutrient and hydration vigilance**

- **Magnesium**: 300-400 mg/day (supports sleep quality, muscle recovery)
- **Zinc**: RDA (8 mg women, 11 mg men) — sleep disruption increases immune demand
- **Iron**: RDA (18 mg women, 8 mg men) — sleep disruption impairs oxygen utilization
- **Hydration**: 3-4 L/day baseline; increase if training is high-intensity (sleep disruption impairs thermoregulation)
- **Vitamin D**: if deficient, supplement to 1000-2000 IU/day (sleep disruption worsens vitamin D-dependent immune function)

**4. Avoid aggressive deficits**

- Large calorie deficits (>750 kcal/day) + sleep disruption = compounded recovery failure
- Stick to **moderate deficits** (500-750 kcal/day) if fat loss is the goal

## Naps: Useful or Counterproductive?

### SBS Position: Context-Dependent

**Naps are useful if:**

- They are **20-30 minutes** (prevents sleep inertia; boosts alertness without disrupting nighttime sleep)
- They occur **early afternoon** (1-3 PM), not late afternoon
- They do **not replace nighttime sleep** (naps supplement, not substitute)
- You have **consistent nap timing** (irregular napping worsens sleep architecture)

**Naps are counterproductive if:**

- They are **>60 minutes** (enters deep sleep → grogginess, disrupts nighttime sleep)
- They occur **late afternoon/evening** (delays nighttime sleep onset)
- They are **irregular** (confuses circadian rhythm further)
- You are already getting **6+ hours nighttime sleep** (additional naps may indicate overtraining, not sleep need)

### Practical Recommendation

If sleep is sporadic (e.g., 5-6 hours/night):

- **One 20-30 min nap in early afternoon** can improve afternoon performance and recovery
- **Do not use naps to "catch up"** on sleep debt; they don't restore deep sleep or hormonal cycles
- **Prioritize fixing nighttime sleep** (consistency, duration, darkness, temperature) over napping

## Training While Sleep-Deprived: When and How

### The Hard Truth

**Never train at high intensity on chronic severe sleep loss** (<5 hours/night consistently). Risk of injury, overtraining, and illness outweighs any training stimulus.

### Tiered Guidance by Sleep Duration

#### **6-7 hours/night (mild disruption)**

- **Training intensity**: Full intensity is acceptable
- **Volume**: May need slight reduction (10-15%) if recovery metrics decline
- **Frequency**: Maintain normal frequency
- **Monitoring**: Watch for form breakdown, unusual soreness, or mood changes; reduce if present

#### **5-6 hours/night (moderate disruption)**

- **Training intensity**: Reduce to **80-85% of normal intensity** (RPE 7-8 instead of 9-10)
- **Volume**: Reduce by **20-30%** (fewer sets/reps per session)
- **Frequency**: Maintain frequency but shorten sessions
- **Focus**: Prioritize compound movements; skip accessory work
- **Example**: 3×5 squats + 3×5 bench instead of 4×6 squats + 4×6 bench + 3×8 accessories

#### **<5 hours/night (severe disruption)**

- **Training intensity**: Reduce to **60-70% of normal** (RPE 5-6, submaximal)
- **Volume**: Reduce by **40-50%**
- **Frequency**: Consider **2-3 sessions/week** instead of 4-5
- **Focus**: Maintenance only; do not pursue strength/hypertrophy gains
- **Alternative**: Prioritize **low-intensity steady-state activity** (walking, light cycling) for recovery and mood

### Practical Adjustments

**1. Reduce volume before intensity**

- Sleep-deprived athletes lose volume tolerance faster than intensity tolerance
- Keep intensity moderate; cut sets/reps

**2. Avoid high-frequency, high-volume splits**

- Upper/lower 4x/week or PPL 6x/week + poor sleep = injury risk
- Switch to **full-body 3x/week** or **upper/lower 2x/week** temporarily

**3. Monitor form and RPE**

- If form breaks down mid-set, stop (don't push to failure)
- If RPE feels >8 on a "moderate" session, you're overreaching

**4. Prioritize recovery days**

- Add **1-2 extra rest days** or **active recovery days** (walking, stretching, light yoga)
- Sleep disruption + high training frequency = compounded fatigue

### Nutrition to Support Reduced Training

If training volume is reduced due to sleep disruption:

- **Calorie target**: Reduce by **10-15%** (less training = lower TDEE)
- **Protein**: Keep at **1.8-2.2 g/kg/day** (preserve lean mass despite lower volume)
- **Carbs**: Reduce proportionally with training volume (e.g., if volume drops 30%, reduce carbs ~20-30%)
- **Fat**: Maintain floor; do not cut further

## Practical Implementation Checklist

### Immediate Actions (This Week)

- [ ] **Set conservative calorie target** (moderate deficit/surplus, not aggressive)
- [ ] **Increase protein to 1.8-2.2 g/kg/day** (distribute across 3-4 meals)
- [ ] **Enforce fat floor** (calculate height-based minimum)
- [ ] **Reduce training volume by 20-30%** (keep intensity moderate)
- [ ] **Add one 20-30 min nap** in early afternoon (if sleep is 5-6 hours/night)
- [ ] **Start daily weigh-ins** (morning, post-restroom, same scale)

### Ongoing Monitoring (Weekly)

- [ ] **Track intake daily** (use app to reduce decision fatigue)
- [ ] **Assess weight trend** (1-2 week windows, not daily)
- [ ] **Monitor training form and RPE** (reduce volume if form breaks down)
- [ ] **Check recovery markers**: soreness, mood, sleep quality, appetite
- [ ] **Adjust calories only if trend misses target** after 1-2 weeks (±250 kcal, not ±500)

### Sleep Prioritization (Non-Negotiable)

- [ ] **Consistent bedtime/wake time** (even on weekends)
- [ ] **Dark, cool bedroom** (65-68°F / 18-20°C)
- [ ] **No screens 30-60 min before bed**
- [ ] **Limit caffeine after 2 PM**
- [ ] **If sleep remains <6 hours/night after 2-3 weeks, consult a sleep specialist** (diet cannot fix severe sleep disorders)

## Summary: The SBS Framework for Sleep-Disrupted Training

| Factor                 | Recommendation                          | Rationale                                                        |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Calorie target**     | Moderate (not aggressive)               | Sleep disruption increases adherence difficulty                  |
| **Protein**            | 1.8-2.2 g/kg/day                        | Preserves lean mass, supports immune function, increases satiety |
| **Fat**                | Enforce height-based floor              | Supports hormonal function (testosterone, cortisol)              |
| **Carbs**              | 3-4 g/kg/day if training high-intensity | Sleep disruption impairs glycogen resynthesis                    |
| **Meal frequency**     | 3-4 protein feedings/day                | Distributed protein maximizes synthesis windows                  |
| **Training intensity** | Reduce 15-40% depending on sleep hours  | Sleep-deprived athletes lose volume tolerance first              |
| **Training volume**    | Reduce 20-50% depending on sleep hours  | Preserve form and injury prevention                              |
| **Naps**               | 20-30 min, early afternoon only         | Supplements nighttime sleep; longer naps disrupt it              |
| **Monitoring**         | Daily intake + weekly trend weigh-ins   | Sleep disruption causes water/sodium shifts; use trends          |
| **Sleep priority**     | Fix nighttime sleep first               | Diet cannot compensate for chronic sleep loss                    |

**Bottom line**: Sleep disruption is a recovery problem, not a diet problem. Optimize calories, macros, and training volume to match reduced recovery capacity. If sleep remains severely disrupted after 2-3 weeks of consistent effort, seek professional help—no diet adjustment will overcome a sleep disorder.
