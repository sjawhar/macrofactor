# Dream Research Study: The Protein Distribution & Lean Mass Retention Mega-Trial

## The Study Design

If ethics and money were no concern, I would run a **multi-year, multi-site randomized controlled trial examining protein distribution patterns during aggressive fat loss in lean, trained individuals**—specifically testing whether the timing, frequency, and dose of protein feedings meaningfully affect lean mass retention when total daily protein, calories, and training are held constant.

## Why This Study Matters (SBS Framework)

The SBS Diet skill establishes clear protein targets:

- **Lifters in deficit: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day** (or 2.0–2.75 g/kg FFM for very lean individuals)
- **Distribution guidance: 3–6 protein feedings/day, ≥0.3 g/kg per feeding, ~2–3 g leucine per dose**

However, the skill explicitly notes that **distribution and quality are secondary to hitting total daily targets**. The evidence base is thin on whether the _pattern_ of protein delivery—beyond total intake—actually drives lean mass outcomes in trained populations during aggressive deficits.

## Study Parameters

### Population

- **N = 600–800 lean, trained lifters** (body fat 8–15%, minimum 2+ years resistance training)
- Stratified by sex, age (20–50), and baseline strength level
- Randomized into 6 arms

### Intervention Arms (12–16 week aggressive cut)

1. **Even distribution**: 6 equal protein feedings (~0.3–0.4 g/kg each), spread 2–3 hours apart
2. **Clustered morning**: 50% of daily protein in first 3 meals (breakfast, mid-morning, lunch); remaining 50% in 2 evening feedings
3. **Clustered post-workout**: 40% of daily protein in 2 meals surrounding training; remaining 60% distributed across 3 other meals
4. **Minimal frequency**: 3 large protein feedings (~0.7–0.8 g/kg each), spaced 4–5 hours apart
5. **Time-restricted feeding (8-hour window)**: All protein compressed into 8-hour eating window, 3–4 feedings
6. **Control (current SBS default)**: 4 feedings, ~0.35 g/kg each, distributed across waking hours

### Held Constant Across All Arms

- **Total daily protein**: 2.0 g/kg body mass (high end of SBS range for lean lifters in deficit)
- **Total daily calories**: 13 kcal/lb (moderate cut, ~0.5–0.75% bodyweight/week loss)
- **Fat intake**: Height-based floor + 0.2 g/kg above floor
- **Carbohydrate**: Remaining calories, ≥3 g/kg/day to support training
- **Training program**: Identical periodized resistance program (3–4 days/week, moderate-to-high volume)
- **Tracking precision**: Daily food logging, weekly weigh-ins, standardized morning protocol

### Primary Outcomes

1. **Lean mass retention** (DEXA scans at baseline, 8 weeks, 16 weeks)
2. **Strength preservation** (1RM bench, squat, deadlift at baseline, 8 weeks, 16 weeks)
3. **Muscle thickness** (ultrasound of quadriceps, triceps at baseline, 8 weeks, 16 weeks)
4. **Adherence and compliance** (daily food logs, protein target hit rate)

### Secondary Outcomes

- Muscle protein synthesis markers (blood biomarkers: myostatin, IGF-1, amino acid profiles)
- Appetite and satiety ratings (daily VAS scales)
- Training performance (volume load, RPE, recovery metrics)
- Metabolic rate (indirect calorimetry at baseline, 8 weeks, 16 weeks)
- Body composition by arm-fat-free mass, leg-fat-free mass (DEXA regional analysis)

## Why This Study Fills a Critical Gap

### Current SBS Position

The skill states: **"Aim for 3–6 protein feedings/day, ≥0.3 g/kg protein, and ~2–3 g leucine per dose."** But it also notes that **total daily protein is the primary driver**, and distribution is secondary to hitting that target.

### The Unanswered Question

- Does a lean, trained lifter in an aggressive deficit _actually_ retain more lean mass with 6 evenly spaced feedings vs. 3 large feedings, if total protein is identical?
- Does post-workout timing matter more than other times?
- Does time-restricted feeding (which compresses feedings) compromise lean mass retention in deficit?
- Are there sex differences in protein distribution sensitivity?

### Why It Matters for SBS Coaching

If the study finds **no meaningful difference** in lean mass retention across distribution patterns (holding total protein constant), it would validate the SBS framework's emphasis on total intake over timing minutiae—and simplify coaching for adherence-limited clients.

If the study finds **significant differences**, it would refine the SBS distribution guidance with evidence-based thresholds (e.g., "minimum 4 feedings for lean lifters in aggressive deficits" or "post-workout timing adds 2–3% lean mass retention").

## Study Logistics (Unlimited Budget)

### Sites

- 4–6 university research centers with DEXA, ultrasound, indirect calorimetry, and blood draw capacity
- Recruitment from local strength communities, online fitness forums, university athletic departments

### Duration

- 16-week intervention + 4-week baseline stabilization + 8-week follow-up = **7 months per cohort**
- 3–4 cohorts staggered = **18–24 months total data collection**

### Staffing

- Nutritionists at each site for weekly check-ins and adherence support
- Strength coaches to supervise training and ensure program fidelity
- Phlebotomists for blood draws
- DEXA/ultrasound technicians
- Data analysts and biostatisticians

### Compliance Mechanisms

- Daily food logging via app with real-time feedback
- Weekly weigh-ins and trend monitoring
- Bi-weekly video check-ins with nutritionists
- Incentives for adherence (gift cards, free supplements, training gear)
- Dropout replacement protocol to maintain N

## Expected Outcomes & Impact

### Scenario A: No Significant Difference

**Conclusion**: Total daily protein is the dominant factor. Distribution patterns matter minimally for lean mass retention in trained lifters.

**SBS Impact**: Strengthens the current framework. Coaches can confidently tell clients: _"Hit 2.0 g/kg daily protein in whatever pattern works for your schedule. 3–6 feedings is a guideline, not a requirement."_

### Scenario B: Significant Differences

**Conclusion**: Specific distribution patterns (e.g., 4+ feedings, post-workout emphasis, or minimum leucine per dose) improve lean mass retention by 5–15%.

**SBS Impact**: Refines the distribution guidance with evidence-based minimums. Coaches can prioritize adherence-friendly patterns that still meet lean mass retention thresholds.

### Scenario C: Sex/Training-Status Interactions

**Conclusion**: Distribution sensitivity varies by sex, age, or training experience.

**SBS Impact**: Enables personalized distribution recommendations. E.g., "Newer lifters can use 3 feedings; advanced lean lifters benefit from 4–5."

## Why This Study Is Uniquely Valuable

1. **Fills a real gap**: Current evidence on protein distribution is mostly in older adults, sedentary populations, or non-lean individuals. Lean, trained lifters are underrepresented.

2. **Directly tests SBS assumptions**: The skill's distribution guidance is evidence-informed but not definitively proven in this population.

3. **High practical impact**: If distribution doesn't matter, it simplifies coaching and removes a source of perfectionism. If it does, it refines best practices.

4. **Mechanistic depth**: Combining DEXA, ultrasound, strength testing, and biomarkers allows investigation of _how_ distribution affects outcomes (muscle protein synthesis, metabolic adaptation, etc.).

5. **Addresses adherence reality**: Many lifters struggle with 5–6 feedings due to work/life constraints. Evidence on minimum effective frequency would be immediately actionable.

## Alignment with SBS Philosophy

This study embodies the SBS approach:

- **Numbers-first**: Precise measurement of lean mass, strength, and protein intake
- **Evidence-driven**: Tests assumptions rather than accepting them
- **Practical**: Results directly inform coaching decisions
- **Iterative**: Findings would refine the framework, not replace it

The SBS skill emphasizes that **total daily protein is the primary lever**, but acknowledges distribution as a secondary consideration. This study would quantify exactly how secondary—and under what conditions distribution becomes primary again.
