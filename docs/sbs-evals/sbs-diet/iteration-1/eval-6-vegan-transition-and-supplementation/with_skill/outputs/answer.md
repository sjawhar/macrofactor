# Eric's Vegan Diet Transition: Training, Performance, Recovery, and Supplementation

## Summary

According to SBS diet principles, switching to a fully vegan diet does not inherently require changes to training, performance expectations, or recovery protocols. However, it does require deliberate adjustments to supplementation and micronutrient strategy to maintain the same outcomes.

---

## Training and Performance

**No necessary changes to training structure or intensity.**

SBS framework treats diet patterns (including vegan) as tools for hitting calorie and macro targets, not as determinants of training capacity. If Eric maintains:

- Total daily calories aligned with his goal
- Protein targets (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day for a lifter)
- Adequate carbohydrate for his training demands (≥3–4 g/kg/day for typical lifting; ≥6 g/kg/day for endurance-heavy work)

...then training performance should remain stable.

**Potential performance risk:** If carbohydrate intake drops during the vegan transition (due to food logistics or incomplete planning), high-intensity training performance may decline. This is not a vegan-specific issue—it's a carbohydrate-availability issue that would affect any diet pattern.

---

## Recovery

**No necessary changes to recovery protocols.**

Recovery depends on:

1. **Protein sufficiency and distribution** (≥0.3 g/kg per feeding, 3–6 feedings/day)
2. **Adequate total calories** (matching energy expenditure)
3. **Sleep, stress management, and training stimulus** (unchanged by diet pattern)

A well-planned vegan diet can support all three. The SBS framework explicitly states: _"Plant-heavy diets can support hypertrophy if protein quantity, EAA complementarity, and dose distribution are handled deliberately."_

**Key adjustment:** Vegan protein sources require more intentional distribution and complementarity. Eric should ensure:

- Multiple substantial protein feedings (3–4 minimum for plant-heavy diets)
- Complete essential amino acid (EAA) coverage across meals
- Adequate leucine per feeding (~2–3 g per dose)

This is a logistics change, not a recovery limitation.

---

## Supplementation Changes

**Vegan diets require deliberate supplementation for micronutrients frequently underrepresented in plant-heavy diets.**

### Critical Supplements for Vegan Athletes

1. **Vitamin B12** (mandatory)
   - Plant foods do not reliably provide bioavailable B12
   - Deficiency impairs energy metabolism and recovery
   - Supplement or use fortified foods consistently

2. **Vitamin D** (likely needed)
   - Plant sources are limited
   - Especially important during low-sun seasons or if training indoors
   - Consider supplementation or regular sun exposure + testing

3. **Iron** (monitor closely)
   - Plant-based iron (non-heme) has lower bioavailability than animal sources
   - High training volume increases iron demands
   - Consider supplementation if blood work shows low ferritin or hemoglobin
   - Pair with vitamin C sources to improve absorption

4. **Zinc** (monitor)
   - Plant sources contain phytates that reduce absorption
   - High training volume increases zinc losses
   - Consider supplementation if dietary intake is consistently low

5. **Iodine** (monitor)
   - Plant foods vary widely in iodine content depending on soil
   - Use iodized salt or consider supplementation

6. **Calcium** (monitor)
   - Adequate plant sources exist (fortified plant milks, leafy greens, tofu)
   - Ensure sufficient intake; supplementation if dietary sources are inadequate

### Protein Supplementation (Optional but Practical)

- Plant-based protein powders (pea, rice, hemp, soy blends) can simplify hitting protein targets
- Not mandatory if whole-food protein sources are adequate, but useful for convenience and dose distribution

## Practical Adjustments Summary

| Factor                        | Change Required? | Rationale                                                          |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Training volume/intensity** | No               | Depends on calories and carbs, not diet pattern                    |
| **Recovery protocols**        | No               | Depends on protein, calories, sleep—all achievable vegan           |
| **Protein target**            | No               | Same 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day; requires deliberate sourcing                |
| **Carbohydrate target**       | No               | Same ≥3–4 g/kg/day; ensure adequate intake during transition       |
| **Micronutrient strategy**    | **Yes**          | B12 (mandatory), D, iron, zinc, iodine (monitor); calcium (ensure) |
| **Meal distribution**         | **Yes**          | Increase to 3–4+ substantial protein feedings for EAA completeness |
| **Supplementation**           | **Yes**          | Add B12; consider D, iron, zinc based on blood work and intake     |

---

## Conclusion

Eric's vegan transition does not require changes to training, performance expectations, or recovery _protocols_. It does require:

1. **Deliberate protein planning** to hit quantity and EAA distribution targets
2. **Micronutrient supplementation** (especially B12, and likely D, iron, zinc)
3. **Consistent carbohydrate intake** to maintain training performance
4. **Monitoring** of blood work (iron, B12, vitamin D) to catch deficiencies early

If these logistics are handled, training and performance outcomes should remain unchanged.
