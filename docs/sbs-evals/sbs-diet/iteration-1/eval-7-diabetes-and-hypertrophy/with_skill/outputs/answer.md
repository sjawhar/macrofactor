# How Insulin Resistance and Type 2 Diabetes Affect Muscle Growth, and Whether Metformin Worsens Hypertrophy

## Summary

The SBS Diet skill and its reference materials do not contain specific quantitative data on how much insulin resistance or type 2 diabetes reduce muscle growth, nor do they address metformin's effects on hypertrophy. The skill is designed for practical nutrition coaching around calories, macros, and adherence—not for managing metabolic disease or medication interactions.

## What the SBS Framework Covers

The SBS Diet skill provides evidence-based guidance on:

- **Calorie targets** for fat loss (0.25–1.0% bodyweight/week) and gain (0.1–0.25% bodyweight/week)
- **Protein requirements** for lifters: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (or 2.0–2.75 g/kg FFM/day if very lean or high body-fat)
- **Fat and carbohydrate distribution** based on training demands and preferences
- **Meal timing and diet patterns** (intermittent fasting, low-carb, vegan, etc.) as adherence tools, not metabolic magic
- **Micronutrient, fiber, and hydration** baselines for health coverage
- **Adjustment protocols** when progress stalls

## What the SBS Framework Does NOT Cover

The skill explicitly does not address:

1. **Insulin resistance or type 2 diabetes pathophysiology** — how these conditions affect muscle protein synthesis, glucose uptake, or anabolic signaling
2. **Quantitative impact on hypertrophy** — no data on reduced muscle growth rates in diabetic or insulin-resistant populations
3. **Medication effects** — metformin, GLP-1 agonists, insulin, or other diabetes medications and their interactions with training or muscle growth
4. **Disease-specific nutrition** — carbohydrate management for blood glucose control, medication timing, or clinical monitoring

## Implication for Your Question

To answer "How much do insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes reduce muscle growth?" and "Does metformin make hypertrophy worse?" requires:

- **Clinical research** on muscle protein synthesis rates in insulin-resistant vs. insulin-sensitive individuals
- **Pharmacology data** on metformin's effects on mTOR signaling, mitochondrial function, or anabolic capacity
- **Metabolic disease expertise** — not general nutrition coaching

The SBS framework is designed for healthy individuals optimizing body composition through calorie and macro management. It does not substitute for medical guidance when metabolic disease is present.

## What You Can Do Within SBS If You Have Diabetes or Insulin Resistance

If you are training for hypertrophy and managing type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance, the SBS framework still applies to the **nutrition mechanics**:

1. **Set your calorie surplus** for gain: 0.1–0.25% bodyweight/week (or 10–20% above TDEE)
2. **Prioritize protein**: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day to support muscle retention and synthesis
3. **Distribute carbs** based on training demands (at least 3–4 g/kg/day for typical lifting) and blood glucose management goals
4. **Monitor adherence** via daily intake logging and weekly weight trends
5. **Adjust if progress stalls** by modifying calories, not by changing the fundamental approach

However, **blood glucose control, medication timing, and metabolic safety** require consultation with your physician or a registered dietitian familiar with both diabetes management and sports nutrition.

## Conclusion

The SBS Diet skill provides a robust framework for setting calories, macros, and adherence strategies—but it is not designed to quantify disease-specific impacts on muscle growth or medication side effects. For your specific question, consult clinical research, your healthcare provider, or a sports dietitian with diabetes expertise.
