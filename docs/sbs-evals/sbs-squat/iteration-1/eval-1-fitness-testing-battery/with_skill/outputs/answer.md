# The SBS Combine: A 12-Week Fitness Testing Battery

Great question — and a fun one to think through. The goal of any good testing battery is to give you _actionable signal_, not just a number to feel good or bad about. So before listing tests, the design principle matters: each test should be repeatable under standardized conditions, sensitive enough to detect real change over 12 weeks, and cover a distinct capacity so you're not just measuring the same thing five different ways. With that framing, here's how I'd build the SBS Combine.

## Strength: The Non-Negotiables

For a generally strong and athletic person, the strength tier should anchor the whole battery. The most informative tests are estimated 1-rep maxes on the big compound movements — squat, deadlift, and either bench press or overhead press depending on your goals. Rather than actually maxing out every 12 weeks (which carries injury risk and requires significant recovery), use a well-validated rep-max protocol: a heavy set of 3–5 reps performed with maximal intent, then plug those numbers into a standard e1RM formula. The key is consistency — same variation, same equipment, same warm-up sequence every time. For the squat specifically, this means locking in your bar position (high bar, low bar, or front squat), your stance, and your depth standard before you ever start tracking. Changing any of those variables between tests makes the comparison meaningless. A useful secondary strength marker is a relative strength ratio: squat-to-bodyweight and deadlift-to-bodyweight give you context that raw kilograms don't. If your squat e1RM goes from 140 kg to 145 kg but you also gained 5 kg of bodyweight, your relative strength didn't move — that's important information.

One additional strength test worth including: a unilateral assessment like a Bulgarian split squat or single-leg press at a fixed load for max reps, or a fixed rep count for load. Bilateral compound lifts can mask asymmetries that accumulate over time, and catching a left-right gap early is far more useful than discovering it after an injury.

## Power and Athleticism

Power is distinct from strength — it's force expressed quickly — and it deserves its own slot in the battery. The countermovement vertical jump (CMJ) is the gold standard here: it's highly reliable, requires no equipment beyond a measuring tape or a jump mat, and is sensitive to both neuromuscular fatigue and genuine power development. Test it fresh, after a thorough warm-up but before any heavy lifting, and take the best of three attempts. A standing broad jump is a solid horizontal-power complement if you want a second data point. Both tests are low-fatigue, low-injury-risk, and respond meaningfully to training over a 12-week block. If you have access to a force plate, peak power output is even more precise, but the jump tests are valid and practical for most people.

Sprint testing — a 10-meter or 40-yard dash — is another excellent power marker if you have the space and surface. It captures acceleration capacity and fast-twitch recruitment in a way that jump tests don't fully replicate. The caveat is that sprint times require very consistent conditions (surface, footwear, timing method) to be meaningful across testing windows.

## Aerobic Capacity

For aerobic fitness, the most practical field test is a 12-minute run (the Cooper Test) or a 1.5-mile time trial. Both have decades of validation data and correlate well with VO2max. The Cooper Test is slightly more forgiving because you're maximizing distance rather than racing a fixed course, which makes pacing errors less catastrophic. If you prefer a non-running option — say, due to lower-body fatigue from the strength tests — a 2,000-meter rowing ergometer time trial is an excellent substitute with its own robust normative data. Whichever you choose, pick one and stick with it across all testing windows. Aerobic capacity is one of the slower-moving capacities, so don't be discouraged if 12 weeks shows modest improvement; the trend over multiple cycles is what matters.

## Anaerobic Capacity and Work Capacity

This is the capacity most people neglect in testing batteries, and it's arguably the most relevant for athletic performance. A 30-second Wingate-style test on a bike is the lab standard, but a practical field substitute is a 500-meter rowing sprint for time, or a max-rep assault bike effort for 60 seconds (calories or RPM). These tests are brutal and require genuine maximal effort, so they should come last in any testing session. A simpler option that requires no equipment: a max-rep set of a bodyweight movement like pull-ups or push-ups at a controlled tempo. This blends local muscular endurance with anaerobic capacity and is easy to standardize. The key is that the test must be genuinely maximal — pacing yourself defeats the purpose.

## Mobility and Movement Quality

The SBS approach to mobility is pragmatic: distinguish between true range-of-motion limitations and loaded control deficits, because the fix is different for each. For a 12-week battery, two tests cover the most ground. First, a dorsiflexion wall test (knee-to-wall distance in centimeters) captures ankle ROM that directly affects squat depth and athletic movement patterns. Second, a hip flexion assessment — either a passive straight-leg raise or a seated hip flexion test — flags restrictions that show up as butt wink in the squat or hamstring tightness in athletic movements. These aren't pass/fail tests; they're baselines. If your wall test distance improves from 8 cm to 11 cm over 12 weeks, that's meaningful progress even if you never had a "problem." A shoulder external rotation assessment (arm at 90° abduction, measuring passive rotation) rounds out the picture for anyone doing overhead work.

## Putting It Together: Testing Day Protocol

The order matters. Run your tests in this sequence to minimize fatigue contamination: (1) mobility assessments first — they require no energy and set a baseline for the session; (2) power tests (CMJ, broad jump, sprint) while the nervous system is fresh; (3) strength tests (e1RM protocols); (4) aerobic test on a separate day or after at least 30 minutes of recovery; (5) anaerobic test last, on the same day as aerobic or on its own day. The whole battery doesn't need to happen in a single session — spreading it across two or three days within the same week is fine and will give you cleaner data. The 12-week cadence is well-chosen: it's long enough for genuine adaptation to show up, short enough to catch problems before they compound, and aligns naturally with most training block structures.

The most important thing is standardization over sophistication. A simple test done identically every 12 weeks beats a complex test done slightly differently each time. Pick your battery, write down every protocol detail, and treat the testing conditions as seriously as you treat the training itself.
