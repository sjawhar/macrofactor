# SBS Combine: A 12-Week Fitness Testing Battery

Great question — and a fun one. If you want a single battery that captures whether you're genuinely strong, fit, and athletic (not just good at one thing), you need tests that span the major physical qualities: maximal strength, strength-endurance, power, aerobic capacity, and body composition. Here's what I'd put in the SBS Combine, designed to be repeatable every 12 weeks with minimal equipment and low injury risk.

## The Battery

**Maximal Strength (1–3 Rep Max)**
Pick two or three compound lifts that you actually train. The squat, deadlift, and bench press are the obvious choices — they're well-validated, easy to track over time, and cover the major movement patterns. If you don't barbell train, a weighted pull-up max or trap bar deadlift works fine. Test a true 1RM or a calculated 3RM (using a reliable e1RM formula) every 12 weeks. This is your primary strength signal.

**Strength-Endurance**
A bodyweight or lightly loaded rep-out test captures a different quality than a 1RM. Classic options: max pull-ups (dead hang, full ROM), max push-ups, or a 20-rep squat test at a fixed percentage of bodyweight. These are sensitive to changes in relative strength and muscular endurance — qualities that often diverge from absolute strength, especially during a cut or a high-volume training block.

**Power**
A vertical jump (countermovement jump) or a broad jump is the gold standard here — fast, reliable, and highly sensitive to neuromuscular fatigue and training adaptations. No equipment needed beyond a wall or tape measure. Power is often the first quality to drop when you're overtrained and the first to rebound when you're fresh, making it a useful canary in the coal mine between testing windows.

**Aerobic Capacity**
A 1-mile run, a 12-minute Cooper run, or a 2km row are all solid choices. The Cooper test (how far you can run in 12 minutes) has decades of normative data and correlates well with VO2max. If you prefer the rower, a 2km time trial is brutally honest and highly reproducible. Pick one and stick with it — the value is in the trend, not the absolute number.

**Body Composition Proxy**
You don't need a DEXA scan every 12 weeks. Bodyweight + waist circumference (at the navel) is a practical, low-noise proxy for fat mass changes. Taken first thing in the morning, fasted, these two numbers together tell you a lot about whether your composition is moving in the right direction alongside your performance metrics.

## Practical Notes

Run the battery over 2–3 days, not all in one session. Strength tests first (when you're fresh), power tests second, aerobic test last. Standardize conditions as much as possible — same time of day, same warm-up, same footwear. The 12-week cadence is ideal: long enough for meaningful adaptation to accumulate, short enough to catch problems before they compound. Track everything in a simple spreadsheet and look at trends across multiple testing windows, not just the last two data points.
