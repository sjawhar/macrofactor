# Post-Meet Transition: Jumping Back In vs. Taking Time Off

Honestly, the answer is "it depends" — but not in a hand-wavy way. There are a few concrete factors that should drive the decision, and I'll walk through how I think about it.

The first thing to recognize is that a meet is not just a hard training session. It's a maximal-effort event preceded by a peaking phase, often a weight cut, travel, and a long competition day with significant psychological stress on top of the physical load. Your nervous system, connective tissue, and motivation reserves are all drawn down in ways that don't show up cleanly on a soreness scale. The squat in particular — especially a heavy low-bar attempt — places enormous demand on the posterior chain, hips, and bracing system. Even if you feel okay two days later, that doesn't mean you're recovered in any meaningful sense. So the default position should be: take at least a short transition period before resuming structured training, not because you're fragile, but because it's the highest-return use of that window.

What that transition period looks like depends on how the meet went and how you feel. If you hit all your attempts, felt strong, and the meet went smoothly, a week of very light movement — think submaximal technique work, maybe 50–60% of your normal training max, low volume, no grinding — is usually enough before easing back into a base-building or hypertrophy block. If you missed attempts, had a rough day, or pushed through something that didn't feel right, I'd extend that window and use it deliberately: get some easy movement in, address any technique issues that showed up under competition conditions, and let the motivation rebuild naturally. Forcing intensity when you're mentally flat is one of the fastest ways to accumulate bad reps and reinforce poor movement patterns. The squat is a skill, and skill acquisition suffers when you're fatigued or checked out.

On the programming side, the transition week is a good time to shift variation. If you've been grinding low bar for a full peaking cycle, throwing in some high bar or front squat work during the transition serves double duty: it keeps you moving, exposes any weak links that got masked by competition-specific technique, and gives your joints a break from the exact loading pattern they've been under. Front squats in particular are useful here — they demand more from the upper back and quads in a way that often reveals what needs work heading into the next block. Keep the loads honest (nothing near max), prioritize position quality, and treat it as diagnostic as much as training.

The one thing I'd push back on is the idea that taking a week easy is "wasted time." The programming principle that matters here is that primary squat frequency and skill exposure need to stay consistent over the long run — but that doesn't mean every single week needs to be a hard training week. A deliberate low-intensity transition week is part of the plan, not a deviation from it. The lifters who make the most progress over years are the ones who manage fatigue intelligently across the full training cycle, not just within individual blocks. Coming back into your next training phase fresh, motivated, and with a clear read on what needs to improve is worth far more than an extra week of grinding when you're already depleted.
