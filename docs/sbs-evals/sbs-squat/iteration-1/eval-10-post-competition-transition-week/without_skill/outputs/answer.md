# Post-Meet Recovery: What Do I Do Right After a Competition?

Honestly, the answer depends heavily on how the meet went and how your body is feeling—but I'll share my general approach and what I'd recommend for most lifters. The short version: I don't jump straight back into structured training. After a meet, your body has been through significant physical and psychological stress, and respecting that recovery window is one of the most important things you can do for long-term progress.

In the immediate aftermath—the first week post-meet—I treat it as a true deload. That means no structured programming, no hitting percentages, no chasing numbers. If I feel like moving, I'll do some light activity: a walk, some easy bodyweight work, maybe a casual gym session where I just do whatever feels good with no agenda. The goal is to let the nervous system recover, let any minor aches and inflammation settle down, and mentally decompress from the competitive mindset. Meets are taxing in ways that don't always show up immediately—the adrenaline, the travel, the weight cut if you did one, the disrupted sleep. Give yourself permission to actually rest.

The second week is where I start to assess. How am I feeling? Are there any lingering injuries or soreness that need attention? Did the meet reveal technical weaknesses I want to address? This is a good time for honest reflection rather than rushing back to heavy loading. I might start reintroducing some light training—submaximal work, technique-focused sessions—but I'm not trying to set PRs or push intensity. Think of it as re-establishing movement patterns and getting the body used to training again, not building fitness.

By weeks three and four, most lifters are ready to begin a proper transition block. This is where you start thinking about your next goal: Is it another meet? A longer hypertrophy phase? Addressing a specific weakness? The post-meet period is actually one of the best times to run a higher-volume, lower-intensity block because your body is primed to respond to new stimulus after the heavy peaking work. I'd resist the urge to immediately start another peaking cycle—that's a recipe for burnout and accumulated fatigue over the long haul.

The biggest mistake I see lifters make is treating the meet as just another training day and jumping back into their next block on Monday. Your body doesn't care about your spreadsheet. The lifters who stay healthy and keep progressing year over year are the ones who treat recovery as part of the training process, not an obstacle to it. Take the time, reflect on what the meet taught you, and come back with a clear plan. You'll be better for it.
