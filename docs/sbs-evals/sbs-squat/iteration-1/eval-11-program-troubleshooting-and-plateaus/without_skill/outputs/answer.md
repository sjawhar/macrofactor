# Program Troubleshooting: Tweak vs. Switch

Great question, and the fact that you're asking it suggests you already have the right instinct — if a program "mostly works," that's actually a strong signal to stay and troubleshoot rather than bail. Most lifters underestimate how much mileage is left in a program before it's truly exhausted. Switching programs is often the path of least resistance that feels productive but isn't. The general rule: exhaust your troubleshooting options first, and only switch when you've genuinely run out of levers to pull.

**Start by diagnosing what "mostly works" actually means.** Is progress stalling on one lift but not others? Is fatigue accumulating faster than you're recovering? Are you losing motivation because the program feels boring, or because it's genuinely not producing results? These have different fixes. A single stalling lift usually means that movement needs a targeted tweak — adjusting frequency, adding a variation, or changing rep ranges — not a full program overhaul. Accumulated fatigue that isn't resolving might mean you need a deload week built in, or that your volume is slightly above your current recovery capacity. Boredom is real but it's not a training variable; adding one exercise you enjoy as an accessory is usually enough to fix it without blowing up the structure.

**The order of operations for troubleshooting should go from smallest to largest change.** First, check the basics: sleep, nutrition, and stress. These are almost always the culprit when a program that was working suddenly stops. If those are solid, try a planned deload (reduce volume and intensity for a week) before concluding the program is broken — many plateaus resolve after a reset. If you're still stuck after a deload, look at the specific lift: add a session of that movement, swap a variation (e.g., pause squats if your squat is stalling), or adjust the rep range to accumulate volume differently. Only after you've tried these targeted adjustments — and they haven't moved the needle over 4–6 weeks — should you consider whether the program's overall structure is the problem.

**When is it actually time to switch?** If the program's structure fundamentally doesn't match your current needs — for example, you've outgrown a linear progression model and need periodization, or the program doesn't train the movements you care about — that's a legitimate reason to move on. Similarly, if you've run the program for a full cycle (typically 12–16 weeks for most intermediate programs), completed a deload, and still see no meaningful progress despite dialing in recovery, it's reasonable to try something new. The key is that the decision should be deliberate and evidence-based, not reactive to a bad week or two.

**One practical framework:** keep a training log and look at trends over 3–4 week blocks, not week to week. If the trend line is flat or declining across a full block despite good recovery, that's a real signal. If it's just noisy week-to-week variation around an upward trend, that's normal and not a reason to change anything. Most lifters who feel "stuck" are actually progressing — just slower than they expected. Patience, targeted adjustments, and honest tracking will serve you far better than program-hopping, which resets your adaptation curve every time and makes it nearly impossible to know what's actually working.
