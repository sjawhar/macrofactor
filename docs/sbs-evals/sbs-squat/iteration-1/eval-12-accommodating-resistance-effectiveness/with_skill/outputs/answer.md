# Bands and Chains for Building Absolute Strength

The logic behind accommodating resistance is genuinely compelling, and it's worth understanding why before evaluating whether it delivers on that promise. A standard barbell squat has a force-curve mismatch: the load is constant, but your mechanical advantage changes dramatically through the range of motion. You're weakest near the bottom and strongest near lockout — which means the top portion of the lift is relatively easy and the bar decelerates as you approach completion. Bands and chains are designed to fix this by adding resistance as you rise, so the load increases precisely where you're strongest. In theory, this keeps tension high throughout the full range, trains the lockout more aggressively, and may help you develop speed and power through the sticking point rather than coasting through the top.

The sticking point connection is particularly relevant here. As covered in the SBS material, most squat misses happen roughly one to six inches above parallel — not at the true bottom. The sticking point is where your mechanical position is worst and where quad contribution tends to drop off if you're quad-limited. Accommodating resistance, in principle, could help by forcing you to maintain bar speed through that region rather than letting it slow down. The problem is that bands and chains don't actually load the sticking point more — they load the _top_ more. The sticking point is still the sticking point; you're just adding more work above it. Whether that translates into better sticking-point performance is a separate question from whether the force-curve rationale is sound.

On the evidence: the meta-analytic picture is more modest than the theoretical case would suggest. Studies comparing accommodating resistance to straight-weight training generally show that bands and chains are not clearly superior for building absolute strength in most lifters. Effect sizes tend to be small and inconsistent, and the advantage — when it appears — is often seen in more advanced lifters who may have already optimized the basics. For intermediate and recreational lifters, the research doesn't support treating accommodating resistance as a meaningful upgrade over well-programmed straight-weight training. The tools work; they're just not the force multiplier the powerlifting community sometimes treats them as.

Practically, the SBS framework treats bands and chains as useful tools rather than prerequisites — and that framing is exactly right. If you're stalling on your squat, the higher-leverage interventions are almost always technical and structural: diagnosing whether you're quad-limited, bracing-limited, or hip-limited, then targeting those weak links directly with front squats, leg press, unilateral work, or bracing drills. Accommodating resistance doesn't replace that diagnostic work. Where bands and chains do earn their place is in more advanced programming contexts — adding variety, training bar speed and intent, or providing a novel stimulus when straight-weight training has been run hard for a long time.

**Bottom line:** The force-curve rationale is real and the tools are legitimate. But the evidence for a meaningful strength advantage over straight-weight training is weak, especially for lifters who haven't yet maximized the basics. If you enjoy training with bands or chains and they fit your setup, there's no reason to avoid them. If you're looking for the highest-leverage way to break a squat plateau, the better investment is usually diagnosing your specific weak link — quads, bracing, or hips — and attacking it directly with targeted accessories and technical work.
