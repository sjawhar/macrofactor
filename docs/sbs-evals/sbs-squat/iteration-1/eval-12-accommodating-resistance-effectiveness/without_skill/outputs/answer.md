# Bands and Chains for Building Absolute Strength

Bands and chains — collectively called "accommodating resistance" — are a staple of powerlifting programming, particularly popularized by Westside Barbell. The core logic is elegant: a barbell loaded with only plates provides constant resistance throughout the lift, but your strength curve isn't constant. In the squat and deadlift, you're weakest in the hole and strongest near lockout. Bands and chains exploit this by adding resistance as you move into your stronger range — chains pile up off the floor as you rise, bands stretch and increase tension. The result is that the load better matches your strength curve, keeping the muscle under meaningful tension throughout the full range of motion rather than letting the top portion become a "free ride."

The theoretical rationale goes a step further: because the load is heavier at the top, you're forced to accelerate through the entire lift rather than decelerating once you've cleared the sticking point. This trains rate of force development and bar speed, which are important for maximal strength expression. There's also a neurological argument — training with bands and chains may improve your ability to recruit motor units quickly and sustain that recruitment through the full range, which transfers to heavier straight-weight attempts.

Does the evidence support this? The research is genuinely supportive, though not overwhelmingly so. Multiple studies have found that adding accommodating resistance to traditional barbell training produces greater strength gains than straight weight alone, particularly in trained lifters. The effect seems most pronounced for people who already have a solid base — beginners likely get enough stimulus from straight weight that the added complexity isn't necessary. The gains appear to transfer well to 1RM performance on straight-weight lifts, which is the key question. It's not just a training novelty; the carryover is real.

Practically speaking, bands and chains are best used as a complement to straight-weight training, not a replacement. A common approach is to use them on dynamic effort days (submaximal loads moved with maximal intent) or as a variation on max effort work. The loading is tricky — you need to account for the weight of the chains or the tension of the bands at the top of the lift, and getting this wrong can throw off your programming. Bands in particular require some experimentation because tension varies by brand, thickness, and how far you stretch them. Chains are more predictable but require equipment most commercial gyms don't have.

Bottom line: the logic is sound and the evidence backs it up, especially for intermediate and advanced lifters looking to break through plateaus or add variety to their training. If you have access to the equipment and understand how to program them, they're a legitimate tool. That said, they're not magic — a well-structured straight-weight program will take most lifters very far, and accommodating resistance is more of an advanced tool than a necessity. If you're curious, try incorporating them for a training block and see how your bar speed and lockout strength respond. The feedback you get from moving a band-loaded bar is pretty immediate and instructive.
