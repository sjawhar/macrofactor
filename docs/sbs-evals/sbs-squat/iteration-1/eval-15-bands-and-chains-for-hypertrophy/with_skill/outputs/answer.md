# Accommodating Resistance for Hypertrophy

Great question, and it's worth separating the hypertrophy conversation from the strength one because the rationale shifts a bit. When we talked about bands and chains for strength, the main argument was about matching the resistance curve to the strength curve — you're strongest at lockout, so adding accommodating resistance lets you overload that range without being limited by what you can handle at the bottom. For hypertrophy, the logic is similar in some ways but the tradeoffs look a little different.

The honest answer is that the direct hypertrophy data on accommodating resistance is sparse. We don't have a robust body of research specifically comparing muscle growth outcomes between straight-weight squats and band or chain squats. What we do have is a reasonable mechanistic case that's probably neutral to mildly positive for most lifters. Accommodating resistance tends to increase total mechanical work across the rep — you're moving more total load through the full range — and it can encourage more explosive intent on the concentric, which may have some benefit for motor unit recruitment. If you're already training with good technique and appropriate volume, adding bands or chains isn't likely to hurt your hypertrophy outcomes, and it might help a little by keeping tension higher through the lockout portion of the rep.

The key caveat, though, is what happens at the bottom of the squat. For hypertrophy, there's a strong case that the stretched position — the bottom of the squat — is particularly valuable. Muscles under load at long lengths appear to be a meaningful driver of hypertrophic stimulus. Bands, by design, reduce tension at the bottom (that's the whole point — they're slack or near-slack in the hole). If you're using very high band tension relative to your bar weight, you can end up with a situation where the bottom of the squat is almost unloaded, which is the opposite of what you'd want for hypertrophy. Chains are a bit more forgiving here since they don't create the same elastic rebound effect, but the principle still applies: if accommodating resistance is dominating your total load, you're potentially shortchanging the stretched-position stimulus.

The practical recommendation is to keep accommodating resistance as a supplementary tool rather than a primary one for hypertrophy-focused training. If you use bands, keep the band tension modest — maybe 15–25% of your total load at most — so that you still have meaningful bar weight in the hole. Chains are generally easier to manage in this regard. And if your primary goal is hypertrophy, you're probably better served by prioritizing deep, controlled ROM with straight weight as your main squat work, then using bands or chains occasionally for variation or to address specific sticking points. The reference files note that for bodybuilding and general hypertrophy, the priority is long controlled ROM and movement quality — accommodating resistance is a useful tool in that context, but it shouldn't come at the expense of loading the bottom position well.
