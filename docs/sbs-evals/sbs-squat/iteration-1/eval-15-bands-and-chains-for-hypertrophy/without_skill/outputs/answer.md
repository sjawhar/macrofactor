# Accommodating Resistance for Hypertrophy

Great question — and it's one that doesn't get enough nuance in the hypertrophy conversation. Accommodating resistance (bands and chains) is most commonly discussed in the context of strength and peaking, but there are legitimate reasons to consider it for muscle growth as well, with some important caveats.

The core mechanism worth thinking about here is the force-velocity and force-length relationship of muscle. For hypertrophy, there's growing evidence that loading muscles in their lengthened (stretched) position produces a strong growth stimulus — possibly because of the mechanical tension placed on titin and the passive structures of the muscle at long lengths, which may amplify the anabolic signal. Accommodating resistance, by design, adds load at the shortened end of the range of motion (where bands are most stretched, chains are fully lifted). This means the resistance profile is essentially the _opposite_ of what you'd want if your goal is maximizing tension in the stretched position. For a squat, the hardest point with bands or chains is near lockout — not in the hole where the quads are most lengthened.

That said, accommodating resistance isn't useless for hypertrophy. It can allow you to train with higher bar speeds and greater intent through the full range, which may improve motor unit recruitment and overall mechanical work done per set. It also lets you overload the top portion of a lift, which can be useful for muscles that are more active in shortened positions or for movements where the sticking point is near the top. The total mechanical tension across the set still matters, and if accommodating resistance lets you move more total load with better bar speed, there's still a hypertrophic stimulus — it's just not optimized for the stretched-position loading that seems to be particularly potent.

For most people focused primarily on hypertrophy, straight weight — or even better, techniques that increase load in the stretched position (like a deficit, a deeper ROM, or a pause at the bottom) — will likely be more effective than bands and chains. If you're using accommodating resistance in a hypertrophy block, it probably makes more sense as a tool for variety, to address specific weaknesses, or to manage joint stress at the bottom of a lift, rather than as a primary driver of muscle growth. It's not a bad tool; it's just not the sharpest one in the box for this specific goal.

Bottom line: accommodating resistance is a valuable training tool, but its resistance profile runs somewhat counter to the principle of prioritizing stretched-position loading for hypertrophy. Use it intentionally — it has a place in a well-rounded program — but don't expect it to be your primary hypertrophy driver. Straight weight with full range of motion, or techniques that emphasize the lengthened position, will generally give you more bang for your buck when muscle growth is the main objective.
