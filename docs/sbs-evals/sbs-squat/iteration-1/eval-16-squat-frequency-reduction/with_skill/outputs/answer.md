# Can You Still Progress Squatting Only Once Per Week?

Yes — you can absolutely still make progress squatting once per week, but there are real trade-offs to understand and a smart way to set it up so you don't leave gains on the table.

## Progression Is Still Possible, Just Slower

Frequency matters for skill acquisition and volume accumulation, but it isn't the only lever. If you've been squatting 2–3 times per week, you've already built a solid technical foundation and a meaningful strength base. Dropping to once per week doesn't erase that. What it does mean is that your rate of progress will likely slow compared to higher-frequency training — you're getting fewer practice reps per week, less frequent stimulus, and less opportunity to accumulate volume across sessions. That's a real cost, but it's not a dealbreaker, especially if life circumstances are forcing the change. Plenty of lifters have made consistent long-term progress on a single squat day when that session is structured well.

## The Volume Management Problem

The biggest mistake people make when cutting frequency is also cutting volume too aggressively. If you were doing, say, 4 sets on Monday and 4 sets on Thursday, dropping to "just one session" and doing 4 sets feels like the natural move — but you've just cut your weekly squat volume in half. That's a significant drop, and it's more than necessary. The goal is to consolidate most of your previous weekly volume into that single session, not to match your old per-session volume.

A practical starting point: take your previous per-session set count and add 1–2 sets. So if you were doing 4 sets per session across two days (8 sets total per week), your single session should be in the range of 5–6 sets. You probably won't fully replace all the volume from three sessions in one, but you want to avoid dropping more than roughly 50% of your total weekly volume. That threshold is where the drop starts to meaningfully compromise your ability to maintain — let alone build — strength and muscle.

## How to Structure the Session

With only one squat day, that session needs to be your primary squat focus — not an afterthought. Treat it as the anchor of your lower-body training week. A few practical guidelines:

- **Prioritize your main squat variation first**, when you're freshest. Don't bury it after a bunch of other leg work.
- **Use a rep range that gives you enough volume in fewer sets** — working sets in the 4–8 rep range tend to be efficient for strength, while sets in the 8–12 range can help accumulate more volume per set for hypertrophy.
- **Keep skill exposure consistent.** One of the programming priorities for stalled squats is maintaining primary squat frequency and skill exposure. Since you're cutting frequency, you need to make sure the technique work in that one session is high quality — full brace sequence, consistent setup, controlled descent, and maximal concentric intent on every rep.
- **Match accessories to your weak links.** If you've been dealing with a good-morning squat pattern (hips shooting up, knees drifting back), prioritize quad accessories like front squats, leg press, or hack squats in the same session or elsewhere in the week. If your squat-to-deadlift gap is large, add bracing and upper-back work. The accessories don't have to happen on squat day — they just need to happen.

## Managing the Transition

Don't try to cram your full previous weekly volume into week one. Give yourself 2–3 weeks to ramp up the per-session volume as your recovery adapts to the higher single-session load. Start conservatively — your joints and connective tissue need time to adjust to doing more work in one shot even if the total weekly volume is similar. After that ramp period, you can push the session harder and track progress normally. If you find after a few months that progress has genuinely stalled and you have any flexibility to add a second squat day back — even a lighter technical session — that's worth doing. But one well-constructed session per week is a legitimate training structure, not a compromise you need to apologize for.
