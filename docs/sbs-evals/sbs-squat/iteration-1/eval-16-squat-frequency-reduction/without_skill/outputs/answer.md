# Can You Still Progress Squatting Once Per Week?

Yes, you can absolutely continue making progress on just one squat day per week — especially if you've already built a solid base from training 2-3 times per week. Frequency is a tool for distributing volume and managing fatigue, not a prerequisite for progress in itself. What actually drives adaptation is sufficient stimulus (volume and intensity) and adequate recovery. One well-constructed squat session per week can deliver both, particularly for intermediate and advanced lifters who have developed the capacity to handle higher per-session loads.

The key adjustment when dropping from 2-3 sessions to one is consolidating your weekly volume into that single day without letting it collapse. If you were doing, say, 12-15 total working sets per week across three sessions, you don't need to cram all 15 into one day — that would likely exceed your recovery capacity and hurt performance within the session. A more practical target is somewhere in the range of 6-10 hard working sets in that one session, prioritizing quality over quantity. You want each set to be genuinely challenging and executed with good technique, not grinding through junk volume just to hit a number.

For the session structure itself, lead with your primary squat variation (back squat, front squat, whatever your main lift is) and do your heaviest, most demanding work first when you're freshest. A common and effective approach is to work up to a top set — either a heavy single, double, or triple, or a rep PR at a given weight — and then follow it with back-off sets at a lower intensity (typically 70-85% of your top set) for additional volume. This gives you both the high-intensity stimulus that drives strength and the volume needed to support hypertrophy and technical reinforcement. You can also include one or two squat accessory movements (pause squats, tempo squats, goblet squats) to round out the session.

One thing to watch for is that reducing frequency often means you'll feel less "practiced" in the movement week to week. The groove can feel a bit rustier when you're only doing it once. To counteract this, be deliberate about your warm-up — don't rush it. Use your lighter warm-up sets to dial in your positioning and bracing before you get to working weights. Over time, if you find your technique is degrading or your progress stalls, that's a signal that once-weekly frequency may not be enough for you specifically, and you might benefit from adding even a light second session (like a moderate-intensity day with fewer sets) to maintain the motor pattern.

The bottom line: once-weekly squatting is a legitimate training structure, not a compromise. Many lifters — especially those with busier schedules or who are prioritizing other lifts — make consistent strength gains this way. The formula is straightforward: keep the intensity high, maintain enough volume to provide a training stimulus, and be consistent week over week. Progress may come a bit more slowly than with higher frequency, but it will come.
