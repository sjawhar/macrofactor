# Why Are Deadlifts More Fatiguing Than Squats — And Should Volume Be Lower?

**Question:** "Why are deadlifts considered more fatiguing than squats, and should deadlift volume usually be lower?"

---

The claim that deadlifts are _inherently_ more fatiguing than squats is one of those gym truisms that gets repeated so often it starts to feel like settled science — but it deserves a harder look. The honest answer is: **it depends on the individual, and the blanket rule is often wrong.** For some lifters, squats are the more systemically demanding movement. For others, deadlifts are. The fatigue difference, where it exists, usually comes down to a handful of specific contributors rather than something fundamental about the deadlift as a movement.

The most commonly cited contributors to deadlift fatigue are grip stress, spinal loading under flexion, and what's sometimes called _path dependency_ — the fact that the deadlift has no eccentric loading phase to "give back" elastic energy the way a squat does. Grip is a real factor: the forearms, hands, and upper back are working hard to maintain bar contact through the entire pull, and that systemic demand adds up across a session in a way that squatting doesn't replicate. Spinal flexion tolerance is another genuine variable — some lifters find that heavy pulling under any degree of lumbar load accumulates fatigue faster than the more braced, extension-biased position of a squat. And because the deadlift starts from a dead stop every rep (hence the name), there's no stretch-shortening cycle to reduce the muscular demand the way a controlled squat descent and rebound provides. These are real mechanisms, not myths — but they're _individual_ in how much they matter.

What often gets missed is that squats carry their own fatigue contributors that can be just as significant. The bracing demands across a full squat session — especially with a belt, where you're generating maximal intra-abdominal pressure rep after rep — are substantial. The eccentric loading that makes squats feel "easier" in the moment also means more muscle damage over time, which is a fatigue cost that shows up in recovery rather than in-session. And for lifters who are quad-limited or who are doing high-bar or front squat work with deep ROM, the systemic demand can rival or exceed what they experience pulling. The squat:deadlift gap in performance (a large gap often signals bracing or upper-back weakness, as covered in weak-link diagnosis) is a useful signal here — if your squat is dramatically lagging your deadlift, that's a sign squats may actually be the harder movement for your body to recover from right now.

Practically, the programming implication isn't "always do less deadlift volume" — it's **match volume to your individual recovery capacity and monitor the signals.** A useful starting framework: if you're finding that deadlift sessions are bleeding into squat quality later in the week (or vice versa), that's the signal to adjust sequencing or volume, not a reason to pre-emptively cap deadlift sets. Many lifters do perfectly well with similar weekly volumes on both lifts. Others genuinely need to pull less frequently or with lower total sets. The right answer comes from running the experiment on yourself — track performance quality across the week, not just how you feel walking out of the gym. If your squat technique is deteriorating on days after heavy pulls, reduce deadlift volume or move it further from squat days. If it's not, there's no reason to leave deadlift volume on the table based on a general rule.

The bottom line: the "deadlifts are more fatiguing" heuristic exists because it's _often_ true for _many_ lifters, particularly those who are newer to pulling heavy or who have grip or spinal flexion tolerance as a limiting factor. But it's not a universal law, and programming based on it without checking your own data is leaving performance on the table. Treat it as a hypothesis to test, not a rule to follow. Adjust volume based on how your training actually responds — not on what the conventional wisdom says should be harder.
