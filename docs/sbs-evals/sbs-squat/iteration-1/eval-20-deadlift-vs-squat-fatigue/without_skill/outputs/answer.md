# Why Are Deadlifts More Fatiguing Than Squats?

Great question, and it's one that comes up a lot in programming discussions. The short answer is: yes, deadlifts are generally considered more systemically fatiguing than squats, and that perception is well-supported by practical experience and some research — though the gap is probably smaller than the lifting community often assumes.

## Why Deadlifts Tend to Be More Fatiguing

The main reason deadlifts carry a higher fatigue cost comes down to the sheer amount of muscle mass involved and the mechanical demands of the lift. Deadlifts recruit the entire posterior chain — spinal erectors, glutes, hamstrings, traps, lats, and grip — all working hard simultaneously, often with heavier absolute loads than you'd use on a squat. The spinal erectors in particular take a beating, and they're notoriously slow to recover. There's also no eccentric loading phase in a conventional deadlift the way there is in a squat — you're not lowering the bar under control — but the concentric demand is enormous, and the isometric spinal loading throughout the pull is significant. Some research and a lot of anecdotal evidence from powerlifters suggests that heavy deadlift sessions leave people feeling beat up for 3–5 days, whereas squat sessions of comparable effort often feel more manageable by day 2–3.

## The Nuances Worth Knowing

That said, context matters a lot. Sumo deadlifts are generally considered less fatiguing than conventional, partly because the range of motion is shorter and the torso stays more upright, reducing spinal erector stress. Romanian deadlifts (RDLs) are a different story — the heavy eccentric loading creates significant hamstring soreness and can be quite fatiguing in their own right. Squats with high bar, deep range of motion, and heavy loads are no joke either. The fatigue comparison also depends on training history: someone who squats frequently and rarely deadlifts will find squats more fatiguing, and vice versa. Adaptation is real.

## Practical Programming Implications

Because of the higher fatigue cost, most evidence-based programs do prescribe lower deadlift volume than squat volume. Where you might run 15–20+ sets of squat work per week across multiple sessions, deadlift volume is often kept to 6–12 working sets per week, sometimes less for advanced lifters pulling very heavy. Frequency is also typically lower — many intermediate and advanced lifters deadlift once or twice per week, while squatting two to four times. This isn't because deadlifts are less important; it's because the recovery demand means you can't pile on volume the same way without accumulating excessive fatigue that bleeds into the rest of your training.

## The Bottom Line

Yes, you should generally program deadlift volume more conservatively than squat volume, especially if you're training both seriously. A good rule of thumb: start with roughly half the weekly sets for deadlifts compared to squats, monitor recovery, and adjust from there. Prioritize quality over quantity — a few heavy, well-executed sets of deadlifts will do more for you than grinding through high-volume sessions that leave you wrecked for days. And if you find deadlifts are consistently tanking your squat performance or leaving you chronically fatigued, that's a clear signal to pull back on deadlift volume or frequency before anything else.
