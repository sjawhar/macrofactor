# How to Respond When a Doctor Says to Stop Squatting

First, it's worth genuinely understanding where that advice is coming from. Most physicians are trained to minimize risk, and "stop doing the thing that hurts" is a reasonable first-pass heuristic in a 15-minute appointment. They're not wrong to be cautious — they just typically aren't evaluating your squat technique, your training history, or what "squatting" actually looks like for you. A doctor who sees someone with knee pain and hears "I squat heavy" is going to pattern-match to a risk they want to eliminate. That's not bad medicine; it's just incomplete information applied to your specific situation.

That said, the blanket claim that squatting is bad for knees isn't well-supported when we look at how the movement actually works. Squatting is a fundamental human movement pattern, and when performed with sound technique — stable foot tripod, knees tracking in line with toes, controlled depth, proper bracing — it loads the knee joint in a way it's designed to handle. The key variables are technique quality, load management, and whether the depth and stance are matched to your anatomy. Someone squatting with excessive knee cave, poor bracing, or a stance that fights their hip structure is going to accumulate more stress than someone who has dialed those things in. The movement itself isn't the problem; how it's being executed and progressed usually is.

The most useful next step is to seek a second opinion from a sports medicine physician or a physical therapist who works with strength athletes. These practitioners evaluate movement under load, assess your specific anatomy and mobility, and can distinguish between "this exercise is contraindicated for you" and "this exercise needs to be modified or reloaded more carefully." They can also identify whether your knee symptoms are coming from a structural issue that genuinely limits squatting, or from a correctable technical or programming problem — things like knee cave, poor ankle dorsiflexion, or a stance that doesn't match your hip socket orientation. Those are solvable problems, not reasons to abandon the lift.

Ultimately, this is a cost-benefit decision that only you can make based on your priorities. If squatting is central to your training goals — whether that's powerlifting, general strength, or body composition — then the cost of eliminating it entirely is real and worth weighing carefully. If your knee symptoms are mild and technique-related, the path forward is usually to reduce load, address the technical flaw, and rebuild. If there's a genuine structural issue, a qualified sports medicine professional can help you find a variation — front squat, goblet squat, heel-elevated squat, or a reduced range of motion — that keeps you training while protecting the joint. Stopping entirely is one option on the spectrum, but it's rarely the only one, and it's worth getting a more complete picture before making that call.

The short version: take the concern seriously, don't dismiss it, but also don't treat one general practitioner's advice as the final word on a complex, technique-dependent movement. Get eyes on your squat from someone who specializes in athletic populations, and make an informed decision from there.
