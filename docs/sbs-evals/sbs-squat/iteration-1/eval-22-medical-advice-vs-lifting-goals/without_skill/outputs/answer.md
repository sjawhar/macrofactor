# How to Respond When a Doctor Tells You to Stop Squatting

This is a situation a lot of lifters run into, and it can be genuinely frustrating — especially when squatting is a core part of your training or something you love doing. The first thing to recognize is that your doctor almost certainly has your best interests at heart, and their concern is worth taking seriously. At the same time, "stop squatting" is a very broad recommendation, and it's worth understanding _why_ they said it before deciding how to respond.

The most important step is to ask your doctor to be more specific. What exactly are they concerned about? Is it a structural issue like meniscus damage, osteoarthritis, or a ligament problem? Or is it more of a general precaution based on the assumption that squatting is inherently risky? These are very different situations. If there's a specific diagnosis driving the recommendation, you want to understand it clearly — that information will help you and any other professionals you work with make smarter decisions. If the advice is more general ("squatting is hard on the knees"), it's worth a respectful follow-up conversation, because the evidence on squatting and knee health is actually more nuanced than that blanket statement suggests.

Consider seeking a second opinion from a sports medicine physician or an orthopedic specialist who works with athletes. General practitioners are excellent at many things, but they may not have deep familiarity with strength training or the biomechanics of the squat. A sports medicine doctor is more likely to ask about your training history, your technique, your pain patterns, and your goals — and to give you a more tailored recommendation rather than a blanket "stop." A physical therapist who works with strength athletes can also be invaluable here, both for assessment and for helping you address any underlying issues that might be contributing to knee discomfort.

If there is a real knee issue, the answer is rarely "never squat again" — it's more often "let's figure out what's causing the problem and address it." That might mean temporarily reducing load, adjusting squat depth or stance, improving mobility, strengthening the surrounding musculature, or working through a structured rehab protocol. Many people with significant knee pathology continue to squat safely and productively with appropriate modifications. The goal is to find a path that respects your body's current state while keeping you moving toward your athletic goals.

The bottom line: don't dismiss your doctor's concern, but don't treat it as the final word either. Get clarity on the diagnosis, seek out professionals who understand athletic training, and approach the situation as a problem to solve rather than a verdict to accept. Squatting is one of the most fundamental human movement patterns — the goal is to do it in a way that keeps you healthy for the long term, not to avoid it entirely out of an abundance of caution.
