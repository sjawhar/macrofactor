# Teen Strength Training: A Guide for a 15-Year-Old

Great question — and the good news is that 15 is a fantastic age to start building a foundation of strength and muscle. Your body is primed to respond well to training, and the habits you build now will pay dividends for decades. Here's how to approach it intelligently.

## Exercise Selection

Focus on compound, multi-joint movements that train your whole body efficiently and build real-world strength. The core of your program should be squats, deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, and rows. Pull-ups and dips are excellent bodyweight additions. These movements recruit the most muscle mass, teach your body to work as a unit, and give you the most return on your training time. Isolation exercises (curls, lateral raises, tricep pushdowns) are fine to include but should be secondary — maybe 20–30% of your total work. At your age, mastering movement patterns is more important than chasing a pump, so spend real time learning how to squat and hinge with good technique before loading the bar heavily.

## Weekly Volume and Frequency

A full-body or upper/lower split 3–4 days per week works extremely well at this stage. Aim for roughly 10–20 sets per muscle group per week, spread across those sessions. For example, if you train 3 days full-body, you might do 3–4 sets of squats, 3–4 sets of a hinge (deadlift or Romanian deadlift), 3–4 sets of a push, and 3–4 sets of a pull each session — that's already solid volume for every major muscle group. Don't try to train 6 days a week right out of the gate; recovery is where growth actually happens, and teenagers often underestimate how much sleep and rest matter.

## Rep Ranges, Intensity, and Effort

For building both strength and muscle, a rep range of 5–15 reps per set covers you well. Heavier sets of 3–6 reps on your main lifts build strength and teach you to strain; sets of 8–15 reps on accessory work drive hypertrophy effectively. The key variable is effort — sets should be challenging, finishing 1–3 reps shy of failure most of the time (what coaches call leaving "reps in reserve"). Training to absolute failure every set isn't necessary and increases injury risk, especially while you're still learning technique. Progressive overload is the engine of progress: try to add a little weight or do one more rep each week or two. That consistent upward trend over months is what builds real size and strength.

## Calories and Macros

You cannot build muscle in a significant calorie deficit — your body needs fuel to grow. At 15, you're also still growing in height, so your calorie needs are genuinely high. A rough starting point is to eat enough that your weight is slowly trending upward (0.25–0.5 lbs per week is a reasonable pace for a lean gain). Don't be afraid of food. Protein is the most important macro for muscle building — aim for around 0.7–1 gram of protein per pound of bodyweight per day (so roughly 100–160g for most teens). Fill the rest of your calories with carbohydrates (your primary training fuel) and healthy fats. Prioritize whole foods — meat, eggs, dairy, rice, potatoes, oats, fruits, vegetables — but don't stress about perfection. Consistency over months matters far more than any single meal.

## A Note on Youth-Specific Considerations

At 15, your growth plates are still open, which means you should be especially mindful of technique and avoid maximal 1-rep-max attempts until you're more experienced (generally late teens). This isn't a reason to avoid lifting — research consistently shows resistance training is safe and beneficial for adolescents — but it is a reason to prioritize form over ego. Sleep is non-negotiable: aim for 8–9 hours per night, as this is when growth hormone peaks and muscle repair happens. Finally, be patient. Teenagers often make rapid early progress ("newbie gains"), but building a truly impressive physique takes years, not months. Enjoy the process, stay consistent, and the results will come.
