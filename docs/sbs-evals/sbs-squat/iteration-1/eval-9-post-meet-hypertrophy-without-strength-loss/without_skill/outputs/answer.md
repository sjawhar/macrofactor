# Post-Meet Hypertrophy Block: Keeping Your Strength Gains

Great question, and it's one a lot of competitive lifters wrestle with. The good news is that transitioning into a hypertrophy block after a meet doesn't mean abandoning your strength — it means building the muscular foundation that will make your next strength peak even better. The key is being strategic about how you bridge the two phases.

**Start with a deload, then ease in gradually.** Right after a meet, your body has been pushed to its limit — CNS fatigue, joint stress, and accumulated wear are all real. Give yourself 1–2 weeks of reduced volume and intensity before ramping into your hypertrophy block. When you do start adding volume, don't jump straight to your maximum recoverable volume. Begin at moderate weekly sets per muscle group (think 10–14 sets for your main movers) and add 2–4 sets per week over the mesocycle. This progressive overload on volume is the same principle you apply to load — it keeps adaptation happening without overwhelming your recovery.

**Keep the competition lifts in the program, just in a different role.** One of the biggest mistakes lifters make is completely dropping the squat, bench, and deadlift during a hypertrophy block. You don't need to max out, but doing the movements 1–2x per week at moderate intensities (65–75% of your 1RM) for sets of 6–10 keeps the neural patterns sharp and maintains your strength expression. Think of these as "maintenance" sessions — enough stimulus to hold onto what you've built, while the bulk of your volume comes from variations and accessories that drive hypertrophy (paused squats, Romanian deadlifts, close-grip bench, etc.).

**Shift your rep ranges and rest periods intentionally.** Hypertrophy responds well to moderate rep ranges (6–15 reps) with moderate rest (90–180 seconds), which is a meaningful shift from the heavy triples and long rests of meet prep. This change in stimulus is actually a feature, not a bug — your muscles will respond to the new stress. Just make sure you're still training close to failure (1–3 RIR) on your working sets. Proximity to failure matters more than the specific rep range for muscle growth.

**Trust the process — hypertrophy blocks protect your strength long-term.** Adding muscle mass is one of the most reliable ways to increase your strength ceiling. A well-executed 8–12 week hypertrophy mesocycle, followed by a proper strength and peaking block, almost always results in new PRs. You're not losing your strength gains — you're investing them. Track your performance on your maintenance compound work, and if you're hitting similar numbers at the end of the block as you were at the start, you've done your job. The strength will come back fast when you return to lower reps and higher intensities, often faster than it took to build the first time.
