# Core Exercises for Getting Bigger and Stronger for General Health and Recreation

For someone who wants to get bigger and stronger without competing in powerlifting or bodybuilding, the exercise selection question has a clear answer: prioritize movements that train the most muscle mass, can be progressively loaded over time, and are sustainable for your joints and schedule.

## The SBS Exercise Selection Framework

The SBS approach organizes exercises into a hierarchy:

1. **Primary lifts** — goal-specific, high skill, heavy force expression
2. **Close variants** — address weak points, improve skill
3. **Accessory compounds** — high stimulus per fatigue unit
4. **Isolation work** — target lagging muscles, improve awareness

For a general health/recreation lifter, you don't need to worry much about competition-specific technique. Instead, you want exercises that check these boxes:

- Trains a large amount of muscle mass
- Can be loaded progressively over months and years
- Doesn't aggravate your joints
- Can be executed consistently in your training environment

## The Core Movement Patterns

Every effective general-purpose program covers these five patterns:

**1. Squat pattern** — Trains quads, glutes, hamstrings, and core. Options include:

- Barbell back squat (high or low bar)
- Goblet squat (great for beginners)
- Leg press (lower skill, good for accumulating quad volume)
- Split squat / Bulgarian split squat (unilateral, excellent for glutes and quads)

**2. Hip hinge pattern** — Trains hamstrings, glutes, and posterior chain. Options include:

- Conventional or sumo deadlift
- Romanian deadlift (RDL) — excellent for hamstring hypertrophy with lower systemic fatigue
- Hip thrust — outstanding glute stimulus
- Kettlebell swing

**3. Horizontal push** — Trains chest, anterior delts, and triceps. Options include:

- Barbell bench press (flat or slight incline)
- Dumbbell press (more shoulder-friendly for many people)
- Machine chest press (good for accumulating volume safely)

**4. Horizontal pull** — Trains lats, upper back, rear delts, and biceps. Options include:

- Barbell or dumbbell row
- Cable row
- Machine row
- Chest-supported row (removes lower back from the equation)

**5. Vertical pull** — Trains lats and biceps. Options include:

- Pull-up / chin-up (bodyweight or weighted)
- Lat pulldown (great for beginners or when pull-ups aren't yet achievable)
- Cable pullover

## What to Prioritize and Why

For a general health/recreation lifter, I'd recommend building a program around:

- **One squat pattern** (e.g., back squat or leg press)
- **One hinge pattern** (e.g., RDL or deadlift)
- **One horizontal push** (e.g., bench press or dumbbell press)
- **One horizontal pull** (e.g., barbell row or cable row)
- **One vertical pull** (e.g., lat pulldown or pull-up)

This covers the entire body with five exercises. Add isolation work (lateral raises, bicep curls, tricep extensions, leg curls) as supplementary volume once the main patterns are established.

## Volume and Frequency Targets

From the SBS framework, a practical baseline for intermediates is 8–18 sets per muscle group per week, distributed across 2–3 sessions. For a general lifter, starting at the lower end (8–12 sets) and building from there is appropriate.

A simple 3-day full-body program hitting each pattern twice per week is sufficient for most general health goals. A 4-day upper/lower split allows more volume and is a natural progression.

## The Most Important Principle

The best exercises are the ones you'll actually do consistently and can progress over time. A program built around exercises you enjoy and can execute well will outperform a theoretically optimal program you hate or can't stick to. Start with the movement patterns above, find the specific exercises within each pattern that feel good for your body, and focus on progressive overload — adding reps or load over time — as the primary driver of progress.
