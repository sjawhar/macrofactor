# A Hypothetical "SBS Combine": What Tests Should a Generally Fit Person Track Every 12 Weeks?

This is a fun design problem. The goal is a testing battery that's comprehensive enough to capture meaningful fitness dimensions, specific enough to be sensitive to real changes, and practical enough to actually do every 12 weeks without it becoming a major production.

## Design Principles

A good testing battery should:

- Cover multiple fitness dimensions (strength, power, endurance, body composition)
- Use tests that are reproducible and minimally technique-dependent
- Be sensitive enough to detect real changes over 12 weeks
- Not require specialized equipment that most people don't have access to
- Take no more than 1–2 sessions to complete

## The Proposed Battery

### Strength Tests (Lower Body)

**1. Trap bar deadlift 3-rep max (or estimated 1RM)**
The trap bar deadlift is the best general-purpose lower body strength test: it involves the most total muscle mass, is less technique-dependent than the conventional deadlift, and is accessible to most people. Test a 3RM and use a conversion formula to estimate 1RM, or just track the 3RM directly. Report relative to bodyweight (e.g., 2.1x BW).

**2. Goblet squat or leg press max (if trap bar unavailable)**
For people without access to a trap bar, a leg press or goblet squat 5RM works as a lower body strength proxy.

### Strength Tests (Upper Body)

**3. Pull-up max reps (bodyweight)**
Bodyweight pull-ups are an excellent upper body pulling strength test. They're self-scaling (heavier people have to move more weight), require no equipment beyond a bar, and are highly reproducible. Track max reps with full range of motion.

**4. Push-up max reps (or weighted push-up / bench press)**
For horizontal pushing, max push-ups are accessible and self-scaling. If you're past the point where push-ups are challenging, switch to a bench press 3RM relative to bodyweight.

### Power Test

**5. Vertical jump (countermovement jump)**
The vertical jump is the gold standard for lower body power assessment. It's quick, requires no equipment beyond a wall and chalk (or a jump mat), and is highly sensitive to changes in neuromuscular function. It also captures something that pure strength tests miss: the ability to express force quickly.

### Endurance Test

**6. 1-mile run time (or 2km row)**
A 1-mile run is a practical test of cardiovascular fitness that most people can do. It's short enough to be a genuine maximal effort, long enough to be aerobically demanding, and requires no equipment. For people with running limitations, a 2km row on an ergometer is an excellent alternative with better reproducibility.

### Body Composition

**7. Bodyweight + waist circumference**
Scale weight alone is a poor body composition metric, but combined with waist circumference it becomes much more informative. Waist circumference is a reliable proxy for visceral fat and tracks body composition changes that scale weight misses (e.g., simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss). Measure at the navel, first thing in the morning, same conditions each time.

### Optional Additions

**8. Grip strength (hand dynamometer)**
If you have access to a grip dynamometer, grip strength is a surprisingly good proxy for overall strength and has strong associations with health outcomes. It's also minimally technique-dependent.

**9. Farmer's carry distance or time**
A loaded carry (e.g., 50% bodyweight per hand) tests grip, core stability, and work capacity in a way that pure strength tests don't. Walk for max distance or time.

## Testing Protocol Notes

- Test on a day when you're fresh (not after a hard training week)
- Standardize conditions: same time of day, same warm-up, same equipment
- For strength tests, use a standard warm-up protocol and take 2–3 attempts at the working weight
- Record everything: the number, the conditions, and how you felt
- Don't test during a deload week or immediately after a competition

## What This Battery Captures

| Dimension              | Test                         |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Lower body strength    | Trap bar deadlift 3RM        |
| Upper body pulling     | Pull-up max reps             |
| Upper body pushing     | Push-up max / bench 3RM      |
| Lower body power       | Vertical jump                |
| Cardiovascular fitness | 1-mile run                   |
| Body composition       | Weight + waist circumference |

This covers the major fitness dimensions without requiring a full day or specialized equipment. Every 12 weeks gives you enough time for meaningful adaptation to occur while keeping the feedback loop tight enough to catch problems early.
