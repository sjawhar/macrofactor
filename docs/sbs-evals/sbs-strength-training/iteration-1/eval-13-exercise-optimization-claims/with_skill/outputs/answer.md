# Exercise "Optimization" Claims: Useful Framework, Bad Certainty

Your instinct is good: anatomy-informed exercise selection can absolutely improve training. The problem is the way this trend is often presented—like tiny setup tweaks are universally superior for everyone.

## What anatomy can do (and what it can’t)

Anatomy is best used for **hypothesis generation**, not dogma. If someone has long femurs, limited shoulder tolerance, or a specific sticking point, that can guide broad choices (e.g., stance width, press angle, ROM strategy). But most hyper-specific claims ("this exact cable path grows the upper pec 23% better") are rarely backed by strong **longitudinal hypertrophy evidence**.

So the practical hierarchy from an SBS lens is:

1. Pick evidence-informed defaults that are broadly effective.
2. Run enough high-quality volume to drive growth (typically **8-18 hard sets/week** per muscle for many intermediates, in **2-3 sessions/week**).
3. Individualize based on real feedback over time.

## Why absolutist messaging is a problem

When influencers frame one setup as “optimal” and alternatives as “bad,” two things happen:

- People with limited equipment think they can’t train effectively (nocebo effect).
- Lifters chase micro-adjustments instead of nailing major drivers: progressive overload, volume, effort, and consistency.

In practice, if you’re training in the right effort zones—roughly **0-3 RIR** for hypertrophy work, usually in **6-15 reps** and about **60-80% 1RM** for most accessories—you’re already capturing most of the signal.

## How to personalize without getting lost

Use a 4-week decision loop:

- Keep a movement if it’s comfortable, you can progress load/reps, and target muscles get clear stimulus (pump/soreness pattern).
- Replace or modify if it repeatedly irritates joints, can’t be progressed, or performance trends down despite recovery.

This aligns with SBS progression logic: start with stable programming, then adjust one lever at a time (load, reps, sets, or variation), rather than changing everything at once.

## Bottom line

The concept of anatomy-guided training is sound in broad strokes. The overconfident “tiny tweak = universally optimal” claims are where things derail. There are real **inter-individual differences**, so one “best” setup won’t generalize perfectly.

Use evidence-informed defaults, keep weekly volume in productive ranges, train hard with good form, and individualize from outcomes and tolerability—not internet certainty.
