# Work-Life Balance in Fitness Careers

This question is really about Greg and Eric specifically, so it's worth being honest: we don't have direct insight into their personal routines. What we can do is reason from what they've shared publicly and apply some general principles about sustainable high-output careers.

## What They've Said Publicly

Greg Nuckols and Eric Trexler have both acknowledged that running Stronger By Science, coaching, producing the MASS Research Review, building MacroFactor, and competing (in Greg's case) simultaneously is genuinely demanding. Neither has presented a tidy "here's my system" answer, which is itself informative — it suggests the balance is messy and evolving rather than solved.

A few themes that come up when they discuss this:

**Ruthless prioritization over optimization.** Rather than trying to do everything well simultaneously, they appear to accept that some things get less attention in a given period. When a major project is in crunch (a MacroFactor release, a MASS deadline), other things compress. This is a realistic model for knowledge workers with multiple high-demand outputs.

**Asynchronous collaboration.** The SBS/MASS model relies heavily on written, asynchronous work — research review articles, podcast prep, written coaching feedback. This allows flexible scheduling rather than requiring synchronized blocks of time, which is a significant structural advantage over careers that require constant real-time presence.

**Training as a non-negotiable anchor, not a flexible variable.** Both have discussed treating their own training as a scheduled commitment rather than something that gets squeezed in when convenient. This is consistent with what the research shows: people who treat exercise as a fixed appointment maintain it better than those who treat it as optional.

## The Broader Lesson for Fitness Professionals

The honest answer is that "work-life balance" in careers like theirs is probably a misleading frame. These aren't jobs with clean edges — the work is also the passion, the research informs the coaching, the coaching informs the content, and the content drives the business. The integration is the point.

What seems to matter more than balance is:

- **Sustainable pace within each domain.** Not burning out on any single output by managing volume the same way you'd manage training volume — enough to drive progress, not so much that recovery collapses.
- **Protecting recovery time explicitly.** Sleep, time away from screens, and social connection don't happen automatically in high-output careers. They require the same intentional scheduling as the work itself.
- **Accepting that some periods are imbalanced.** A product launch or a competition prep block will temporarily dominate. The goal is returning to equilibrium, not maintaining perfect balance at all times.

## The Training-Specific Angle

One thing worth noting: both Greg and Eric have discussed how their own training has evolved as their careers have grown. Volume and frequency have generally come down from peak competitive levels, not because they stopped caring, but because the recovery cost of high-volume training competes with the cognitive demands of their work. This is a real tradeoff that most fitness professionals eventually face — the training that made you credible may not be sustainable alongside the career that credibility enabled.

The practical takeaway for anyone in a similar position: treat your training like an intermediate lifter treats their program. Enough volume to maintain and slowly progress, distributed across the week in manageable sessions, with planned deloads when life stress accumulates. Chasing peak training volume while also running a demanding career is a recipe for doing both poorly.
