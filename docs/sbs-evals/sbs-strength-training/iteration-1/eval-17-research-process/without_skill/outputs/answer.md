## Framework for Learning Unfamiliar Topics: A Research Process

When facing a topic you don't know well, a structured approach beats random reading. Here's a practical framework used by researchers and coaches to get up to speed efficiently.

### Step 1: Define the Scope and Key Questions

Before diving into sources, spend 15-30 minutes clarifying what you actually need to know. Write down:

- What's the core question?
- What decisions depend on this knowledge?
- What's the time constraint?
- What's your current understanding (even if incomplete)?

This prevents rabbit holes. If you're learning about insulin resistance for hypertrophy, you don't need to become an endocrinologist—you need to know how it affects muscle protein synthesis and nutrient partitioning.

### Step 2: Find High-Level Overviews First

Start with review articles, textbooks, or expert summaries rather than primary research. A good review paper gives you:

- The current consensus
- Major areas of disagreement
- Key mechanisms and definitions
- Pointers to landmark studies

This builds a mental scaffold before you add details. Reading primary research without this scaffold is inefficient—you'll miss context and misinterpret findings.

### Step 3: Identify Landmark Studies and Mechanisms

Once you understand the landscape, find 3-5 key papers that define the field. These are usually:

- Foundational studies that established core mechanisms
- Recent meta-analyses or systematic reviews
- Papers cited repeatedly by other researchers

Read these carefully, not just abstracts. Understand the methods, sample sizes, effect sizes, and limitations. This is where you build real knowledge.

### Step 4: Check for Disagreement and Nuance

Look for papers that contradict or complicate the consensus. Science progresses by finding exceptions and edge cases. If everyone agrees on something, it's probably true but might be oversimplified. If there's disagreement, understand why—it usually reveals important nuance.

### Step 5: Consult Domain Experts

If possible, talk to someone who works in the field. A 30-minute conversation with an expert can clarify what matters, what's settled, and what's still debated. They can also point you toward the best resources and warn you about common misconceptions.

### Step 6: Test Your Understanding

The best test is explaining it to someone else or writing about it. If you can't explain a concept clearly, you don't understand it yet. This also reveals gaps in your knowledge.

### Practical Heuristics

**Prefer recent over old.** Older papers are often foundational, but recent reviews incorporate newer findings. A 2023 review is usually better than a 2003 paper unless the older one is a landmark study.

**Prefer meta-analyses over single studies.** A meta-analysis of 30 studies is more reliable than one study, even if that study is well-designed. Single studies are useful for understanding mechanisms, not for drawing conclusions.

**Check sample sizes and effect sizes.** A study with 500 subjects showing a 10% effect is more meaningful than one with 20 subjects showing a 50% effect. Small studies are prone to noise and publication bias.

**Be skeptical of extreme claims.** If a paper claims something contradicts everything we know, it's usually wrong or the claim is overstated. Revolutionary findings are rare and usually require replication.

**Distinguish mechanism from outcome.** Understanding how something works (mechanism) is different from knowing if it matters in practice (outcome). Both matter, but they're different questions.

### Time Investment

For a moderately complex topic, expect 10-20 hours to reach competence: 2-3 hours on overviews, 5-10 hours on landmark studies, 2-3 hours on expert consultation or writing, and the rest on filling gaps and testing understanding.

This is faster than random reading and produces deeper knowledge because you're building a coherent mental model rather than collecting isolated facts.
