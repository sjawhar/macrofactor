# NSAIDs Around Training: What the Evidence Actually Says

This is a genuinely nuanced topic where the research is more complicated than most gym advice suggests, and where age really does change the calculus.

## The Theoretical Concern

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin, etc.) work by inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, which reduces prostaglandin synthesis. Prostaglandins are inflammatory signaling molecules — and some of them, particularly PGF2α and PGE2, appear to play a role in muscle protein synthesis and satellite cell activation following resistance training. The concern is that by blunting this inflammatory response, NSAIDs might also blunt the hypertrophic signal.

This is a mechanistically plausible concern, and some cell culture and animal studies support it. The question is whether it translates meaningfully to real-world outcomes in humans.

## What the Human Research Shows

The human evidence is genuinely mixed, and the answer appears to depend heavily on dose, duration, and the population studied.

**In younger adults:** Several studies have found that regular, high-dose NSAID use (e.g., 1200mg ibuprofen/day) during a training program does appear to attenuate hypertrophy compared to placebo — not dramatically, but measurably. A notable study by Trappe et al. found that older adults actually showed _greater_ hypertrophy with NSAID use, which flips the script entirely.

**In older adults (50+):** The picture reverses. Older individuals have chronically elevated baseline inflammation, and NSAIDs may actually improve the anabolic environment by reducing this background noise. Some studies suggest modest benefits to muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy in this population with regular NSAID use.

## Practical Implications by Age

**If you're younger (roughly under 40):**

- Occasional NSAID use for acute pain or injury management is unlikely to meaningfully impair your gains. The concern is with _chronic, high-dose_ use taken specifically to blunt post-workout soreness.
- Deliberately taking ibuprofen after every session to reduce DOMS is probably counterproductive if hypertrophy is your goal. The soreness you're blunting is part of the same signaling cascade that drives adaptation.
- For acute injuries or joint flare-ups, short-term use is a reasonable trade-off. Don't let pain management concerns override injury management.

**If you're older (roughly 50+):**

- The calculus shifts. Chronic low-grade inflammation is a real impediment to muscle protein synthesis in older adults, and NSAIDs may actually help rather than hurt.
- Regular use at standard doses (not megadoses) may be net neutral or even slightly beneficial for hypertrophy in this population.
- That said, the cardiovascular and gastrointestinal risks of chronic NSAID use are real and increase with age — this is a conversation to have with a physician, not just a training decision.

## The Bigger Picture

For most recreational lifters, NSAID use around training is a minor variable compared to the big levers: training volume, intensity, sleep, protein intake, and consistency. If you're taking ibuprofen occasionally for a sore knee or a headache, don't stress about it. If you're taking it daily to manage training soreness, that's worth reconsidering — both for the potential hypertrophy impact and because chronic NSAID use carries real health risks (GI bleeding, kidney stress, cardiovascular effects) that compound over time.

The bottom line: use NSAIDs when you need them for pain management, not as a recovery tool. And if you're older, the hypertrophy concern is largely moot — the inflammation-reducing effect may actually work in your favor.
