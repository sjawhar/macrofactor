# NSAIDs and Hypertrophy: Age, Timing, and Risk

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, etc.) are commonly used for pain and inflammation, but their relationship with muscle growth is nuanced and age-dependent.

## The Mechanism: Why NSAIDs Might Blunt Hypertrophy

Muscle growth is partly driven by inflammation—specifically, the inflammatory response to training triggers satellite cell activation and protein synthesis. NSAIDs suppress prostaglandins and reduce inflammation, which _theoretically_ could blunt this adaptive response.

Research in young, healthy lifters shows mixed results:

- Some studies find modest reductions in hypertrophy when NSAIDs are used chronically around training.
- The effect size is small (5–15% reduction in some studies), and many studies show no significant difference.
- The effect is most pronounced with chronic use (daily or near-daily), not occasional use.

## Age Matters—But Differently Than You'd Think

**Younger lifters (18–35):**

- Have robust inflammatory responses and satellite cell activation.
- Can likely tolerate occasional NSAID use without meaningful hypertrophy loss.
- Chronic daily use is more problematic; occasional use (1–2x per week) is probably fine.
- The risk-benefit calculation favors avoiding NSAIDs if possible, but occasional use isn't a dealbreaker.

**Older lifters (40+):**

- Have naturally blunted inflammatory responses and slower muscle protein synthesis.
- May be _more_ sensitive to NSAID-induced suppression of the adaptive response.
- However, older lifters often have legitimate pain/injury issues where NSAIDs provide functional benefit.
- The trade-off becomes: does pain relief allow better training adherence? If yes, the NSAID might be net-positive.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Avoid chronic use**: Don't take NSAIDs daily or near-daily around training. This is where the evidence is clearest.

2. **Occasional use is probably fine**: Taking ibuprofen once or twice per week for legitimate pain is unlikely to meaningfully blunt hypertrophy.

3. **Timing matters less than frequency**: The idea of "don't take NSAIDs immediately post-workout" is reasonable but probably overstated. Chronic use is the real issue.

4. **Consider alternatives first**:
   - Ice/heat for acute soreness
   - Adequate sleep and nutrition
   - Mobility work
   - Acetaminophen (different mechanism, doesn't suppress prostaglandins)

5. **Age-adjusted thinking**:
   - Younger: prioritize avoiding NSAIDs; you have time and recovery capacity.
   - Older: if NSAIDs enable pain-free training, the hypertrophy cost is likely worth the adherence gain.

## The Bottom Line

NSAIDs probably do suppress hypertrophy somewhat, especially with chronic use. But the effect is modest, and occasional use is unlikely to meaningfully impact your results. If you're taking ibuprofen multiple times per week, it's worth reconsidering. If it's occasional, don't stress about it.
