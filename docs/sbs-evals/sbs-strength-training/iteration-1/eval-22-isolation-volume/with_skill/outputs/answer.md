# How Much Isolation Work Is Enough After Compounds?

This is one of the most practically useful questions in program design, and the answer depends on how much compound work you're already doing and what your goals are.

## The Compound Overlap Principle

Compound movements contribute to multiple muscle groups simultaneously. When counting weekly volume, you need to account for this overlap — but not double-count it. A useful rule from the SBS framework:

- **Prime mover = full set credit**
- **Strong secondary = ~0.5 set credit**

So a set of bench press counts as 1 set for chest, ~0.5 sets for front delts, and ~0.5 sets for triceps. A set of rows counts as 1 set for lats/upper back, ~0.5 sets for biceps.

This means your isolation needs depend heavily on your compound volume.

## Muscle-by-Muscle Breakdown

Using the SBS volume landmarks (MEV = minimum effective volume, MAV = maximum adaptive volume):

### Biceps (MEV: 6-8 sets/week, MAV: 10-16 sets/week)

If you're doing 12-16 sets of rows and pull-ups per week, you're already getting 6-8 sets of bicep credit from compounds. That puts you at or above MEV. **2-4 sets of direct curl work** (2-3x/week) is typically sufficient to push into MAV territory. More than 6-8 direct sets is rarely necessary unless biceps are a specific weak point.

### Forearms

Forearms get substantial indirect work from any grip-intensive pulling. Direct forearm work (wrist curls, reverse curls, hammer curls) is rarely necessary for most people. If grip is a limiting factor in your pulling, farmer carries and thick-bar work address this more functionally. **0-3 sets/week** of direct work is usually plenty.

### Calves (MEV: 6-8 sets/week, MAV: 10-16 sets/week)

Calves are unusual — they get almost no meaningful compound overlap from typical lower-body training. Squats and deadlifts don't load the calf through a meaningful range of motion. Calves also tend to respond better to higher frequency (3-5x/week) and higher reps (15-25+). **6-12 direct sets/week** spread across 3-4 sessions is a reasonable starting point. Many people need more than this to see calf growth.

### Delts (MEV: 8-10 sets/week, MAV: 12-20 sets/week)

Pressing movements (bench, overhead press) hit the front delts well. Rows hit the rear delts. The lateral head — which gives the shoulder its width — gets minimal compound stimulus. **4-8 sets of lateral raises per week** is typically the key addition. Rear delt work (face pulls, reverse flies) is valuable for shoulder health even if rows provide some stimulus.

### Core/Abs (MEV: 4-6 sets/week, MAV: 8-12 sets/week)

Heavy compound work (squats, deadlifts, overhead press) provides substantial core stimulus through isometric bracing. Direct ab work is still valuable for full range-of-motion flexion and rotation. **4-8 direct sets/week** is sufficient for most people — more than this has diminishing returns and the core recovers quickly enough to handle 3-4x/week frequency.

## The General Principle

The SBS framework recommends using accessories to carry much of the weekly hypertrophy volume with lower joint and systemic cost. The question isn't "how much isolation is enough" in isolation — it's "what does my total weekly volume look like for each muscle, and what's the most efficient way to get there?"

A practical approach:

1. Count your compound volume with appropriate overlap credits
2. Compare to MEV/MAV targets for each muscle
3. Add isolation work to fill the gap, prioritizing muscles that get little compound stimulus (calves, lateral delts, biceps)
4. Keep per-session isolation volume manageable — 2-4 sets per muscle per session is usually sufficient

**The mistake to avoid:** Adding isolation work on top of already-adequate compound volume, pushing total weekly sets above MRV. More is not always better, and isolation work still creates fatigue even if it's lower than compound fatigue.
