# Near-Max Deadlifting, Body Weight Defense, and Intentional Weight Loss

This question touches on several intersecting phenomena — body weight regulation, the psychological and physiological effects of heavy training, and the relationship between strength and body composition. It deserves a careful, honest answer.

## The Body Weight Regulation Question

The observation that body weight tends to stay close to deadlift max is interesting but almost certainly coincidental rather than causal. Body weight and deadlift max are both measured in kilograms/pounds, but they're not mechanistically linked in the way the question implies.

**What actually defends body weight:**

The body does have regulatory mechanisms that resist weight change — this is well-established. Adaptive thermogenesis (metabolic rate decreasing during caloric restriction), changes in hunger hormones (ghrelin rising, leptin falling), and increased appetite all work against intentional weight loss. These mechanisms are real and significant.

However, these mechanisms are driven by **energy balance and body fat levels**, not by the training stimulus itself. Heavy deadlifting doesn't signal the body to defend a particular weight. What it does do is:

1. **Build and maintain muscle mass**, which increases resting metabolic rate slightly
2. **Increase appetite**, particularly in the days following heavy training
3. **Create fatigue** that may reduce non-exercise activity (NEAT) — you move less the rest of the day after a brutal training session

The appetite increase from heavy training is real and can meaningfully interfere with weight loss efforts if not accounted for. But this is a caloric intake issue, not a body weight "set point" being defended by the training itself.

## The Binge Eating and Body Image Dimension

This part of the question is the most important and deserves direct engagement.

The listener describes developing binge-eating and body-image struggles alongside years of near-maximal lifting. This is a significant concern that goes beyond training programming. A few things worth considering:

**Heavy lifting and eating behavior:** Extreme training demands can create a psychological permission structure around eating — "I train hard, so I need to eat a lot." This isn't inherently problematic, but it can become entangled with disordered eating patterns, particularly if the eating is driven by emotional states rather than genuine hunger.

**The identity dimension:** For many serious lifters, their body weight and strength numbers become deeply tied to identity. The prospect of losing weight — which would likely reduce absolute strength numbers, at least temporarily — can feel threatening in ways that go beyond the physical. This can create genuine psychological resistance to weight loss that has nothing to do with physiology.

**Near-maximal testing and psychological stress:** Frequent 1RM testing is psychologically demanding. The chronic stress of repeatedly pushing to absolute limits, combined with the identity investment in the numbers, can create a high-stress relationship with training and body that contributes to disordered eating patterns.

## Does Weekly Near-Max Lifting Make Weight Loss Harder?

Physiologically: somewhat, primarily through appetite effects. Heavy training increases hunger, and if you're already struggling with binge eating, the appetite drive from heavy training can make restriction harder.

Psychologically: potentially significantly. If your identity is built around being strong at a certain body weight, intentional weight loss feels like self-sabotage. The binge-restrict cycle can be partly driven by this conflict.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **The eating and body image struggles are the primary issue to address**, not the training. Working with a therapist who specializes in eating disorders and/or a registered dietitian with experience in athlete populations is the most important step — more important than any training modification.

2. **Reducing 1RM testing frequency is reasonable.** The SBS framework doesn't recommend frequent maximal testing — it recommends training in the 75-85% range most of the time, with maximal testing reserved for competition or specific assessment periods. Shifting away from weekly near-max work toward submaximal training with progressive overload is both safer and more productive for long-term strength development.

3. **Weight loss during heavy training is possible but requires explicit caloric management.** The appetite effects of heavy training are real. Tracking food intake (not just "eating healthy") is usually necessary to achieve a consistent deficit.

4. **Consider whether the training structure is serving you.** Years of near-maximal work with a trainer, frequent 1RM testing, and body-image struggles suggest the current approach may not be optimal for your wellbeing. A program that prioritizes sustainable progress over maximal performance testing might serve you better.

The body weight staying near the deadlift max is almost certainly a coincidence of units, not a physiological mechanism. But the underlying struggles with eating and body image are real and worth taking seriously.
