# Resistance Curve vs. Range of Motion for Hypertrophy

This is one of the more mechanistically interesting questions in current hypertrophy research, and the honest answer is that the field is still working it out — but we have enough evidence to make practical recommendations.

## The Two Variables

**Range of motion (ROM):** How far the muscle moves through its length during the exercise. Full ROM means the muscle goes from a shortened to a lengthened position (or vice versa). Lengthened partials means performing only the portion of the movement where the muscle is at or near its longest length.

**Resistance curve:** How the load changes across the ROM. A barbell curl has a resistance curve that peaks at 90 degrees of elbow flexion (where the moment arm is longest) and drops off at both ends. A cable curl can be set up to maintain tension throughout. A machine can be designed with a cam to match the strength curve of the muscle.

## What the Evidence Shows

### Full ROM vs. Partial ROM

The traditional recommendation was always "full ROM for maximum hypertrophy," and this is still generally supported. Full ROM training produces greater hypertrophy than shortened partials in most studies.

However, the more recent and interesting finding is about **lengthened partials specifically**. Several studies (Pedrosa et al., 2022; Kassiano et al., 2023; and others) have found that training only the lengthened portion of the ROM — the bottom of a curl, the stretched position of a fly, the deep portion of a squat — produces hypertrophy equal to or sometimes greater than full ROM training, particularly in the distal portions of the muscle.

This has generated significant interest because it suggests that **mechanical tension at long muscle lengths** may be a particularly potent hypertrophic stimulus.

### Why Long-Length Loading Might Be Special

The proposed mechanisms include:

- **Titin-based force enhancement:** Titin, a giant structural protein in muscle, contributes more to force production when the muscle is stretched. This may create a unique mechanical signal.
- **Greater metabolic stress at long lengths:** Muscles working at long lengths may accumulate more metabolic byproducts.
- **Stretch-mediated hypertrophy:** Some evidence suggests that passive stretch itself can stimulate hypertrophy, independent of active contraction.

### The Resistance Curve Question

Here's where it gets nuanced: **lengthened partials only produce superior hypertrophy if the exercise actually loads the muscle at long lengths.** This is the critical caveat.

Many exercises that people assume load the stretched position actually don't. For example:

- **Dumbbell flyes:** The chest is most stretched at the bottom, but the load (gravity on the dumbbell) is also lowest there (the moment arm is shortest when the arm is perpendicular to the floor). The resistance curve works against you.
- **Cable flyes:** By adjusting the cable angle, you can maintain tension in the stretched position. Much better for loading the lengthened position.
- **Leg curls:** The hamstring is most stretched at full knee extension, and the machine typically provides good resistance there. Good for lengthened loading.
- **Romanian deadlifts:** Excellent lengthened hamstring loading.

So the answer to the question is: **resistance curve and ROM interact, and you can't separate them.** An exercise that takes you through full ROM but provides minimal resistance at the lengthened position (like a dumbbell fly) may be inferior to an exercise that loads the lengthened position well even if it doesn't go through full ROM.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Prioritize exercises that load the muscle at long lengths.** For each muscle group, identify which exercises provide meaningful resistance in the stretched position:
   - Hamstrings: RDLs, leg curls (especially lying), good mornings
   - Quads: deep squats, leg press with full depth, hack squats
   - Chest: cable flyes, low-to-high cables, incline DB press (bottom position)
   - Biceps: incline DB curls (arm behind body), cable curls with cable below
   - Triceps: overhead triceps extensions (long head stretched)

2. **Full ROM is still the default.** Unless you have a specific reason to use lengthened partials (joint pain, specific weak point), full ROM training that also loads the lengthened position is optimal.

3. **Don't assume a machine's resistance curve is optimal.** Many machines are designed for convenience, not optimal resistance curves. Evaluate each piece of equipment critically.

4. **The research is still evolving.** The lengthened partials findings are exciting but come from relatively short studies (8-12 weeks) with specific exercises. Long-term effects and generalizability across all muscles are still being established.

The bottom line: resistance curve matters enormously, and it determines whether your ROM is actually doing what you think it's doing for hypertrophy. An exercise that loads the lengthened position well is likely superior to one that doesn't, regardless of total ROM.
