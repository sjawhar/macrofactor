## Resistance Curve vs. Range of Motion: What Matters More for Hypertrophy?

This is a nuanced question because both matter, but they matter in different ways. The short answer is: range of motion is probably more important, but resistance curve is also significant.

### What These Terms Mean

**Resistance curve:** How the difficulty of an exercise changes throughout the range of motion. Some exercises have a flat curve (constant difficulty), others have a rising curve (harder at the top), others have a falling curve (harder at the bottom).

Examples:

- Barbell bench press: Rising curve (hardest at lockout)
- Leg press: Rising curve (hardest at lockout)
- Dumbbell fly: Falling curve (hardest at the bottom)
- Cable machine exercises: Often flat or rising curve

**Range of motion (ROM):** How far the muscle stretches and contracts. Full ROM means moving from full stretch to full contraction. Partial ROM means moving through only part of that range.

### Why Range of Motion Matters

Full range of motion is important for hypertrophy because:

1. **Stretch stimulus.** Muscles grow in response to being stretched under tension. Full ROM provides more stretch stimulus than partial ROM. This is why eccentric (lowering) phases are important—they provide stretch stimulus.

2. **Metabolite accumulation.** Full ROM creates more metabolite accumulation (lactate, phosphate, hydrogen ions), which is a hypertrophy signal.

3. **Mechanical tension throughout the range.** Full ROM means the muscle is under tension throughout the movement, not just at the top.

4. **Muscle fiber recruitment.** Full ROM recruits more muscle fibers, especially at the bottom of the movement where the muscle is stretched.

The research supports this: exercises performed with full ROM produce more hypertrophy than partial ROM, even with the same weight.

### Why Resistance Curve Matters

Resistance curve matters because:

1. **Mechanical tension at different points.** A rising curve (harder at the top) provides more tension at lockout, where the muscle is shortened. A falling curve (harder at the bottom) provides more tension at the stretch, where the muscle is lengthened.

2. **Weak points in the range.** If an exercise has a weak point (where the muscle is under less tension), that part of the muscle gets less stimulus. For example, the bottom of a barbell bench press is easier than the top, so the chest gets less stimulus at the bottom.

3. **Matching the strength curve.** Muscles are stronger in some positions than others. A resistance curve that matches the strength curve (harder where the muscle is stronger) allows you to use more weight throughout the range.

### Which Matters More?

**Range of motion is probably more important** because:

- The difference between full ROM and partial ROM is large (maybe 20-30% difference in hypertrophy)
- Full ROM provides stretch stimulus, which is a key hypertrophy signal
- Full ROM recruits more muscle fibers

**Resistance curve matters but is secondary** because:

- The difference between a good curve and a bad curve is smaller (maybe 10-15% difference)
- You can get good hypertrophy with a suboptimal curve if you use full ROM
- Resistance curve is more important for strength than for hypertrophy

### Practical Implications

**Prioritize full ROM.** Always use full range of motion. This is more important than optimizing the resistance curve.

**Choose exercises with good resistance curves when possible.** If you have a choice between two exercises with the same ROM, choose the one with a better curve. For example:

- Dumbbell bench press (falling curve) might be better than barbell bench press (rising curve) for chest hypertrophy because it provides more tension at the bottom
- Cable flyes (flat curve) might be better than machine flyes (rising curve) for the same reason

**Use machines strategically.** Machines often have a rising curve (harder at the top), which is suboptimal for hypertrophy. But if a machine allows full ROM, it's still useful. Don't avoid machines just because of the curve.

**Combine exercises with different curves.** A good program might include:

- A compound with a rising curve (barbell bench press) for strength
- An isolation with a falling curve (dumbbell fly) for hypertrophy
- This provides both strength stimulus and optimal hypertrophy stimulus

**Don't sacrifice ROM for curve.** If an exercise has a better curve but requires partial ROM, stick with full ROM. The ROM advantage outweighs the curve advantage.

### Example: Chest Training

**Suboptimal approach:**

- Barbell bench press 4x5 (rising curve, full ROM)
- Machine chest press 3x10 (rising curve, full ROM)
- Total: Rising curve throughout, limited hypertrophy stimulus at the bottom

**Better approach:**

- Barbell bench press 4x5 (rising curve, full ROM) — for strength
- Dumbbell bench press 3x8 (falling curve, full ROM) — for hypertrophy
- Dumbbell fly 3x10 (falling curve, full ROM) — for isolation and stretch stimulus
- Total: Mix of curves, full ROM throughout, good strength and hypertrophy stimulus

### The Research

Studies comparing exercises with different resistance curves show:

- Full ROM always beats partial ROM for hypertrophy
- Exercises with a falling curve (harder at the bottom) produce slightly more hypertrophy than exercises with a rising curve
- The difference is modest (10-15%) compared to the difference between full and partial ROM (20-30%)

### Bottom Line

For hypertrophy, range of motion is more important than resistance curve. Always use full ROM. When choosing between exercises, prefer those with a falling or flat curve over a rising curve, but don't sacrifice ROM to get a better curve. A good program combines exercises with different curves to provide both strength stimulus (rising curve) and hypertrophy stimulus (falling curve), all with full range of motion.
