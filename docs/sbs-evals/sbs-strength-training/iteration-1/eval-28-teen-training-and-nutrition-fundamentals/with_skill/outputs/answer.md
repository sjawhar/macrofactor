# Training and Nutrition for a 15-Year-Old: Building Muscle and Strength

A 15-year-old is in a genuinely advantageous position for building muscle and strength — hormonal environment is favorable, motor learning is rapid, and the training age is zero, meaning almost any consistent stimulus will produce results. The goal is to build good habits and a solid foundation without overdoing it.

## Exercise Selection

At 15, the priority is learning movement patterns well, not maximizing load. The exercise menu should be simple and focused on fundamental patterns:

**Primary movements to learn:**

- **Squat pattern:** Goblet squat → barbell back squat or front squat. Start with goblet squats to learn the pattern before adding a barbell.
- **Hinge pattern:** Romanian deadlift → conventional deadlift. RDLs teach the hip hinge safely before loading a full deadlift.
- **Horizontal push:** Push-ups → dumbbell bench press → barbell bench press
- **Horizontal pull:** Dumbbell rows, cable rows, machine rows
- **Vertical pull:** Lat pulldowns → pull-ups/chin-ups as strength develops
- **Vertical push:** Dumbbell overhead press

Avoid exercises that require significant technical skill before the basics are solid. No need for Olympic lifts, heavy barbell good mornings, or complex variations early on.

## Weekly Volume and Frequency

A 15-year-old is a beginner by definition. The SBS framework for beginners emphasizes:

- **2-4 training sessions per week** — 3 full-body sessions is ideal
- **2-4 sets per exercise, 2-3 exercises per pattern per week**
- **Total weekly sets per muscle group: 8-12** is plenty — beginners don't need high volume to progress

A simple 3-day full-body program works extremely well:

- **Day 1:** Squat, bench press, row, 2-3 accessories
- **Day 3:** Deadlift, overhead press, pull-down/pull-up, 2-3 accessories
- **Day 5:** Squat variant, bench variant, row variant, 2-3 accessories

Each session should take 45-60 minutes. More than this is unnecessary and may interfere with recovery and school/life demands.

## Reps, Intensity, and Proximity to Failure

For a 15-year-old, **stay well away from failure**. The SBS framework recommends beginners keep 3+ RIR (reps in reserve) — meaning stop each set when you could still do 3 more reps with good form.

**Rep ranges:**

- Main compound lifts: 5-8 reps at 60-75% of estimated max
- Accessory exercises: 8-15 reps

**Why avoid failure:**

1. Technique breaks down near failure, and at 15 you're still grooving movement patterns
2. The neural and hormonal response to training is so robust at this age that you don't need to push hard to get results
3. Injury risk from grinding near-maximal reps is real and not worth it

Progress by adding small amounts of weight (2.5 kg on compounds, 1-2 kg on accessories) when all sets feel easy. This is linear progression and it works extremely well for beginners.

## Calories and Macros

At 15, the body is already in a growth phase. The nutritional priorities are:

**Calories:** A 15-year-old who is training consistently should be eating enough to support both growth and training. This is not the time for caloric restriction. A rough starting point is eating until satisfied at each meal, with an emphasis on whole foods. If weight isn't increasing slowly (0.5-1 kg/month is reasonable for a lean bulk), eat more.

**Protein:** This is the most important macronutrient for muscle building. Target **1.6-2.0 g per kg of body weight per day**. For a 60 kg teenager, that's 96-120 g of protein daily. This is achievable through regular meals with protein sources (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes) without needing supplements.

**Carbohydrates:** Don't restrict carbs. Carbohydrates fuel training and support recovery. Whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and starches should make up a large portion of the diet.

**Supplements:** At 15, supplements are largely unnecessary. Creatine monohydrate (3-5 g/day) has a strong safety and efficacy record and is worth considering, but it's not essential. Protein powder is a convenient food, not a magic supplement — use it if whole food protein is hard to hit, not as a replacement for real meals.

## The Most Important Thing

Consistency over months and years matters far more than any specific program detail. A 15-year-old who trains 3 days a week, eats enough protein, and sleeps 8-9 hours will make remarkable progress. The biggest mistake is overcomplicating it or trying to train like an advanced athlete before the foundation is built.
