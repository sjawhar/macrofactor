# Are Lateral Raises Enough for Side Delts, or Do You Need Overhead Pressing Too?

The short answer: lateral raises are the most direct stimulus for the lateral (medial) head of the deltoid, and for pure hypertrophy of that specific head, they're probably your best tool. But the full picture is more nuanced than "just do laterals."

## Understanding Delt Anatomy and Exercise Mechanics

The deltoid has three heads: anterior (front), lateral (side), and posterior (rear). Overhead pressing — whether barbell, dumbbell, or machine — is primarily an anterior delt exercise. The lateral head gets some work from pressing, but it's not the primary mover. Upright rows hit the lateral head more directly than pressing does, but they also involve the traps and can be uncomfortable for some people's shoulders.

Lateral raises, by contrast, put the lateral delt in a mechanically advantaged position for the specific abduction movement it's designed to perform. They're the most targeted isolation exercise for that head.

## The Case for Lateral Raises as Your Primary Tool

From an exercise selection standpoint, the SBS framework emphasizes choosing movements that:

- Build the target muscle specifically
- Can be progressed consistently over time
- Have a manageable fatigue cost relative to the stimulus they provide

Lateral raises check all three boxes for the lateral delt. They're low-fatigue, highly specific, and easy to progress with double progression (e.g., 3 sets of 12–20 reps, add reps until you hit the top of the range, then add a small load increment).

The lateral delt's MEV sits around 8–10 sets per week, with a MAV of 12–20 sets. Lateral raises can easily supply all of that volume without the systemic fatigue cost of heavy overhead pressing.

## What Overhead Pressing Actually Does

Overhead pressing is valuable — but primarily for:

1. **Anterior delt development** (the front head gets the most work)
2. **Shoulder girdle strength and stability** (pressing builds the whole shoulder complex)
3. **Triceps and upper chest involvement**
4. **Functional overhead strength** (useful for athletes and general fitness)

If your goal is specifically to build wider-looking shoulders (the "capped" look), overhead pressing alone will disappoint you. The anterior delt is already heavily stimulated by bench pressing, so most people's front delts are not the limiting factor in shoulder width.

## Upright Rows: Worth Including?

Upright rows do hit the lateral delt more directly than pressing, and some people find them a useful addition. The concern is that wide-grip upright rows can cause shoulder impingement in some individuals. If they feel fine for you, they're a reasonable complement to lateral raises. If they cause discomfort, skip them — the lateral raise covers the same muscle without the risk.

## Practical Recommendation

For most people focused on side delt development:

- **Make lateral raises your primary side delt exercise.** Aim for 12–20 sets per week distributed across 2–4 sessions. Cable laterals or machine laterals are often preferable to dumbbells because they maintain tension throughout the full range of motion.
- **Keep overhead pressing in your program** for overall shoulder health, anterior delt work, and pressing strength — but don't count on it to build your lateral delts.
- **Upright rows are optional.** Include them if you enjoy them and they don't bother your shoulders.

The mistake many people make is assuming that because overhead pressing is a "bigger" exercise, it must be doing more for shoulder development. For the lateral head specifically, it's not. Lateral raises are the right tool for the job.

One practical note: the lateral delt responds well to higher rep ranges (15–30 reps) and higher frequencies (3–5x/week) compared to larger muscle groups. Don't be afraid to do a few sets of laterals multiple times per week — the recovery cost is low enough to support it.
