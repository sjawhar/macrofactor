# Improving Science Writing and Getting Published on Larger Fitness Websites

This is a craft question as much as a strategy question. The good news is that both skills — writing clearly about science and getting published — are learnable and follow predictable patterns.

## Improving Your Science Writing

**Read widely and analytically.** The best way to improve is to read a lot of good science writing and ask yourself _why_ it works. Sites like Stronger By Science, Examine.com, and Renaissance Periodization publish evidence-based content at varying levels of technical depth. Notice how they structure arguments, how they handle uncertainty, and how they translate study findings into practical recommendations.

**Lead with the practical, support with the evidence.** A common mistake in science writing is burying the actionable takeaway under layers of methodology. Most readers want to know what to do and why — not a full literature review. State your conclusion early, then use the evidence to justify it.

**Be precise about uncertainty.** Good science writing distinguishes between "the evidence strongly suggests X," "one study found X but replication is limited," and "we don't know." Overclaiming damages credibility; appropriate hedging builds it. This is especially important in fitness, where the literature is often underpowered and effect sizes are modest.

**Explain mechanisms, not just findings.** Readers retain information better when they understand _why_ something works, not just _that_ it works. If you're writing about protein timing, explain the underlying physiology — muscle protein synthesis, leucine thresholds, etc. — rather than just citing a study.

**Edit ruthlessly.** Science writing often suffers from passive voice, jargon, and unnecessary complexity. After drafting, ask: can I say this more simply? Does every sentence earn its place?

**Practice with a blog or newsletter.** Writing regularly — even without an audience — builds the habit and gives you a portfolio. Substack, Ghost, and similar platforms make this easy.

## Getting Published on Larger Websites

**Start smaller and build a portfolio.** Most large fitness publications (T-Nation, BarBend, Breaking Muscle, Stronger By Science) receive many pitches. Having published work — even on smaller sites or your own platform — demonstrates you can write and follow through.

**Study the publication's style and audience.** Before pitching, read 10–20 articles from the target site. Understand their tone (technical vs. accessible), their typical article length, and what topics they've covered recently. Pitch something that fits their voice and fills a gap.

**Write a strong pitch, not just a topic idea.** A good pitch includes: the specific angle you're taking, why it's relevant to their audience now, a brief outline, and a sample of your writing. "I want to write about protein" is not a pitch. "I want to write a 1,500-word piece on why the leucine threshold model explains why protein distribution matters more than total daily intake, with practical meal-planning implications" is a pitch.

**Offer to write on spec for your first piece.** For a first submission to a new publication, offering to write the full piece before they commit reduces their risk and demonstrates confidence in your work.

**Build relationships.** Follow editors and writers on social media, engage thoughtfully with their content, and make yourself a known quantity before cold-pitching. Many opportunities come through relationships, not cold outreach.

**Be persistent and thick-skinned.** Rejection is normal. Most successful writers have been rejected many times. Treat each rejection as information — sometimes it's about fit, not quality — and keep submitting.

## The Long Game

The fitness science writing space rewards consistency and credibility over time. Building a reputation for accuracy, nuance, and practical utility will open doors that cold pitching alone won't. Focus on being genuinely useful to readers, and the publication opportunities tend to follow.
