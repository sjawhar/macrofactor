# What to Do the Week After a Meet

This question asks for a personal approach to post-competition recovery, which is inherently individual — but there are well-supported principles that most experienced competitive lifters converge on.

## The General Principle: Don't Rush Back

The week immediately after a meet is not the time to start your next training block. A powerlifting competition — especially after a full peaking cycle — represents a significant accumulation of fatigue: weeks of high-intensity training, the psychological stress of competition, the physical demands of maximal attempts, and often disrupted sleep and travel. Jumping straight back into structured training before that fatigue clears is a recipe for poor quality work and potentially setting yourself up for injury or burnout.

## A Reasonable Post-Meet Week

**Days 1–3: Full rest or very light activity**

Most experienced lifters take at least 2–3 days completely off after a meet. This isn't laziness — it's allowing the acute fatigue from the competition itself to dissipate. Light walking, mobility work, or recreational activity is fine. Structured lifting is not.

**Days 4–7: Optional light movement**

If you feel good and want to move, a light session with very low intensity (50–60% of normal loads, staying 4–5 RIR from failure) can help with blood flow and psychological readiness without adding meaningful fatigue. Keep it short, keep it easy, and don't treat it as training — treat it as active recovery.

## The Deload Framing

From an SBS perspective, the post-meet week functions as a deload: reduce volume by 30–50%, reduce intensity by 5–10%, keep 3–5 RIR, and maintain movement patterns without accumulating new fatigue. The goal is to restore readiness, not to maintain fitness — fitness is not going anywhere in one week.

## Individual Variation Matters

How long you need before returning to productive training depends on:

- **How hard the peaking block was** — a 12-week peak with multiple near-maximal sessions requires more recovery than a shorter, lower-volume peak
- **How the meet went** — a meet where you hit all nine attempts and felt great is less draining than one where you missed lifts, had to make weight aggressively, or dealt with unexpected stressors
- **Your training age** — more experienced lifters often have better-developed recovery capacity but also push harder in competition
- **Life stress** — if work, family, or other demands are high, recovery takes longer

## The Return to Training

Most competitive lifters return to structured training 1–2 weeks after a meet. The first block back is typically a hypertrophy or general strength phase — not another peaking block. This is the right time to address weak points, build muscle, and let the competitive-specific fatigue fully clear before ramping intensity again.

## Bottom Line

The week after a meet should be mostly rest with optional light movement. Don't try to maintain fitness or start the next block immediately. The fatigue from a full peaking cycle and competition is real, and clearing it properly sets you up for a more productive next training phase. One week of rest will not cost you meaningful fitness — but rushing back before you're recovered can cost you weeks of productive training down the line.
