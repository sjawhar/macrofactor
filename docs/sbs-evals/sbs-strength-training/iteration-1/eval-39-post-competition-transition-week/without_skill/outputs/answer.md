# Post-Competition Recovery: Deload vs Immediate Training

The optimal approach after a competition depends on several factors, but the general principle is: **take a brief deload, then transition into your next training block.**

## Why a Deload Matters

After a competition (especially a powerlifting meet), your body is in a state of accumulated fatigue:

- **CNS fatigue** - Heavy competition lifts create significant neural fatigue
- **Accumulated volume** - Meet prep typically involves high volume leading into the competition
- **Systemic fatigue** - Stress hormones are elevated, recovery capacity is reduced
- **Tissue damage** - Muscles, joints, and connective tissue have accumulated microtrauma

Jumping immediately into another high-intensity block without recovery increases injury risk and reduces adaptation quality.

## Recommended Approach

**Week 1 (Post-Meet Deload):**

- Reduce volume by 50-60%
- Reduce intensity (RPE 5-6 instead of 8-9)
- Focus on movement quality and technique
- Include mobility work, stretching, and recovery modalities
- Maintain some training stimulus to prevent detraining, but prioritize recovery

**Week 2-3 (Transition):**

- Gradually increase volume and intensity
- Begin introducing the training style of your next block
- If moving to hypertrophy, gradually increase reps and volume
- If staying strength-focused, gradually increase intensity

**Week 4+ (New Block):**

- Full training intensity and volume for your next mesocycle

## Individual Variation

Some athletes prefer:

- **Complete rest** (3-7 days) - Useful if extremely fatigued or injured
- **Active recovery only** (light walking, stretching, mobility)
- **Immediate transition** - Some athletes feel better jumping into new training

The best approach depends on:

- How fatigued you feel
- Whether you have any injuries or pain
- Your psychological state (burnout vs. motivation)
- Your next training goal

## Practical Recommendation

A 1-week deload is the safest default. It allows recovery while maintaining training habit and preventing detraining. If you feel great and recovered, you can shorten it to 3-4 days. If you're beat up or injured, take a full week or more.

The key is listening to your body. If you're still sore, fatigued, or unmotivated, extend the deload. If you're feeling good, transition sooner. There's no universal "right" answer—context matters.
