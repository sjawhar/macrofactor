# How to Troubleshoot a Program Before Switching Entirely

Program-hopping is one of the most common mistakes in strength training. Most programs that "mostly work" can be fixed with targeted adjustments — and switching programs entirely often just resets the clock without addressing the underlying issue. Here's a systematic approach.

## Step 1: Diagnose Before You Change Anything

Before tweaking anything, identify what specifically isn't working. "The program isn't working" is too vague to act on. Be specific:

- Which lifts are stalling?
- Is performance declining, flat, or just progressing slower than expected?
- Are you recovering between sessions (soreness, fatigue, motivation)?
- Has anything changed outside the gym (sleep, stress, nutrition, body weight)?

The SBS decision ladder is useful here: if performance is down, check recovery basics first — sleep, stress, calories, protein. Many "program problems" are actually lifestyle problems in disguise.

## Step 2: Check Recovery Variables First

Before changing the program, verify:

- **Sleep:** 7–9 hours of quality sleep is a prerequisite for adaptation. If this is compromised, no program adjustment will fully compensate.
- **Calories:** Are you eating enough to support your training? Chronic undereating suppresses recovery and adaptation.
- **Protein:** ~0.7–1g per pound of body weight is the practical target for most people.
- **Life stress:** High external stress competes with training stress for recovery resources.

If any of these are off, fix them before changing the program.

## Step 3: Assess Volume — Too Much or Too Little?

Volume is the primary driver of hypertrophy and a key variable for strength. Two failure modes:

**Below MEV (minimum effective volume):** If you're doing fewer than 8–10 hard sets per week for a muscle group, you may simply not be doing enough to drive adaptation. Add 2–4 sets per week for the lagging muscle or lift.

**Above MRV (maximum recoverable volume):** If performance is declining despite adequate recovery basics, you may be doing too much. Reduce volume by 20–40% for 1–2 weeks and see if performance rebounds. If it does, you were above your MRV.

## Step 4: Adjust the Progression Model

If volume is appropriate and recovery is solid but progress has stalled, the progression model may be the issue:

- **Still on linear progression?** If you've been training for more than a few months and can no longer add weight session-to-session, you've outgrown linear progression. Move to wave loading or block periodization.
- **Waves too short or too long?** Most intermediates need 4–8 week waves. If you're trying to PR every 2 weeks, that's too frequent. If you haven't had a PR in 12+ weeks, the wave may be too long or the loading scheme may be off.
- **Double progression stalling on accessories?** If you've been stuck at the same weight for 3+ weeks on an accessory, add a small load increment and accept slightly lower reps temporarily.

## Step 5: Address Weak Points With Exercise Variants

If a specific lift is stalling, consider whether a weak point in the movement is the limiting factor:

- Bench stalling at lockout → add more triceps work (close-grip bench, triceps extensions)
- Squat stalling out of the hole → pause squats, front squats
- Deadlift stalling at the knee → deficit deadlifts, Romanian deadlifts for hamstring strength

Changing to a close variant for 4–8 weeks can break a plateau without abandoning the movement pattern entirely.

## When to Actually Switch Programs

Switching programs is warranted when:

- The program's structure is fundamentally mismatched to your current level (e.g., still running a beginner program as an intermediate)
- The program doesn't include the movements or volume needed for your specific goals
- You've made all reasonable adjustments and progress has been absent for 8–12+ weeks

Even then, the new program should be chosen based on what the old one was missing — not just novelty.

## Bottom Line

Most program problems are either recovery problems or volume/progression problems. Work through the diagnostic sequence before switching: check lifestyle variables, assess volume relative to MEV/MRV, adjust the progression model, and target weak points. Switching programs should be a last resort, not a first response to a plateau.
