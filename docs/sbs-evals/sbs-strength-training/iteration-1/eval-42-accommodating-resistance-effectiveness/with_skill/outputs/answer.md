# Bands and Chains for Absolute Strength: Do They Actually Work?

The theoretical rationale for accommodating resistance is sound, and the evidence suggests it can be a useful tool — but with important caveats about who benefits most and how it should be implemented.

## The Theory

Bands and chains add resistance that increases as you move through the concentric phase of a lift. The idea is to match the resistance curve to the strength curve: most lifters are weakest at the bottom of a squat or bench press and strongest at lockout. Standard barbells provide constant resistance, meaning the load that challenges you at the bottom is relatively easy at the top. Accommodating resistance theoretically allows you to train the full strength curve more effectively — heavier at lockout, lighter at the bottom — and may also train rate of force development by requiring you to accelerate through the entire range of motion.

## What the Research Shows

The evidence is moderately supportive but not overwhelming:

**Positive findings:** Several studies and meta-analyses have found that adding bands or chains to barbell training produces greater strength gains than straight weight alone, particularly in trained lifters. A commonly cited meta-analysis found that accommodating resistance produced meaningfully larger strength improvements compared to free weights alone when used as a supplement to standard training.

**Important nuances:**

- Most of the research is in intermediate-to-advanced lifters. Beginners don't appear to benefit more from accommodating resistance than from standard progressive overload — and the added complexity may be a distraction from the fundamentals.
- The effect sizes are real but modest. Accommodating resistance is not a magic tool; it's a marginal enhancement for people who have already optimized the basics.
- Implementation matters. Using too much band/chain tension (more than ~25–35% of total load) can compromise technique and reduce the training stimulus at the bottom of the lift. Most practitioners recommend keeping accommodating resistance at 20–30% of total load.

## Practical Considerations

**Who benefits most:** Intermediate and advanced lifters who have already maximized gains from standard progressive overload and are looking for additional tools. Powerlifters specifically may benefit from the carryover to competition performance, since the strength curve of competition lifts is what accommodating resistance is designed to address.

**Who probably doesn't need it:** Beginners and early intermediates. The fundamentals — progressive overload, adequate volume, good technique, sufficient recovery — will produce far more progress than any specialized tool. Adding bands and chains before mastering these basics is putting the cart before the horse.

**How to implement:** Use bands or chains as a supplement to, not a replacement for, standard barbell work. A common approach is to use accommodating resistance on 1–2 sessions per week while keeping at least one session of straight weight to maintain feel for standard loading. Keep band/chain tension at 20–30% of total load.

**Practical barriers:** Bands and chains require equipment and setup time. Chains in particular are expensive and not available in most commercial gyms. Bands are more accessible but require anchoring points and can be awkward to set up consistently.

## The Bottom Line

The logic is sound and the evidence is real — accommodating resistance can enhance strength development, particularly for intermediate and advanced lifters. But it's a refinement, not a foundation. If you're already doing the basics well (progressive overload, adequate volume, good technique, sufficient recovery), adding bands or chains is a reasonable tool to experiment with. If you're not yet doing the basics consistently, that's where your attention should go first.
