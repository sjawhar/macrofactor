# Bands and Chains for Building Absolute Strength

Bands and chains (accommodating resistance) are effective tools for strength development, but they're not superior to traditional free weights—they're complementary tools with specific applications.

## How Accommodating Resistance Works

Bands and chains increase resistance as you move through the range of motion:

- **Bands** - Tension increases as the band stretches
- **Chains** - Weight increases as more chain leaves the ground

This creates a strength curve that matches human biomechanics: you're weakest at the bottom and strongest at the top. Accommodating resistance increases load where you're strongest, potentially increasing strength development.

## Effectiveness for Strength

Research shows accommodating resistance can be effective for strength, but with important caveats:

**Advantages:**

- Increased load in the strong range (top of lift)
- Reduced joint stress at the bottom (less load where you're weak)
- Novelty stimulus that can break plateaus
- Useful for accommodating strength curves (e.g., weak lockout)

**Disadvantages:**

- Less mechanical tension at the bottom (where hypertrophy stimulus is greatest)
- Reduced range of motion stimulus
- More complex to set up and adjust
- Not necessarily superior to traditional free weights for overall strength

## What the Literature Says

Studies comparing accommodating resistance to traditional free weights show:

- **Similar strength gains** - When volume is equated, bands/chains produce similar strength gains to free weights
- **Specific applications** - Accommodating resistance is useful for addressing weak points (e.g., lockout weakness)
- **Not a magic tool** - Accommodating resistance doesn't produce superior results to well-designed free weight training

The key finding: accommodating resistance is effective, but not inherently better than traditional methods. It's a tool for specific purposes, not a replacement for free weights.

## Practical Applications

Accommodating resistance is most useful for:

1. **Addressing weak points** - If your lockout is weak, chains on the bench press can help
2. **Reducing joint stress** - Bands reduce bottom-position stress while maintaining top-position load
3. **Breaking plateaus** - Novelty stimulus can restart progress
4. **Accommodating strength curves** - Matching resistance to your strength curve

## Recommendation

Bands and chains are useful tools, but they shouldn't replace traditional free weight training. A practical approach:

- **Primary training** - Use free weights (barbells, dumbbells) for main strength work
- **Supplemental work** - Add bands/chains for weak points or as a variation
- **Frequency** - Use accommodating resistance 1-2x per week, not as your primary stimulus

The best strength training combines multiple tools: free weights, machines, bands, and chains. Accommodating resistance is one tool in the toolbox, not the solution to strength development.
