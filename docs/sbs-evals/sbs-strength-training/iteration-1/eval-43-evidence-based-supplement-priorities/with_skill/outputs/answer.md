# Evidence-Based Supplements: What Works and What Doesn't

The supplement industry is enormous and largely built on marketing rather than evidence. Here's an honest breakdown of what the research actually supports.

## Tier 1: Strong Evidence, Meaningful Effect

**Creatine monohydrate** is the most well-supported performance supplement in existence. Hundreds of studies consistently show it increases phosphocreatine availability, improving performance in short, high-intensity efforts (strength training, sprinting). It also appears to support muscle protein synthesis and may have cognitive benefits. The effect size is real and meaningful — roughly 5–15% improvement in strength and power output over time. Dose: 3–5g/day. No need to load. Monohydrate is as effective as any other form and far cheaper.

**Caffeine** has robust evidence for improving strength, power, endurance, and perceived effort. It works by blocking adenosine receptors, reducing perceived fatigue. Effect sizes are meaningful, particularly for trained individuals. Dose: 3–6mg/kg body weight, taken 30–60 minutes before training. Tolerance develops with regular use, so some people cycle it or reserve it for key sessions. Individual response varies significantly.

**Protein supplements (whey, casein, plant-based)** are not magic — they're just food. But if you're struggling to hit adequate protein intake (~0.7–1g/lb body weight) through whole foods, protein powder is a convenient and cost-effective way to close the gap. The evidence for protein's role in muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy is overwhelming. The supplement itself isn't special; the protein content is.

## Tier 2: Moderate Evidence, Situational Benefit

**Beta-alanine** buffers hydrogen ions during high-intensity exercise, delaying fatigue in efforts lasting 1–4 minutes. The evidence is solid for endurance-type efforts and high-rep training. Less relevant for pure strength work (sets of 1–5 reps). Causes harmless tingling (paresthesia) in some people. Dose: 3.2–6.4g/day, split to reduce tingling.

**Citrulline malate** may improve blood flow and reduce fatigue during training. Some evidence for improved performance and reduced soreness. Effect sizes are smaller than creatine or caffeine. Dose: 6–8g of citrulline malate, taken pre-workout.

**Vitamin D** — if you're deficient (common in people who spend most time indoors or live at northern latitudes), supplementing can improve muscle function, immune health, and mood. If you're not deficient, supplementing doesn't appear to provide additional benefit. Worth getting blood levels checked.

**Fish oil (omega-3s)** has evidence for reducing inflammation, supporting cardiovascular health, and potentially modest benefits for muscle protein synthesis. Not a performance supplement per se, but a reasonable general health supplement if dietary fish intake is low.

## Tier 3: Weak or Inconsistent Evidence

**BCAAs:** If you're already hitting adequate total protein intake, BCAAs provide no additional benefit. The leucine in your protein is doing the same job. BCAAs are essentially expensive protein fragments.

**Pre-workout blends:** Usually a combination of caffeine (which works), beta-alanine (which works situationally), and a bunch of other ingredients at sub-effective doses. You're mostly paying for caffeine and marketing. Better to buy caffeine and creatine separately.

**Glutamine:** No meaningful evidence for muscle gain or recovery in people eating adequate protein.

**Testosterone boosters:** Essentially no evidence for meaningful effects in healthy individuals. The ingredients (zinc, D-aspartic acid, tribulus, etc.) either don't work or work only in deficient populations.

**HMB:** Some evidence in untrained individuals, minimal evidence in trained lifters.

## The Honest Summary

The supplement industry is worth billions, but the list of supplements with strong evidence for meaningful effects is short: creatine, caffeine, and adequate protein. Everything else is either situational, weakly supported, or outright ineffective. Start with the basics — training, nutrition, sleep — and add creatine and caffeine if you want to optimize. The rest is largely noise.
