# Protein Intake: Downsides, Optimal Targets, and Whether to Go Higher

This is a question with a clear evidence-based answer for most of it, and some genuine uncertainty at the margins.

## Is ~1g/lb Enough for Building Muscle?

Yes, for most people. The research on protein and muscle protein synthesis converges on a practical target of approximately **0.7–1g per pound of body weight per day** (roughly 1.6–2.2g/kg) as sufficient to maximize muscle protein synthesis in most training contexts. Studies that have pushed protein intake higher — to 1.5g/lb or beyond — generally don't find additional hypertrophy benefits in people who are already hitting the 0.7–1g/lb range.

The 1g/lb figure is a convenient, slightly conservative target that's easy to remember and ensures most people are in the optimal range. It's not a magic number, but it's a reasonable practical target.

## Should You Go Higher to Maximize Gains?

Probably not, for most people. The dose-response curve for protein and muscle protein synthesis appears to plateau somewhere around 1.6–2.2g/kg (0.7–1g/lb). Going significantly above this doesn't appear to produce meaningfully more muscle growth in controlled studies.

There are some situations where higher protein intake may be warranted:

- **Aggressive caloric restriction (cutting):** Higher protein (up to 1.2–1.5g/lb) during a cut helps preserve lean mass when calories are low. The muscle-sparing effect of protein is more pronounced in a deficit.
- **Very high training volumes:** Some evidence suggests that very high training volumes may increase protein requirements modestly, though the effect is not large.
- **Older adults:** Protein requirements for muscle protein synthesis appear to be somewhat higher in older individuals (50+), who show blunted anabolic responses to lower protein doses.

## Are There Downsides to High Protein Intake?

For healthy individuals, high protein intake is generally safe. The concerns you may have heard — kidney damage, bone loss — are largely myths or apply only to specific populations:

**Kidney health:** High protein intake does increase the filtration load on the kidneys, but in people with healthy kidney function, this does not appear to cause damage or accelerate decline. The concern is legitimate only for people with pre-existing kidney disease, for whom protein restriction may be medically indicated.

**Bone health:** Early concerns that high protein increased calcium excretion and harmed bone density have not been supported by the broader evidence base. If anything, adequate protein appears to support bone health.

**Cardiovascular risk:** This depends heavily on the protein sources. High intake of red and processed meat is associated with cardiovascular risk, but this is likely due to saturated fat and other factors rather than protein per se. Protein from lean meats, dairy, eggs, legumes, and fish does not appear to carry the same risk.

**Practical downsides:** Very high protein intake can crowd out other macronutrients (carbohydrates and fats) that also serve important functions. It can also be expensive and, for some people, difficult to hit without relying heavily on supplements. And some people find very high protein diets less palatable or harder to sustain.

## The Practical Recommendation

For most people training for hypertrophy or strength:

- **Target 0.7–1g/lb (1.6–2.2g/kg) of body weight per day**
- **Distribute across 3–5 meals** to maximize muscle protein synthesis throughout the day (each meal should contain ~30–50g of protein to reliably trigger a maximal synthetic response)
- **Prioritize high-quality sources** (meat, fish, dairy, eggs, legumes)
- **Going higher is not harmful but also not necessary** — the extra calories are better spent on carbohydrates for training performance

If you're already hitting 1g/lb consistently, you're in the optimal range. Pushing to 1.5g/lb won't hurt you, but it's unlikely to meaningfully accelerate your gains.
