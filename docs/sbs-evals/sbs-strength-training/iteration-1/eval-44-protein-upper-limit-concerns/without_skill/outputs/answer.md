# Protein Intake: Upper Limits and Muscle Building Requirements

The common recommendation of ~1g per pound of bodyweight is reasonable, but there's no evidence of harm from higher protein intake, and the minimum effective dose is lower than many believe.

## Minimum Protein for Muscle Building

Research consistently shows that **0.7-0.8g per pound of bodyweight** is sufficient for muscle growth in resistance-trained individuals. This is lower than the popular 1g per pound recommendation.

Key studies:

- Schoenfeld et al. (2017) meta-analysis found that protein intake above 0.7-0.8g per lb showed no additional benefit for hypertrophy
- Helms et al. (2014) recommended 0.7-1.0g per lb as optimal
- Individual variation exists, but 0.7g per lb is adequate for most people

## Is 1g Per Pound Necessary?

No, but it's not harmful either. The 1g per pound recommendation is:

- **Safe** - No evidence of harm from high protein intake in healthy individuals
- **Practical** - Easy to remember and implement
- **Slightly excessive** - More than the minimum, but the extra protein isn't wasted

If you're eating 1g per pound and making progress, there's no reason to reduce it. The extra protein simply provides a margin of safety and may help with satiety during a cut.

## Downsides of Very High Protein

**Potential concerns (mostly minor):**

1. **Kidney stress** - Myth for healthy individuals. High protein doesn't damage kidneys in people with normal kidney function. People with kidney disease should be cautious, but healthy athletes are fine.

2. **Liver stress** - No evidence of harm in healthy individuals

3. **Dehydration** - High protein requires adequate water intake, but this is easily managed

4. **Cost** - High protein intake is more expensive than lower protein

5. **Satiety** - Very high protein (>1.2g per lb) may cause excessive fullness, making it hard to eat enough calories

## Practical Recommendations

**For muscle building:**

- Minimum: 0.7g per pound
- Optimal: 0.8-1.0g per pound
- Safe upper range: 1.2-1.5g per pound

**For fat loss:**

- Higher protein (1.0-1.2g per pound) is beneficial for preserving muscle and increasing satiety
- Protein helps you feel fuller longer, making calorie restriction easier

**Individual variation:**

- Some people do well at 0.7g per pound
- Others prefer higher protein for satiety
- Experiment and find what works for you

## Bottom Line

The 1g per pound recommendation is safe and effective, though slightly higher than the minimum. There's no evidence of harm from higher protein intake in healthy individuals. If you're making progress and feeling good, your current protein intake is fine. Don't worry about exceeding 1g per pound—it's not harmful, just potentially unnecessary.

The bigger factors for muscle growth are:

- Total calories (slight surplus)
- Training volume and intensity
- Progressive overload
- Sleep and recovery

Protein is important, but it's not the limiting factor for most people eating 0.7g+ per pound.
