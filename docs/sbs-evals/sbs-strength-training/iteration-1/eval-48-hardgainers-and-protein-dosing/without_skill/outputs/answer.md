# Hardgainers, Protein Targets, and Sex Differences

The term "hardgainer" is controversial in sports science, but the phenomenon is real: some people gain muscle more slowly than others despite similar training and nutrition. The protein question is nuanced, and sex differences add important context.

## Do Hardgainers Need More Protein?

The standard recommendation is 0.7–1.0 g/lb bodyweight daily for muscle growth. Research suggests this is sufficient for most people, but hardgainers might benefit from the upper end or slightly beyond:

**The case for higher protein**: Hardgainers often have lower baseline muscle protein synthesis rates or higher protein turnover. Some evidence suggests they may have suboptimal amino acid utilization or higher protein requirements. A few studies hint that hardgainers might respond better to protein intakes at 1.0–1.2 g/lb rather than 0.7 g/lb.

**The practical reality**: Most "hardgainers" aren't actually eating enough total calories or protein. When calories and protein are truly optimized, the hardgainer label often disappears. Increasing protein from 0.7 to 1.0+ g/lb is a reasonable strategy if progress stalls, but it's rarely the primary limiting factor.

**Genetic factors**: True hardgainers likely have genetic variations affecting myostatin, IGF-1 signaling, or muscle fiber type distribution. These are real but rare. Most people labeled "hardgainers" simply haven't optimized training volume, intensity, or recovery.

## Hardgainers and Female Athletes

This is where the evidence becomes sparse. Female hardgainers are understudied, but we can infer from general female physiology:

- Women typically have lower baseline testosterone, which may slow muscle protein synthesis
- Women may have slightly higher protein requirements (some evidence suggests 0.8–1.1 g/lb for women vs. 0.7–1.0 for men)
- Female athletes often undereat relative to training demands, making protein targets harder to hit
- Menstrual cycle disruption (common in female athletes) impairs muscle growth independent of protein intake

**For female hardgainers**: Prioritize adequate total calories first, then ensure protein at 0.8–1.0+ g/lb. The evidence for exceeding 1.0 g/lb is weak for both sexes, but the upper range is reasonable if progress stalls.

## Bottom Line

Exceeding typical protein targets (0.7–1.0 g/lb) for hardgainers is a reasonable experiment, but it's rarely the primary solution. Ensure training volume, intensity, and total calories are optimized first. For female hardgainers, the same principles apply, with attention to menstrual health and total energy availability.
