# Is Bench Press the Best Measure of Raw Strength?

The bench press is a popular strength metric, but it's not the best single measure of raw strength. The answer depends on what you mean by "raw strength" and what you're trying to measure.

## Why Bench Press Is Overrated as a Strength Metric

1. **Upper body only**: Bench press measures pressing strength in the horizontal plane. It tells you nothing about lower body strength, pulling strength, or core strength.

2. **Technique-dependent**: Bench press performance is heavily influenced by limb length, shoulder mobility, and technique. Two people with identical "raw strength" might have very different bench press maxes.

3. **Narrow movement pattern**: It's a single-joint-dominant movement (shoulder and elbow extension). It doesn't reflect functional strength in complex, multi-joint movements.

4. **Popularity ≠ validity**: Bench press is popular because it's easy to measure and compare, not because it's the best strength test.

## What "Raw Strength" Actually Means

Raw strength is typically defined as the ability to produce force against resistance, independent of technique or leverage advantages. This is best measured by:

1. **Compound movements that involve multiple joints and muscle groups**
2. **Movements that are less technique-dependent**
3. **Movements that reflect functional, real-world strength**

## Better Single Measures of Raw Strength

**Deadlift**:

- Involves the entire posterior chain (back, glutes, hamstrings, calves)
- Highly correlated with overall strength
- Less technique-dependent than bench press (though technique still matters)
- Reflects functional strength (picking something heavy off the ground)

**Squat**:

- Involves the entire lower body and core
- Highly correlated with overall strength
- Reflects functional strength (standing up with weight)
- More technique-dependent than deadlift, but still a good measure

**Overhead press**:

- Involves shoulders, core, and stabilizers
- Less technique-dependent than bench press
- Reflects functional strength (pressing weight overhead)
- Smaller range of motion than bench press, so less leverage advantage

## The Best Approach: Multiple Measures

No single exercise perfectly measures raw strength. A comprehensive strength assessment should include:

1. **Lower body**: Squat or deadlift (or both)
2. **Upper body pressing**: Overhead press or bench press
3. **Upper body pulling**: Deadlift (if not already included) or weighted pull-ups
4. **Core/posterior chain**: Deadlift or weighted carries

This gives you a complete picture of strength across all major movement patterns.

## Practical Recommendation

If you had to pick one exercise as the best measure of raw strength, **deadlift** is the strongest candidate because:

- It involves the most muscle mass
- It's less technique-dependent than bench press
- It reflects functional, real-world strength
- It's highly correlated with overall strength

However, the best approach is to track strength across multiple movements (squat, deadlift, overhead press, pull-ups) to get a complete picture.

## The Bottom Line

Bench press is a useful strength metric, but it's not the best single measure of raw strength. Deadlift is a better choice, and the best approach is to track multiple movements across all major patterns.
