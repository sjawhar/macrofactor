# Sodium Bicarbonate for HIIT: Is It Useful?

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) has a legitimate mechanistic basis for improving high-intensity performance, and the evidence is reasonably consistent — but the practical picture is more nuanced than the hype suggests.

## The Mechanism

During high-intensity exercise, hydrogen ions (H⁺) accumulate in muscle as a byproduct of anaerobic glycolysis. This acidosis impairs muscle contractility and contributes to fatigue. Sodium bicarbonate is an extracellular buffer — it raises blood pH before exercise, which increases the gradient for H⁺ to move out of muscle cells into the bloodstream, where bicarbonate can neutralize them. The result is a delay in the onset of acidosis-related fatigue.

This mechanism is most relevant for efforts lasting **roughly 1–7 minutes** at high intensity — the zone where anaerobic glycolysis is a primary energy contributor and acidosis is a meaningful limiter. This maps well onto many HIIT protocols.

## What the Evidence Shows

Meta-analyses on sodium bicarbonate consistently find **small but real performance improvements** for high-intensity efforts in this duration range:

- Effect sizes are typically in the range of 1–3% improvement in performance
- Benefits are most consistent for repeated sprint efforts and sustained high-intensity work
- Single all-out efforts (like a 10-second sprint) show less benefit because they're too short for acidosis to be the primary limiter
- Longer aerobic efforts show less benefit because aerobic metabolism doesn't produce the same H⁺ accumulation

For HIIT specifically — which typically involves repeated high-intensity intervals with incomplete rest — sodium bicarbonate is a reasonable fit. The repeated nature of the intervals means acidosis accumulates across the session, and buffering capacity becomes increasingly relevant as the session progresses.

## The Practical Problems

The main issue with sodium bicarbonate is **gastrointestinal distress**. A meaningful proportion of users experience nausea, cramping, and diarrhea, particularly at the doses needed for performance effects (typically 0.2–0.3 g/kg body weight, taken 60–90 minutes before exercise). This is not a minor inconvenience — it can completely negate any performance benefit.

Strategies to mitigate GI issues:

- **Split dosing**: take the total dose in smaller amounts over 30–60 minutes rather than all at once
- **Take with food**: reduces GI irritation for some people
- **Gradual introduction**: start with lower doses (0.1 g/kg) and build tolerance over several weeks
- **Sodium citrate as an alternative**: some evidence suggests similar buffering with better GI tolerance

## Is It Worth It for HIIT?

For most recreational athletes doing HIIT for general fitness, the answer is probably no — the performance benefit is small, the GI risk is real, and the practical hassle isn't worth it. For competitive athletes where a 1–2% improvement in repeated sprint performance matters, it's worth experimenting with carefully.

The honest framing: sodium bicarbonate is one of the better-supported ergogenic aids for high-intensity work, but "better-supported" in a field full of supplements with no evidence still means modest effects with meaningful individual variability. Test it in training before relying on it in competition, and be prepared for the possibility that your GI system simply won't tolerate it.

## Relevance to Strength Training

For pure strength training (heavy compound work, low reps), sodium bicarbonate is largely irrelevant — the energy systems and fatigue mechanisms are different. It's specifically a tool for the glycolytic, high-intensity zone. If your training includes conditioning work or metabolic circuits, it's more applicable there than to your main strength work.
