# Sodium Bicarbonate for HIIT: Evidence and Practical Use

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) has solid evidence for reducing fatigue in high-intensity work, but its utility for HIIT specifically is more nuanced than the hype suggests.

## The Mechanism

During high-intensity exercise, muscles produce lactate and hydrogen ions (H+), which accumulate and lower muscle pH. This acidification impairs muscle contraction and contributes to fatigue. Sodium bicarbonate buffers this acidity, theoretically allowing longer or harder efforts before fatigue sets in.

## The Evidence

**What works**: Sodium bicarbonate consistently improves performance in repeated high-intensity efforts lasting 1–10 minutes, particularly in activities like rowing, cycling sprints, and repeated-effort sports. The effect is modest but real—typically 2–3% improvement in power output or work capacity.

**Dose**: 0.3 g/kg bodyweight (about 20–25 g for a 70 kg person), taken 60–90 minutes before exercise.

**Timing**: Most effective for repeated efforts (multiple sprints with short rest), less effective for single maximal efforts.

## For HIIT Specifically

HIIT is heterogeneous—the term covers everything from 30-second sprints to 4-minute efforts. Sodium bicarbonate's utility depends on the protocol:

**Where it helps**: Repeated short sprints (20–60 seconds) with incomplete recovery. This is where lactate accumulation is most problematic.

**Where it's less useful**: Single long efforts (4+ minutes) or very short sprints (10–20 seconds). The buffering effect matters less when lactate isn't the primary limiting factor.

**Practical reality**: Most people doing HIIT won't notice a dramatic difference. The 2–3% improvement is real but subtle.

## Practical Considerations

**Side effects**: Sodium bicarbonate commonly causes GI distress (nausea, bloating, diarrhea). This is a real limitation for many people.

**Cost**: Cheap and legal, but the GI issues often outweigh the modest performance benefit.

**Alternatives**: Beta-alanine (which increases muscle carnosine buffering) has similar evidence and fewer GI issues, though the effect takes weeks to build.

## Bottom Line

Sodium bicarbonate works for HIIT, but the effect is modest and comes with GI risk. It's worth experimenting with if you're doing repeated high-intensity efforts and tolerate it well. For most people, focusing on training consistency and nutrition will yield larger returns than supplementation.
