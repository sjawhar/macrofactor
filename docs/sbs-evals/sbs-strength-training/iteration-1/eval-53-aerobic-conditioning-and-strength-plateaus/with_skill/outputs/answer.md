# Low VO2 Max, Poor Aerobic Conditioning, and Strength Plateaus

This is a genuinely important question, and the answer is more nuanced than most people expect. A VO2 max of ~25 ml/kg/min is in the low range (below average for sedentary adults), and combined with mild asthma, it's worth thinking carefully about how aerobic capacity interacts with strength training.

## Can Low Aerobic Fitness Hold Back Strength Progress?

The short answer is: **yes, but probably not in the way you think.**

Aerobic fitness doesn't directly limit your 1RM on a squat or deadlift — those efforts are primarily anaerobic. However, aerobic conditioning affects strength training in several indirect ways:

**1. Recovery between sets and sessions**
Your aerobic system is responsible for clearing metabolites (lactate, H⁺) between sets and restoring phosphocreatine between sessions. Poor aerobic conditioning means slower intra-set recovery, which can reduce the quality of later sets in a session. If you're doing 4–6 sets per muscle group per session (the recommended range), your ability to maintain quality across those sets depends partly on aerobic capacity.

**2. Work capacity and volume tolerance**
Higher aerobic fitness generally supports higher training volumes. If your cardiovascular system is a bottleneck, you may hit fatigue limits before your muscles do, artificially capping the volume you can productively complete. This is particularly relevant for lower-body training (squats, deadlifts) which is more cardiovascularly demanding than upper-body work.

**3. General recovery and adaptation**
Aerobic fitness is associated with better sleep quality, lower resting heart rate, better hormonal profiles, and faster overall recovery. These systemic benefits support the recovery process that drives strength adaptation.

## The Asthma Complication

Mild asthma adds a layer of complexity. Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction can limit your ability to push intensity during conditioning work, and if asthma is poorly controlled, it can impair oxygen delivery during training sessions. Practical considerations:

- Ensure your asthma is well-managed with appropriate medication before attributing plateaus to conditioning
- Cold, dry air and high-intensity efforts are common triggers — warm up gradually and consider your training environment
- Swimming and cycling are often better tolerated than running for asthmatic individuals if you want to add conditioning work

## What to Do About It

If poor aerobic conditioning is contributing to your plateau, the solution isn't to abandon strength training — it's to add **low-interference conditioning work** alongside it.

**Recommended approach:**

- **Zone 2 cardio** (conversational pace, 60–70% max HR) for 20–40 minutes, 2–3x/week — this builds aerobic base with minimal interference with strength adaptations
- Keep conditioning sessions separate from strength sessions when possible (different days or at least several hours apart)
- Avoid high-intensity conditioning (HIIT) if it's impairing recovery from strength work — the interference effect is real and dose-dependent

**Timeline:** Meaningful improvements in VO2 max from consistent aerobic training typically take 8–16 weeks. A VO2 max of 25 can realistically improve to 30–35 with consistent effort, which would meaningfully improve your recovery capacity.

## Diagnosing Whether This Is Your Bottleneck

Signs that aerobic conditioning is limiting your strength progress:

- You feel cardiovascularly gassed during lower-body sessions before your muscles are fatigued
- Your performance drops significantly in later sets compared to earlier sets
- You need very long rest periods (5+ minutes) to recover between sets
- Your strength is disproportionately worse on higher-rep sets compared to low-rep sets

If these patterns fit, improving aerobic conditioning is likely to help. If your strength plateaus are more about load progression stalling on low-rep work, the bottleneck is probably elsewhere (volume, periodization, nutrition, sleep).

## Bottom Line

A VO2 max of ~25 with mild asthma is a real constraint worth addressing. Add consistent Zone 2 conditioning, manage your asthma well, and give it 2–3 months. You may find that your strength training quality improves meaningfully once your aerobic system can better support recovery between sets and sessions.
