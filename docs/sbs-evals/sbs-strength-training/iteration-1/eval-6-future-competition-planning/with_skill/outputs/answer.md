# Thinking Through Future Competition: A Framework for Deciding Whether and How to Compete

This question is personal and context-dependent, but there's a useful framework for thinking through it — whether you're considering powerlifting, bodybuilding, a marathon, or any other competitive endeavor.

## Why Compete at All?

Competition serves a specific function in training that's hard to replicate otherwise: it forces you to peak, to execute under pressure, and to confront your actual performance level rather than your training-day performance level. From a programming standpoint, the SBS framework explicitly notes that advanced lifters should "compete ~2x/year to practice execution skills" — not just to test fitness, but because the competition itself is a skill that requires practice.

Even if you're not advanced, competition provides a concrete goal that structures your training in a way that open-ended "getting stronger" often doesn't. Having a meet date forces you to periodize, to taper, and to make decisions about what matters most.

## The Programming Implications of Competing

Deciding to compete changes how you should structure your training in concrete ways:

**Powerlifting or strength sports:** The SBS phasic model applies directly. You'd run an offseason block focused on volume and variation (building muscle and work capacity), then transition to a pre-peak block 6–8 weeks out with lower volume, higher intensity (85–95%), and tighter specificity to the competition lifts. The last 1–2 weeks involve a taper to maximize readiness. Accessories get trimmed aggressively in the final phase to free recovery for heavy specific work.

**Bodybuilding or physique competition:** The structure is different — a longer building phase followed by a caloric deficit and peak week manipulation. The training itself shifts toward higher volume, more isolation work, and eventually lower intensity as the cut progresses and recovery capacity decreases.

**Endurance events (marathon, triathlon):** These require a fundamentally different periodization model, but the principle is the same: build a base, then sharpen specificity as the event approaches, then taper.

## Practical Considerations Before Committing

A few questions worth answering honestly before signing up:

1. **What's your current training age and phase?** Beginners and early intermediates often benefit more from consistent training than from competition-specific peaking. If you're still making weekly progress, a competition might interrupt a productive training block without adding much.

2. **What's your recovery capacity?** Competition prep — especially the final weeks — is demanding. If life stress is high, sleep is poor, or you're managing injuries, a competition deadline can turn a manageable situation into an unsustainable one.

3. **What's the goal?** If the goal is to win or place well, that's a different preparation than if the goal is to experience competition and have a benchmark. Both are valid, but they imply different levels of sacrifice in training and lifestyle.

4. **What sport fits your current strengths?** Powerlifting rewards strength relative to bodyweight; bodybuilding rewards muscle mass and conditioning; endurance sports reward aerobic capacity and body composition. Competing in a sport that aligns with your current strengths makes the experience more rewarding and the preparation more efficient.

## The Underrated Value of Low-Stakes Competition

One underappreciated option: start with a low-stakes local competition before committing to a major event. Local powerlifting meets, small bodybuilding shows, or community 5Ks let you experience the competition environment, learn what the prep process feels like, and identify gaps in your preparation — without the pressure of a major investment. Many people discover that they love competition; others discover that they prefer training for its own sake. Both are valid outcomes.

## Bottom Line

If you're considering competing, the most important first step is picking a specific event and date. Everything else — the programming, the diet, the peaking strategy — flows from that anchor. Without a concrete goal, training tends to drift. With one, it sharpens.
