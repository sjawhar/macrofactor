# Should I Take L-Citrulline, How Much, and When?

L-citrulline is one of the better-supported pre-workout supplements for both endurance and resistance training performance. Here's what the evidence actually supports.

## What It Does

L-citrulline is an amino acid that converts to L-arginine in the kidneys, which then drives nitric oxide production. Unlike supplementing arginine directly (which is poorly absorbed orally), citrulline bypasses first-pass metabolism in the gut and liver, making it a much more effective way to raise plasma arginine and, downstream, nitric oxide levels.

The practical effects include:

- Reduced muscle soreness and fatigue during high-rep training
- Modest improvements in muscular endurance (more reps at a given load)
- Improved blood flow and "pump" during training
- Some evidence for reduced perceived exertion

## Should You Take It?

It depends on your goals and context. Citrulline is most useful if:

- You're doing high-volume hypertrophy work where muscular endurance matters
- You want to squeeze out more reps on later sets
- You're interested in reducing post-workout soreness
- You're doing aerobic or mixed-modality training

For pure strength work — heavy singles, doubles, triples — the evidence is thinner. Citrulline is unlikely to meaningfully move your 1RM, but it may help you sustain quality across a high-volume session. From a programming standpoint, anything that helps you maintain quality across your MAV-range sets (8–18 sets per muscle per week) has indirect value.

It's not a must-have supplement, but it's one of the few with a reasonable evidence base and a good safety profile.

## Dosing

The research-supported dose is **6–8 grams of L-citrulline** per session. This is the form used in most positive studies.

**Important distinction**: citrulline malate (a common form in pre-workouts) is typically dosed at 6–8 grams as well, but citrulline malate is roughly 57% citrulline by weight. So 8 grams of citrulline malate delivers about 4.5 grams of actual citrulline — below the effective threshold. If you're using citrulline malate, you need closer to **10–12 grams** to match the effective citrulline dose from studies.

Check your label carefully. Many pre-workout products underdose citrulline significantly (2–4 grams of citrulline malate is common and likely ineffective).

## Timing

Take it **30–60 minutes before training**. Plasma arginine peaks around 60–90 minutes after ingestion, so earlier is generally better. Some people take it 45 minutes out as a practical middle ground.

There's no strong evidence for taking it at other times of day for performance purposes. Post-workout citrulline doesn't appear to offer meaningful recovery benefits beyond what adequate protein and carbohydrates provide.

## Practical Notes

- L-citrulline is generally well-tolerated; GI distress is uncommon at standard doses
- It can be taken fasted or with food — absorption isn't significantly affected
- It stacks well with caffeine and creatine without interaction concerns
- Chronic daily use is fine; you don't need to cycle it
- If you're already eating a diet high in watermelon (a natural citrulline source), you're still not getting close to the effective dose from food alone — supplementation is necessary to hit the threshold

## Bottom Line

If you're doing meaningful volume work and want a supplement with a legitimate evidence base, 6–8 grams of L-citrulline (or 10–12 grams of citrulline malate) taken 30–60 minutes pre-workout is a reasonable addition. Don't expect dramatic strength gains, but expect better endurance across your sets and potentially less soreness the next day.
