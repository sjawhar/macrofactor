# I'm 16–19 and Couldn't Reach a 405 Deadlift on Linear Progression. Does Being Younger Hurt Strength Progress?

Being young doesn't hurt strength progress — in fact, adolescence and early adulthood are among the most favorable periods for strength development. But the framing of "couldn't reach 405 on linear progression" reveals a misunderstanding of what linear progression is designed to do and what realistic expectations look like.

## What Linear Progression Actually Is

Linear progression (LP) — adding weight every session — is a beginner tool. It works because beginners are primarily limited by motor learning and neural adaptation, not muscle mass. The gains come fast because you're getting better at the skill of lifting, not because you're building enormous amounts of muscle.

LP is not a program that takes you to elite strength levels. It's a program that takes you from untrained to "no longer a beginner." For most people, that means somewhere in the range of a few months of consistent training before session-to-session progress stalls.

A 405 lb deadlift is a legitimately strong number. For context, it's roughly 3x bodyweight for a 135 lb person, or 2x bodyweight for a 200 lb person. Expecting LP to get you there is like expecting a beginner running program to get you to a sub-3-hour marathon. The tool isn't designed for that destination.

## Does Age Affect Strength Progress?

At 16–19, you have several advantages:

- **High anabolic hormone levels** — testosterone and growth hormone are naturally elevated during late adolescence
- **Rapid neural adaptation** — young nervous systems are highly plastic and respond quickly to new motor demands
- **Long runway** — you have decades of potential training ahead of you

The main caveat for younger teens (16–17) is that **skeletal maturity** may not be complete. Growth plates in the spine and hips typically close by 17–18 in males. This doesn't mean heavy lifting is dangerous — the evidence doesn't support that fear — but it does mean that technique quality and load management matter, and that some coaches prefer to emphasize movement quality over maximum loading until growth is complete.

## Why You Didn't Hit 405

The more likely explanations:

1. **LP ran its course** — you exhausted beginner gains and needed to transition to intermediate programming before reaching that number
2. **Bodyweight** — a 405 deadlift requires substantial muscle mass. If you're 150 lbs, you may simply not have enough muscle yet, regardless of programming
3. **Training duration** — LP typically lasts 2–4 months. 405 may require years of intermediate training
4. **Technique** — inefficient mechanics limit how much load you can express

## What to Do Instead

The SBS framework is clear: when LP stalls, you've outgrown the beginner phase and need to transition to intermediate programming. This means:

- Moving from session-to-session progression to **block-based progression** (4–8 week waves)
- Increasing accessory volume to build the muscle mass that will eventually support heavier main lifts
- Training the deadlift 2–3x/week at varied intensities (75–85% for most work)
- Accepting that PRs now come every few weeks, not every session

At 16–19, you have an enormous advantage: time. A 405 deadlift is achievable for most males with a few years of consistent intermediate training and adequate nutrition. The path there is through building muscle and accumulating quality volume — not through forcing LP past its useful life.
