# Why Are Deadlifts Considered More Fatiguing Than Squats, and Should Deadlift Volume Usually Be Lower?

The deadlift-is-more-fatiguing claim is widely repeated in strength training circles, and it has real mechanistic support — but it's also more nuanced than the blanket statement suggests.

## Why Deadlifts Are Considered More Fatiguing

Several factors contribute to the perception and reality of higher deadlift fatigue:

**1. Greater absolute load.** Most people deadlift significantly more than they squat. A 500 lb deadlift creates more systemic stress than a 400 lb squat, even if the relative effort (RPE) is similar. More total load means more mechanical stress on the spine, hips, and posterior chain.

**2. Larger muscle mass involved.** The deadlift engages the entire posterior chain — hamstrings, glutes, erectors, upper back, traps — plus the quads and core. The sheer volume of muscle tissue recruited creates substantial metabolic and systemic demand.

**3. Spinal loading.** The deadlift places the spine under significant compressive and shear forces, particularly in the lumbar region. The erector spinae work isometrically under heavy load throughout the pull. This type of sustained isometric loading is particularly fatiguing for the lower back and takes longer to recover from than the more dynamic loading pattern of the squat.

**4. No eccentric loading benefit.** The deadlift is typically performed with a controlled but not slow eccentric (lowering) phase, and many lifters drop the bar from lockout. This means less eccentric muscle damage per rep compared to the squat, which might seem like it would reduce fatigue — but it also means less of the "repeated bout effect" that builds eccentric tolerance over time.

**5. Grip and upper back fatigue.** The deadlift taxes grip, forearms, and upper back in ways the squat doesn't. These aren't limiting factors for most people, but they contribute to overall session fatigue.

## Is the Fatigue Difference Real or Overstated?

It's real, but the magnitude varies considerably by individual. Some people find heavy squatting more systemically fatiguing than deadlifting. Factors that influence this:

- **Leverages and body proportions** — long-torso lifters often find deadlifts more taxing; long-femur lifters often find squats more demanding
- **Training history** — if you've deadlifted for years, your posterior chain has adapted and recovery is faster
- **Stance and style** — sumo deadlifts are generally considered less fatiguing than conventional, partly due to reduced range of motion and different muscle recruitment

## Should Deadlift Volume Be Lower?

Generally, yes — and this is reflected in how most evidence-based programs are structured. The SBS framework acknowledges that the deadlift carries a higher fatigue cost per set than most other exercises, which means:

- **Fewer direct deadlift sets per week** are needed to drive adaptation compared to squat or bench
- **Accessory work** (RDLs, back extensions, hamstring curls, rows) can carry much of the posterior chain hypertrophy volume with lower systemic cost
- **Frequency** for the deadlift is often lower than for the squat — many intermediate programs have lifters deadlifting heavy 1–2x/week rather than 2–3x

A practical starting point for intermediate deadlift programming:

- 1–2 heavy deadlift sessions per week (3–5 sets at 75–85%)
- Supplemented by 2–4 sets of RDLs or similar hinge accessories per session
- Total posterior chain volume (hamstrings, glutes, erectors) built through accessories rather than more deadlift sets

## The Practical Takeaway

Don't avoid deadlifts — they're one of the most effective strength and muscle-building exercises available. But respect their fatigue cost. If your recovery is suffering, your lower back is chronically fatigued, or your squat performance is declining, deadlift volume is often the first place to look. Reduce direct deadlift sets before cutting accessories or other lifts.
