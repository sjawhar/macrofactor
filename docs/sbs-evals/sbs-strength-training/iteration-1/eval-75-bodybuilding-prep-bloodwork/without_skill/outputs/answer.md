# Abnormal Blood Markers in Natural Bodybuilders: What's Expected?

During intense contest prep and overreaching blocks, natural bodybuilders often see abnormal blood markers. The question is: what's expected and normal, and what's concerning?

## Common Abnormalities During Contest Prep

**1. Lipid panel changes**

- **Increased LDL cholesterol**: Common during cutting (especially high-fat diets)
- **Decreased HDL cholesterol**: Common during severe caloric deficit
- **Increased triglycerides**: Can occur with high carb intake or stress
- **Why it happens**: Caloric deficit, hormonal changes, stress

**2. Liver function markers**

- **Elevated AST/ALT**: Can occur with intense training and caloric deficit
- **Elevated bilirubin**: May indicate stress on liver
- **Why it happens**: Muscle breakdown, metabolic stress, dehydration

**3. Kidney function markers**

- **Elevated creatinine**: Can occur with high protein intake and dehydration
- **Elevated BUN**: Common with high protein and low carbs
- **Why it happens**: High protein intake, dehydration, muscle breakdown

**4. Electrolyte imbalances**

- **Low sodium**: From excessive sweating and water manipulation
- **Low potassium**: From diuretics or inadequate intake
- **Low magnesium**: From caloric deficit and stress
- **Why it happens**: Dehydration, water manipulation, inadequate nutrition

**5. Thyroid markers**

- **Decreased T3**: Common during severe caloric deficit
- **Increased TSH**: May indicate metabolic adaptation
- **Why it happens**: Caloric deficit suppresses thyroid function

**6. Glucose and insulin**

- **Elevated fasting glucose**: Can occur with stress and cortisol elevation
- **Altered insulin sensitivity**: May occur with extreme dieting
- **Why it happens**: Metabolic stress, hormonal changes

**7. Inflammatory markers**

- **Elevated CRP**: Common with intense training and caloric deficit
- **Elevated WBC**: May indicate stress response
- **Why it happens**: Training stress, caloric deficit, immune suppression

## What's "Normal" During Prep

**Mild abnormalities are expected:**

- 10-20% elevation in liver enzymes
- 10-15% elevation in kidney markers
- Lipid panel changes (especially LDL increase, HDL decrease)
- Mild electrolyte imbalances
- Decreased T3
- Elevated inflammatory markers

These typically normalize within 2-4 weeks of returning to maintenance calories and normal training.

## What's Concerning

**Red flags that warrant medical attention:**

- **Severe liver dysfunction**: AST/ALT >3x normal
- **Severe kidney dysfunction**: Creatinine >2.0 or BUN >40
- **Severe electrolyte imbalances**: Sodium <130 or potassium <3.0
- **Cardiac symptoms**: Chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath
- **Severe anemia**: Hemoglobin <12 g/dL
- **Severe thyroid suppression**: T3 <80 pg/mL
- **Persistent abnormalities**: Don't normalize after returning to maintenance

## Individual Variation

Some natural bodybuilders tolerate extreme dieting better than others:

- **Genetics**: Some people's bodies adapt better to caloric deficit
- **Age**: Younger athletes often tolerate stress better
- **Training experience**: More experienced lifters may have better metabolic adaptation
- **Baseline health**: People with pre-existing conditions are at higher risk
- **Prep duration**: Longer preps create more cumulative stress

## Overreaching Blocks

During intentional overreaching (high volume, high intensity, moderate deficit):

- Similar abnormalities occur
- Usually less severe than contest prep
- Should normalize within 1-2 weeks of deload

## Practical Recommendations

**During prep:**

- Get bloodwork done 4-6 weeks before competition
- Identify any concerning abnormalities early
- Adjust diet/training if markers are severely abnormal
- Retest 2 weeks before competition if initial results were concerning

**To minimize abnormalities:**

- Maintain adequate protein intake (0.8-1g per lb bodyweight)
- Don't drop calories too aggressively (aim for 500-750 cal deficit, not 1000+)
- Maintain adequate micronutrients (especially electrolytes)
- Stay hydrated (don't over-restrict water until final days)
- Manage training volume (don't add excessive volume during deficit)
- Prioritize sleep (8-9 hours per night)
- Manage stress (meditation, yoga, etc.)

**Post-prep:**

- Return to maintenance calories gradually (not all at once)
- Retest bloodwork 2-4 weeks after competition
- Most abnormalities should normalize
- If they don't, consult a doctor

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Stop prep and seek medical attention if:**

- You experience chest pain or palpitations
- You have severe dizziness or fainting
- You have severe muscle cramps or weakness
- You have signs of kidney dysfunction (dark urine, swelling)
- You have signs of liver dysfunction (jaundice, severe fatigue)
- Bloodwork shows severe abnormalities (see "concerning" section above)

## The Reality

**Mild abnormalities during contest prep are expected and usually harmless.** Your body is under significant stress, and blood markers reflect that. However, severe abnormalities warrant medical attention and may require adjusting your prep approach.

**Natural bodybuilding is inherently stressful on the body.** The goal is to minimize that stress while still achieving your goals. This means:

- Not dropping calories too aggressively
- Not extending prep too long
- Maintaining adequate nutrition
- Prioritizing recovery
- Getting regular bloodwork

## Bottom Line

Abnormal blood markers during contest prep and overreaching blocks are expected and usually normalize within 2-4 weeks of returning to maintenance. Mild elevations in liver enzymes, kidney markers, and inflammatory markers are normal. However, severe abnormalities warrant medical attention. To minimize abnormalities, maintain adequate nutrition, don't drop calories too aggressively, prioritize sleep, and manage stress. Get bloodwork done 4-6 weeks before competition to identify any concerning abnormalities early. If severe abnormalities occur, adjust your prep or seek medical attention.
