# Can You Gain Muscle at Maintenance? Yes—In the Right Context

Yes, muscle gain at around maintenance calories is possible via recomposition. It’s not guaranteed, but it’s a real outcome when the context is favorable. That fits the SBS skill’s core framing: adaptation depends on your current bottleneck and training status, not one rigid calorie rule.

## Why It Can Happen

Hypertrophy is primarily driven by progressive training stimulus plus sufficient protein and recovery. A calorie surplus often helps, but it’s not always required for every lifter at every stage.

People most likely to recomp at maintenance:

- Beginners or near-beginners
- Lifters returning after detraining (retraining effect)
- Individuals with higher fat mass and room to mobilize stored energy
- Lifters who meaningfully improve training quality and consistency

From the SBS perspective (SKILL sections 4 and 5), these groups often have a lot of “easy adaptation runway” once programming and execution improve.

## Key Conditions That Raise Success Odds

1. **High-quality training stimulus**
   - Keep main work in productive ranges (e.g., compounds often 5–8 or 6–15 depending goal)
   - Use progressive overload models (double progression for accessories; structured loading for compounds)

2. **Adequate protein**
   - Sufficient daily protein intake supports lean mass retention/gain during neutral energy balance

3. **Recoverable volume**
   - Volume should sit in an effective zone (SBS baseline often 8–18 hard sets/week per muscle, then individualized)
   - Chasing MRV constantly can reduce progress by outpacing recovery

4. **Phase realism**
   - Advanced lifters near muscular potential generally need tighter programming and may benefit more from slight surplus phases for best hypertrophy rates

## What This Means Practically

If you’re an intermediate recreational lifter with body fat to spare, a maintenance-calorie phase can still add muscle—especially if prior training was inconsistent or under-dosed. You don’t need to force a large bulk to make progress.

But “possible” is not “automatic.” Track objective indicators:

- Performance trend at matched effort
- Circumference/body-comp trend over months (not days)
- Recovery quality and session output

If progress stalls with good recovery, nudge a lever: slightly increase volume, improve exercise selection, or introduce a small surplus.

## Bottom Line

Muscle gain at maintenance is a legitimate, context-dependent strategy. In SBS terms, prioritize good programming, progressive overload, and adequate protein first; then decide whether a surplus is truly necessary. For many lifters—especially earlier in training or during retraining—recomp is not just theory, it’s a practical outcome.
