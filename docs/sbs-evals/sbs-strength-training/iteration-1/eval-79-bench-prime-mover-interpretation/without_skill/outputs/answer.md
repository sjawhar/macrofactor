# Triceps as Prime Mover in Bench Press: Implications for Accessory Selection

The triceps do become increasingly involved as bench press loads approach maximum, but this does NOT mean you should prioritize triceps accessories over pec-focused work. This is a common misinterpretation of biomechanics that leads to suboptimal training decisions.

## Understanding Prime Mover Contribution

In the bench press, the pectoralis major is the primary mover throughout the entire range of motion, but muscle contribution shifts with load and range of motion. At lighter loads and greater chest stretch, the pecs dominate. As loads increase and the bar approaches lockout, the triceps and anterior deltoids contribute more to complete the movement.

This is normal biomechanics, not a reason to shift training focus.

## Why This Doesn't Change Accessory Priorities

The bench press itself is already an excellent triceps exercise. The triceps receive substantial stimulus from heavy bench pressing, especially in the lockout phase. Adding additional triceps-focused work (close-grip bench, skull crushers, dips) is valuable for addressing weak points and building arm mass, but it shouldn't replace pec-focused accessories.

The pectoralis major is a large muscle group with significant hypertrophy potential. Pec-focused accessories (incline bench, dumbbell press, machine chest press, pec deck) provide:

- Different angles of loading that the flat bench doesn't fully address
- Isolated stimulus that allows higher reps and volume
- Injury prevention through balanced development

## The Weak Point Principle

If your bench press stalls at lockout, triceps weakness may be limiting. In this case, adding triceps work makes sense. But if you're stalling mid-range or off the chest, pec weakness is likely the limiting factor, and pec-focused accessories are more appropriate.

## Balanced Accessory Selection

A well-designed bench press accessory program should include:

- **Pec-focused work** (incline press, dumbbell variations, machine work)
- **Triceps-focused work** (close-grip bench, skull crushers, dips)
- **Shoulder-focused work** (overhead press, lateral raises)

The ratio depends on individual weak points, but most lifters benefit from roughly equal emphasis on pecs and triceps, with some shoulder work.

## The Verdict

Triceps involvement at heavy loads is biomechanically accurate, but it's not a reason to neglect pec development. The bench press itself provides substantial triceps stimulus. Accessory selection should be based on weak points and individual needs, not on a misinterpretation of muscle contribution curves. Most lifters benefit from balanced pec and triceps accessory work.
