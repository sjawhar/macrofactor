# When Do You Need Extra Conditioning for Lifting Progress?

The clearest sign is not “I’m out of breath sometimes.” The key sign is: **the amount of quality training you need for progress now exceeds what you can complete or recover from.**

## The symptom pattern to watch

From an SBS fatigue/work-capacity lens, look for this cluster:

- You need more volume to keep gaining, but sessions become unsustainably long or sloppy.
- Performance quality drops across sets or across the week at matched effort.
- Recovery debt accumulates (persistent fatigue, declining bar speed, repeated underperformance).

That usually means your specific work capacity for your sport/task is lagging behind your current programming demands.

## Best first lever: specific conditioning inside lifting

Rather than defaulting to separate cardio blocks, a strong approach is to **condition within the same movement family** by stepping down the intensity ladder for a phase.

### If strength is the priority

If you’re currently centered around heavy triples/doubles and stalling, run a block with more 5–8 rep work on your main patterns. This aligns with the skill’s intensity map (strength-hypertrophy bridge zone) and builds local work capacity with high specificity.

### If hypertrophy is the priority

If your main work is 8–12 reps and session tolerance is the bottleneck, spend a phase in 12–15 or even 20–25 rep accessories. That raises metabolic tolerance and lets you handle more productive weekly work later.

This matches the skill’s progression framework: change overload levers and rep emphasis by phase, then return to your primary rep zone with better capacity.

## Where general cardio fits

Low-intensity cardio (walking, easy cycling) is still excellent for health and can support recovery. But for this specific problem—lifting-specific session capacity—it is usually not the first or most targeted lever.

## Practical template

1. Keep movement patterns similar (don’t fully swap out your lifts).
2. Run 3–6 weeks with slightly higher reps and manageable RIR.
3. Maintain weekly frequency (often 2–3 exposures per pattern).
4. Deload if fatigue markers accumulate.
5. Return to lower-rep emphasis and reassess tolerance/performance.

So, yes: conditioning matters, but the most efficient version is often **specific conditioning through higher-rep versions of your existing lifting patterns**, not immediately adding big standalone cardio blocks.
