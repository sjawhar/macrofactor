# Conditioning for Strength and Hypertrophy: Signs You Need It

You need additional conditioning when your work capacity—the ability to perform high volume at high intensity—becomes the limiting factor in your training. Several signs indicate this threshold.

## Signs You Need More Conditioning

**Excessive fatigue between sets**: If you're unable to recover adequately between heavy sets (breathing heavily, elevated heart rate preventing focus), your cardiovascular capacity is limiting performance. You should be able to complete a heavy set, rest 2-3 minutes, and feel ready for the next set.

**Inability to complete planned volume**: If you consistently fail to finish your programmed sets and reps due to fatigue (not strength), conditioning is limiting. You might complete 3 sets of 5 reps on squats but be too fatigued to finish your accessory work.

**Excessive soreness or fatigue accumulation**: If you're sore and fatigued for days after training, your recovery capacity is insufficient. This often reflects poor conditioning rather than overtraining.

**Declining performance in later sets**: If your first 2-3 sets are strong but later sets show significant performance drops, work capacity is the issue.

**Difficulty maintaining intensity**: If you can't maintain consistent weight and reps across all sets due to fatigue, conditioning needs improvement.

## Types of Conditioning for Lifters

**Low-intensity steady-state (LISS)**: Walking, cycling, or rowing at conversational intensity for 20-40 minutes. This improves aerobic capacity and recovery without interfering with strength training. Best for general work capacity.

**High-intensity interval training (HIIT)**: Short bursts of high effort (30 seconds) followed by recovery. Improves anaerobic capacity and work capacity in shorter timeframes. Use sparingly (1-2x per week) to avoid interference with strength training.

**Sled work and carries**: Farmer carries, sled pushes, and sled drags build work capacity while maintaining strength. These are excellent for lifters because they're strength-compatible.

**Conditioning circuits**: Moderate-intensity circuits using light weights or bodyweight movements. These build work capacity while maintaining movement quality.

## Practical Implementation

Start with 2-3 sessions of LISS per week (20-30 minutes each). This is the safest approach that rarely interferes with strength training. If you need faster results, add one HIIT session per week.

Avoid excessive conditioning volume. More than 4-5 hours per week of conditioning can interfere with strength and hypertrophy progress by increasing overall fatigue and caloric expenditure.

## The Verdict

Most lifters benefit from some conditioning work, but it should be secondary to strength training. Add conditioning when work capacity becomes limiting, not as a default. Start with LISS and progress to more intense methods if needed.
