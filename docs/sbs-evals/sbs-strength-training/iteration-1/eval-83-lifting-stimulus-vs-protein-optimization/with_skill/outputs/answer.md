# Training Stimulus vs Protein Strategy for MPS: What Matters Most?

If you force-rank the levers, **resistance training is the dominant driver**, and protein strategy is a critical but secondary support layer.

## Why training sits at the top

Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) is highly sensitive to mechanical loading. Across many contexts, removing or drastically reducing loading causes major atrophy pressure, while reintroducing even modest loading provides a strong preservation signal. That’s why studies around immobilization or low-activity periods repeatedly show that “nutrition alone” has limited ability to fully rescue muscle without mechanical stimulus.

So yes, protein matters—but mostly as permissive infrastructure for adaptation that training initiates.

## Are we over-focusing on protein minutiae?

Sometimes, yes. The hierarchy is usually:

1. Keep some safe, repeatable resistance training in place.
2. Then optimize total daily protein.
3. Then distribution/timing/source details.

This aligns with the SBS skill philosophy of bottleneck targeting: if the stimulus is missing, polishing timing details has low return.

## Layoff/constraint practicals

A useful practical point: during constrained periods, very reduced training volumes (roughly 10–15% of normal, if intensity and execution are sensible) can still help preserve a surprising amount of muscle and strength. That does not mean optimal progress, but it’s often far better than complete unloading.

By contrast, protein-only interventions during immobilization are mixed and generally modest, though higher-dose amino acid/leucine approaches sometimes show small benefits. Helpful? Potentially. Sufficient alone? Usually not.

## Programming implications (SBS-aligned)

- Keep movement exposure frequency as high as safely possible.
- Use lower volume if needed, but retain meaningful effort and load.
- Manage fatigue so consistency is sustainable.
- Layer protein best practices on top (adequate total intake, sensible distribution).

This mirrors the skill’s progression and fatigue model: adaptation comes from training stress + recovery ecology, not from nutrient timing hacks in isolation.

Bottom line: don’t ignore protein, but don’t invert priorities. Preserve loading first, then optimize protein amount/distribution/timing as an amplifier—not as a substitute for resistance training.
