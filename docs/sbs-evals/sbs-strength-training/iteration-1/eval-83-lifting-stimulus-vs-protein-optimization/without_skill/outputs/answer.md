# Resistance Training vs. Protein: Which Drives Muscle Protein Synthesis?

Resistance training is the primary driver of muscle protein synthesis (MPS), and protein feeding is secondary. The training stimulus is necessary; protein is permissive. This distinction is critical for understanding how to optimize muscle growth.

## The Hierarchy of Stimulus

**Resistance training** creates mechanical tension and muscle damage that signals the need for muscle growth. This is the primary stimulus. Without training, protein feeding alone does not increase MPS significantly.

**Protein feeding** provides amino acids that support MPS. However, protein feeding without training stimulus produces minimal hypertrophy. The training stimulus is the signal; protein is the building material.

## The Research

Studies show that resistance training increases MPS by 50-100% above baseline. Adding protein feeding to post-workout increases MPS further, but the magnitude of increase is smaller than the training stimulus itself. A study by Schoenfeld et al. found that resistance training increased MPS by approximately 50%, while adding protein increased it by an additional 20-30%.

This means the training stimulus accounts for roughly 60-70% of the MPS response, while protein feeding accounts for 30-40%.

## Practical Implications

**Training is non-negotiable**: You cannot build muscle without resistance training. Protein feeding cannot compensate for inadequate training stimulus.

**Protein is essential but not optimized**: You need adequate protein (0.7-1.0g per pound of body weight daily), but the timing and amount of protein feeding has modest effects on MPS compared to the training stimulus. Eating 20g of protein post-workout vs. 40g produces a small difference in MPS; the difference between training and not training is enormous.

**Total daily protein matters more than timing**: Distributing protein across meals (20-40g per meal) is more important than consuming a large amount immediately post-workout. Your body can utilize protein throughout the day.

## The Practical Takeaway

If you're choosing between optimizing your training program or optimizing your protein intake, optimize training first. A lifter with excellent training and adequate protein will build far more muscle than a lifter with suboptimal training and perfect protein timing.

That said, adequate protein is necessary. You can't build muscle on insufficient protein, even with perfect training. The hierarchy is: (1) adequate training stimulus, (2) adequate total protein, (3) optimized protein timing and distribution.

## The Verdict

Resistance training is the primary driver of MPS, accounting for the majority of the growth signal. Protein feeding is essential but secondary. Prioritize training quality and consistency first, then ensure adequate protein intake. Obsessing over protein timing while neglecting training stimulus is backwards.
