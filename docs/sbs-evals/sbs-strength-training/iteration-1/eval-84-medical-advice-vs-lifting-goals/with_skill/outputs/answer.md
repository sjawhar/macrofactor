# “Stop Squatting, It’s Bad for Your Knees”: How to Respond Productively

Start with this mindset: your doctor is usually operating from a different goal framework than you are.

## Why that advice happens

Most general clinicians are trained to prioritize symptom reduction, risk minimization, and preserving activities of daily living for broad populations. In that context, telling someone to stop a painful, non-essential activity can be a rational conservative call.

For a strength athlete, though, squat performance may be a core value and goal—not an optional extra. So the apparent conflict is often a **goal mismatch**, not necessarily bad faith or incompetence.

## Better next step than arguing online-style

Don’t automatically dismiss the advice, but don’t treat it as your only option either. Seek a second opinion from a sports-medicine physician or PT who works with lifters and can evaluate:

- your specific tissue irritability profile,
- squat mechanics and loading history,
- what movement modifications keep training possible,
- and your personal risk tolerance.

That approach fits the SBS decision model: solve the bottleneck while preserving as much specific training as safely possible.

## Practical training response (SBS principles)

Instead of binary “squat or never squat,” use load-management tools:

1. Reduce provocative variables (load, ROM depth, weekly set load, tempo).
2. Keep some squat-pattern exposure if tolerable (specificity principle).
3. Shift volume to lower-irritation variants/accessories temporarily.
4. Progress back using conservative overload (one lever at a time).
5. Deload proactively if pain/fatigue markers stack up.

This maps directly to the skill’s fatigue and progression guidance: maintain stimulus, manage recoverability, and avoid all-or-nothing swings.

## Cost-benefit is personal

There’s no universal answer independent of your priorities. Someone training for general health may reasonably choose to stop barbell squatting permanently. A competitive lifter may accept higher complexity and monitored risk to keep squatting.

Bottom line: respect the clinician’s context, then get context-specific guidance from professionals experienced with athletes. Your best decision is a shared one that accounts for medical risk, your goals, and a practical path to keep training what matters to you.
