# Responding to Medical Advice Against Squatting

If a doctor tells you to stop squatting because "it's bad for your knees," you should seek clarification and potentially a second opinion. This advice is often overly broad and not evidence-based, but it may reflect a legitimate concern about your specific situation.

## The Evidence on Squats and Knee Health

Research consistently shows that squats, when performed with proper form, do not damage healthy knees. In fact, squats strengthen the muscles around the knee (quadriceps, hamstrings, glutes) and improve knee stability. Epidemiological studies of powerlifters and weightlifters show lower rates of knee injury compared to the general population.

The concern that "squats are bad for knees" is outdated and not supported by modern evidence. Knees are designed to bend, and loaded bending (squatting) strengthens them.

## Why Doctors Might Say This

Doctors may recommend avoiding squats for several legitimate reasons:

- **Recent knee injury or surgery**: Squats may be contraindicated during acute recovery
- **Specific pathology**: Certain knee conditions (severe osteoarthritis, meniscal tears) may require modified loading
- **Poor form**: If you're squatting with poor form, a doctor might recommend stopping until form improves
- **Outdated training philosophy**: Some doctors aren't familiar with modern evidence on resistance training

## How to Respond

**Ask for specifics**: "What specifically about squats concerns you? Is it all squats, or certain variations? Are there modifications that would be safe?"

**Seek clarification on your condition**: "Do I have a specific knee condition that contraindicates squats, or is this general advice?"

**Request a second opinion**: If the advice seems overly restrictive, consult a sports medicine doctor or physical therapist who specializes in strength training. They're more likely to be familiar with evidence-based recommendations.

**Propose modifications**: "Would a modified squat (box squat, partial range, different stance) be acceptable while I recover?"

## When to Follow Medical Advice

If you have a specific knee injury, recent surgery, or diagnosed pathology, follow your doctor's advice during acute recovery. However, most doctors will support a gradual return to squatting as you heal.

If you have chronic knee pain, work with a physical therapist to identify the cause. Often, knee pain reflects weakness in the hips or glutes, poor movement patterns, or inadequate recovery—all of which can be addressed through modified training.

## The Verdict

Generic advice that "squats are bad for knees" is not evidence-based. However, your doctor may have legitimate concerns about your specific situation. Seek clarification, ask for specifics, and consider a second opinion from a sports medicine professional. In most cases, you can return to squatting with proper form and appropriate progression, even after knee issues.
