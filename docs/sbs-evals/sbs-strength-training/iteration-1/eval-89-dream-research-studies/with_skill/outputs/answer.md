# Dream Research Studies: What Would SBS Most Want to Know?

This is a fun hypothetical, but the answers reveal real gaps in training science that matter for programming decisions.

## The Headline Studies

**Human hyperplasia**: Greg's provocative pick was a definitive study on whether humans can grow new muscle fibers (hyperplasia), not just enlarge existing ones. Counting fibers directly requires biopsy or cadaver analysis under tightly controlled conditions, which is ethically impractical in normal research. This would fundamentally change how we think about long-term hypertrophy ceilings.

**Massive responder-variability project**: Greg's more realistic preference was a large-scale mechanistic study with rich omics data (genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics) and multiple training programs, designed to map _why_ individuals respond differently to identical training. This would move us beyond "average" volume/frequency recommendations and toward genuinely personalized programming — something current MEV/MAV/MRV landmarks can only approximate through trial-and-error.

## Eric's Practical Trial Priorities

Eric's wish list was more immediately actionable:

1. **Creatine-caffeine interaction trial** — properly powered, because existing data on whether caffeine blunts creatine's ergogenic effects is inconclusive and practically important given how many lifters use both daily.

2. **Nitrate + citrulline hypertrophy studies** — with adequate doses and training-relevant protocols, not the underdosed supplement-company designs that dominate current literature.

3. **Protein-dose scaling studies** — across different body compositions and energy states (deficit vs surplus vs maintenance). Current recommendations (~1.6–2.2 g/kg/day) work well on average, but optimal scaling for very lean vs higher-body-fat individuals during cuts remains unclear.

## The Big Programming Gap

Both hosts called out a major unanswered question: **how should you train during caloric deficits?** Specifically, should you maintain volume and reduce intensity, or maintain intensity and reduce volume? There is strong opinion on both sides but surprisingly little direct data. This matters because most lifters spend significant time in deficits, and current guidance (maintain intensity, reduce volume if needed) is based more on reasoning from first principles than controlled trials.

## Why This Matters for Real Programming

These gaps affect everyday decisions: how to set volume during a cut, whether to individualize frequency based on recovery markers, and how much to trust supplement stacks. Until these studies exist, the practical approach remains what SBS consistently recommends — start with evidence-based defaults, track outcomes, and adjust based on individual response.
