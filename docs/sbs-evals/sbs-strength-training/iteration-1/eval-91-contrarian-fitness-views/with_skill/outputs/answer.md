# Contrarian Fitness Views: What SBS Pushes Back On

The first thing to challenge here is the premise. There is no single unified "common wisdom" across all fitness subcultures. What powerlifters consider obvious, bodybuilders may disagree with, and what functional-fitness advocates believe may conflict with both. Context and subcommunity matter more than any universal orthodoxy.

## Stretching: The Main Concrete Example

Greg's primary example was **static stretching**. In many lifting circles, static stretching is treated as harmful or useless — something that weakens muscles and should be replaced entirely by dynamic warm-ups. Greg argued this is an overcorrection based on selective reading of the evidence.

The actual picture:

- **Chronic stretching** has strong evidence for improving flexibility. If range of motion is a limiting factor, stretching works.
- **Acute pre-performance effects** can occur with very long, intense stretching immediately before maximal tests — but these effects often dissipate after a normal warm-up. A few minutes of static stretching before training is not going to meaningfully hurt your squat.
- The anti-stretching position became "common wisdom" partly because one finding (acute strength reduction from extreme static stretching protocols) got overgeneralized into "never stretch before lifting."

This is a useful case study in how fitness advice becomes distorted: a nuanced finding gets simplified, amplified, and applied far beyond its original scope.

## Training Frequency: Shifting Consensus

Greg also used **training frequency** as an example of how "common wisdom" reverses over time. A decade ago, the default recommendation in many lifting communities was once-per-week per muscle group (classic bro splits). Now, most evidence-informed recommendations favor 2–3x/week per muscle for most goals — and the SBS framework explicitly recommends this range for intermediates.

The point was not that higher frequency is always better, but that what counts as "common knowledge" can flip within a single decade based on new data and shifting community norms. Today's contrarian take may be tomorrow's default advice.

## The Broader Lesson

Rather than listing specific contrarian positions, the more useful takeaway is epistemic humility: be suspicious of any claim presented as obvious or universal in fitness. Evaluate the evidence behind it, consider the context it applies to, and recognize that "common wisdom" varies by who you ask and when.
