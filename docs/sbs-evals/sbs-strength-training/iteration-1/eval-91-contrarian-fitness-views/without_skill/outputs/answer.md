# Contrarian Fitness Views

Several widely-held fitness beliefs don't hold up well under scrutiny, and I think it's worth questioning them:

## 1. "You Must Train to Failure for Hypertrophy"

This is probably the most overblown myth. Research consistently shows that stopping 1-3 reps short of failure produces similar hypertrophy to training to failure, while being safer and less fatiguing. Training to failure is useful occasionally for intensity, but it's not necessary and can accumulate unnecessary joint stress and CNS fatigue. Many people conflate "hard training" with "training to failure," but you can be very hard without hitting failure every set.

## 2. "Cardio Kills Gains"

The idea that any cardio will destroy muscle is exaggerated. Moderate cardio (150-300 min/week of steady-state or equivalent HIIT) doesn't meaningfully interfere with strength or hypertrophy when protein and calories are adequate. The problem isn't cardio itself—it's excessive volume combined with inadequate nutrition. Cardio is actually beneficial for work capacity, recovery, and health.

## 3. "You Need a Specific Meal Timing Window"

The obsession with eating within 30-60 minutes post-workout is largely unfounded. Total daily protein and calories matter far more than the timing of individual meals. Studies show that as long as you eat adequate protein throughout the day, meal timing has minimal impact on muscle protein synthesis or hypertrophy. This is one of the most anxiety-inducing myths that doesn't match the evidence.

## 4. "More Volume Always Equals More Gains"

There's a dose-response relationship with training volume, but it plateaus. Beyond ~10-20 sets per muscle group per week, additional volume shows diminishing returns and increases injury risk. Many people equate "dedication" with "more sets," but intelligent programming beats volume chasing every time.

## 5. "You Must Eat in a Surplus to Build Muscle"

While a surplus makes hypertrophy easier, body recomposition (gaining muscle while losing fat) is absolutely possible, especially for beginners and those returning to training. The obsession with "bulking" and "cutting" phases ignores that many people can make simultaneous progress on both fronts with proper training and adequate protein.

## 6. "Soreness Indicates a Good Workout"

DOMS (delayed-onset muscle soreness) is a poor indicator of training effectiveness. You can have excellent workouts with minimal soreness, and soreness doesn't correlate strongly with hypertrophy or strength gains. Chasing soreness often leads to excessive volume and poor recovery.

## 7. "You Need Supplements to Make Progress"

The supplement industry thrives on the belief that you need their products. In reality, adequate protein (from food), calories, sleep, and consistent training account for 95% of results. Creatine and caffeine have solid evidence, but most other supplements have minimal effects. Food and consistency beat supplements every time.

The common thread: these myths often serve the fitness industry's interests (selling programs, supplements, coaching) more than they serve your actual progress. Question everything, look at the evidence, and remember that boring consistency beats sexy complexity.
