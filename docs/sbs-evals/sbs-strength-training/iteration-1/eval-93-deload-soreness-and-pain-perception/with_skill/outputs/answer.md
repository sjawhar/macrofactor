# Why Deload Weeks Sometimes Feel More Sore Than Hard Training Weeks

This is a common experience and a reasonable question. The important caveat upfront: **there is not solid direct human evidence** that definitively explains this phenomenon. What follows are plausible mechanisms, not proven causes. High-confidence single-cause explanations are overstated.

## Plausible Mechanism 1: Reduced Sympathetic Masking

Hard training acutely elevates sympathetic output — adrenaline and related catecholamines — which can transiently blunt pain perception. During a normal hard training week, you are operating with elevated arousal and its associated analgesic effects. During a deload, that sympathetic drive drops, and background aches that were being masked may become more noticeable.

This is consistent with the SBS deload framework: deload weeks deliberately reduce effort to 3–5 RIR and cut volume by 30–50%. That is a large enough stimulus reduction to meaningfully lower the sympathetic response.

## Plausible Mechanism 2: Movement-Related Analgesia

General movement itself has an analgesic effect. When total training volume and activity level drop during a deload, there is less ongoing movement to produce this effect. Discomfort that was being modulated by regular high-volume activity may feel more prominent.

This parallels the observation that many lifters report feeling "creakier" on rest days than on training days.

## Plausible Mechanism 3: Attentional Shift

During hard training, you are focused on performance — hitting rep targets, monitoring RPE, managing fatigue across sets. This performance focus leaves less mental bandwidth to notice minor aches. During a deload, training is easier and less mentally demanding, so attention naturally shifts inward. You have more bandwidth to notice and ruminate on background discomfort.

## What This Means Practically

Several mechanisms are plausible, none are proven, and they likely overlap. The key takeaway:

- Increased soreness during a deload is **usually not a sign that something is wrong**.
- It does not mean the deload is failing or that you should skip it.
- The purpose of a deload is to dissipate fatigue while preserving skill — some paradoxical aching during that process is a normal experience, not an alarm.

If soreness is severe, localized, or accompanied by swelling or loss of range of motion, that warrants investigation. But diffuse, mild-to-moderate aching during a deload week is common enough to be unremarkable.
