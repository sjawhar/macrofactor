# Ashwagandha for Performance and Strength: A Skeptical SBS-Aligned View

The meta-analysis itself may look competently done, but Greg does not think the underlying evidence base is consistent enough to strongly recommend ashwagandha as an ergogenic aid. The distinction between "competent meta-analysis" and "reliable underlying data" matters here.

## The Problem With the Evidence

Several studies in the ashwagandha literature report very large, hard-to-generalize effect sizes. Greg highlighted one particularly striking example: an approximately 8-week trial reporting about **+46 kg bench press gain** in the ashwagandha group. Even if you take the supplement at face value, the placebo group also showed very large gains, which suggests the study environment itself was unusual. Effect sizes that large in trained populations over 8 weeks are implausible for any supplement, and they signal high noise and limited external validity rather than a reliable expected effect.

When a meta-analysis pools studies with this kind of variability, the aggregate result inherits the instability of its component studies. A positive mean effect can be real in a statistical sense while being unreliable as a practical prediction for any individual user.

## Weak Mechanistic Coherence

Beyond the statistical issues, the mechanistic case is not especially compelling. The proposed pathways (cortisol reduction, modest testosterone changes, adaptogenic stress modulation) do not converge on a clear, strong mechanism for strength or hypertrophy gains. Compare this with creatine, where the mechanism (increased phosphocreatine resynthesis) is well-understood, directly relevant to performance, and consistently observed.

## Practical Recommendation

Greg's position was cautious rather than dismissive:

- If someone is very interested in trying ashwagandha, uses a reputable product from a tested brand, and stays within labeled dosing (typically 300–600 mg/day of a root extract), trying it is **reasonable**.
- Expectations should be **modest**. Do not expect creatine-level effects.
- It may have some value for stress management, sleep quality, or subjective well-being — contexts where ashwagandha has somewhat more coherent evidence — even if the direct strength/hypertrophy case is weak.

Current evidence is not strong enough for confident performance claims. The training and nutrition fundamentals (progressive overload, adequate volume in the MAV range, sufficient protein, good sleep) will always dwarf any supplement effect. Prioritize those first.
