# HIIT vs Steady-State Cardio for Body Composition

The short answer: there is **no meaningful body-composition advantage** of interval training over steady-state cardio when matched for overall effort. Both are modest standalone fat-loss tools, and the best choice is the one you can adhere to safely.

## What the Evidence Shows

Eric summarized a meta-analysis (Steele et al.) finding very small average body-composition changes from cardio of either type: about **-0.25 kg fat** and **+0.1 kg fat-free mass**. These are not impressive numbers. Cardio is excellent for health and can contribute to an energy deficit, but as a standalone fat-loss intervention it is usually modest. **Pairing cardio with dietary management** is far more effective than relying on either modality alone.

## Concrete Interval Protocols

If you want to use intervals, Eric offered several practical setups:

1. **30 s all-out (RPE 10) + 90 s easy recovery**, 10–15 rounds. High-intensity, time-efficient, but demanding.
2. **1 min hard / 1 min easy** around RPE 9–10. A good middle ground for most people.
3. **2 min hard / 2 min easy** at slightly lower intensity (about RPE 8–9.5) for fewer total rounds. More sustainable per session.

These are not the only options, but they cover the practical spectrum from maximal-effort sprints to harder sustained-effort intervals.

## Interference With Lifting

Interference between cardio and strength training is usually small if total stress is managed and sessions are programmed sensibly. From an SBS programming perspective:

- Place hard interval sessions on non-lifting days or well-separated from heavy compound work.
- If combining in one session, do the priority work first (usually lifting).
- Monitor recovery markers: if persistent soreness, performance decline, or elevated fatigue appear, the total training load (lifting + cardio) may be too high. Adjust cardio volume first, since it is typically the lower-priority stimulus for strength and hypertrophy goals.

## Practical Takeaway

Choose the cardio modality and protocol you can perform safely and adhere to consistently. Neither HIIT nor steady-state has a meaningful edge for body composition. What matters is total energy balance, adequate protein, and resistance training. Cardio fills in around those priorities.
