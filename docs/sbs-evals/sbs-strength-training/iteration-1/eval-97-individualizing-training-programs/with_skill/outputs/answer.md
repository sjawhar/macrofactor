# Individualizing Training Programs: When Does Personalization Matter?

Direct resistance-training evidence for the benefits of individualized programming is limited, but conceptually supportive findings exist. The practical question is: where does individualization provide the most return?

## What the Evidence Suggests

Greg cited a **force-velocity-profile jump study** where individualized programming outperformed a generalized approach for jump outcomes. This is proof-of-concept rather than direct maximal-strength evidence, but it demonstrates that matching training stimulus to individual profiles can produce better results when the right variables are targeted.

**Supervision research** shows that even on the same program, more direct coaching tends to improve strength outcomes. This suggests that individualization is not just about program design — the feedback loop of having someone assess and adjust your execution matters.

From **within-subject unilateral studies**, individual differences appear most meaningful for volume and frequency tolerance. In one frequency-volume design (comparing 2x vs 3x vs 5x per week), higher frequency won on average, but some individuals responded better at lower frequencies. This is consistent with the SBS framework's emphasis on MEV/MAV/MRV as individual landmarks, not universal prescriptions.

## Where to Individualize First

Greg's applied recommendation was to **individualize volume and frequency first**, since other training variables likely matter less for most people. This aligns directly with SBS programming principles:

1. **Volume tolerance** — Some people progress well at 10 sets/week per muscle; others need 16–18 to see continued adaptation. The MEV-to-MRV range varies meaningfully between individuals based on training age, recovery capacity, sleep, stress, and nutrition.

2. **Frequency preference** — Training each muscle 2x/week works for many people, but some respond better to 3x or even 4x for certain muscles (especially back and glutes, which often tolerate higher frequency). Others do well with concentrated volume in fewer sessions.

3. **Exercise selection** — This matters less than volume and frequency for most people, but it becomes more important for advanced lifters with specific weak points or joint limitations.

## When Personalization Matters Most

- **Advanced lifters** near their muscular potential, where the margin between productive and non-productive training narrows
- **Lifters with injury history or movement restrictions** that rule out standard exercises
- **Lifters who have stalled on well-designed general programs** after honest assessment of recovery and adherence

## When It Matters Least

- **Beginners** — Any reasonable program with progressive overload, moderate volume, and consistent practice works well. The limiter is skill and habit formation, not program optimization.
- **Intermediates making steady progress** — If performance is trending up and recovery is manageable, the current approach is working regardless of how generic it is.

## Bottom Line

Personalization is real but narrower than the coaching industry suggests. Start with evidence-based defaults (2–3x/week per muscle, 10–18 sets/week, 1–3 RIR on compounds), track outcomes, and adjust volume and frequency based on individual response. That process — not a bespoke program from day one — is how individualization actually works in practice.
